Introduc+on 

Age -related macular degeneraMon (AMD) remains a leading cause of irreversible vision loss among older 

adults. It affects nearly 200 million people worldwide, with numbers projected to increase with 

populaMon aging 1. Clinically, AMD encompasses a spectrum of progressive macular changes, ranging

and accurate risk straMficaMon are essenMal for paMent counseling, monitoring, and Mmely intervenMon. 

In clinical pracMce and screening, tradiMonal diagnosis relies on human expert interpretaMon of reMnal 

images within clinical context. However, this process is Mme -consuming, subject to inter -observer 

variability, and difficult to scale across large p opulaMons. Timely idenMficaMon and risk straMficaMon of 

AMD therefore remain challenging, highlighMng the need for automated tools capable of idenMfying 

advanced disease and analyzing precursor features such as drusen characterisMcs and pigmentary 

abnormaliMes 5.

Automated methods, parMcularly those based on deep learning, have become increasingly central to 

medical image analysis 6-8, and have demonstrated robust performance in ophthalmology, including the 

detecMon of diabeMc reMnopathy (DR) 9-12 , glaucoma 13 -16 , cataract 17 -19 , and AMD 5, 20 -24 . Many of these 

systems have achieved high diagnosMc accuracy for specific tasks. However, most operate as task -specific

contrast, clinical interpretaMon of reMnal findings is inherently contextual and communicaMve: clinicians

and integrate imaging results into broader care decisions. 

Recent advances in mulMmodal large language models (MLLMs), which integrate both image and

can answer and explain quesMons. Such capabiliMes align closely with the communicaMve and 

interpreMve aspects of ophthalmic care, where effecMve explanaMon of reMnal findings is central to 

paMent counseling and shared decision -making. Rather than f uncMoning solely as automated classifiers, 

mulMmodal systems may support clinicians by enhancing interpretability, consistency, and efficiency in 

reMnal image assessment and communicaMon 28 -33 .

In this study, we developed and evaluated OcularChat, an MLLM speci fica lly designed for AMD -related 

reMnal image assessment and clinically grounded dialogue. In contrast to convenMonal vision -language 

models (VLMs) that rely on contrasMve language -image pre -training (CLIP) -style pretraining 28 or separate 

visual and language encoders 30 , OcularChat was fine -tuned end -to -end from a pretrained Qwen2.5 -VL 

model using reMnal images paired with simulated paMent -physician dialogues derived from the Age -

Related Eye Disease Study (AREDS). We assessed its performance on three clinically relevan t tasks: 

idenMfying advanced AMD, categorizing drusen size, and detecMng pigmentary abnormaliMes. More

with those of the reference model. 

By jointly integraMng image -based diagnosis with interpretable, dialogue -based outputs and clinician -

centered evaluaMon, this work advances beyond prior benchmarking studies of mulMmodal ophthalmic 

models toward a clinically oriented assessment framewor k34 . As MLLMs begin to enter clinical pracMce, 

rigorous clinician -centered evaluaMon is essenMal to ensure not only accurate feature idenMficaMon but 

also reliable diagnosMc reasoning and dialogue that are safe and trustworthy in clinical semngs.

Discussion 

In this study, OcularChat was developed as a mulMmodal system for reMnal image interpretaMon, 

including interacMve dialogue and reasoning. Across all diagnosMc tasks, it achieved higher performance 

than general -purpose VLMs, as well as a domain -specific model. AddiMonally, according to subjecMve 

assessment by ophthalmologists, it achieved higher raMngs for diagnosMc reasoning and clinical 

usefulness compared with the reference model. 

Most VLMs are pretrained on general -domain data, with limited exposure to ophthalmic images and 

terminology, which can restrict performance in detailed reMnal image interpretaMon. OcularChat 

incorporates ophthalmology -specific data and dialogue -based supervisi on to address this gap. It was 

adapted from a pretrained VLM (Qwen2.5 -VL -32B) and trained using CFPs and syntheMc paMent -

physician dialogues derived from clinical informaMon in the AREDS dataset. 

External validaMon showed that OcularChat generalizes more consistently across tasks on external data. 

Across all tested models, we observed a notable reducMon in performance for detecMng advanced AMD 

in AREDS2. This decline likely reflects differences in disease appearance in the modern treatment era, in 

which neovascular AMD is managed with anM –vascula r endothelial growth factor (VEGF) therapy and 

may present with subtle or absent features on CFP, in comparison to the florid appearance of untreated 

ne ovascular AMD at the Mme of AREDS. Nonetheless , OcularChat sMll maintained a high accuracy score. 

Most prior works have focused on developing deep learning -based classificaMon models that produce 

fixed numeric outputs, such as DeepSeeNet 5, which demonstrates strong predicMve performance but 

provides limited interpretability. More recent efforts, including large mulMmodal ophthalmology dataset 12 

(LMOD) 34 , evaluated VLMs for ophthalmic image understanding and diagnosis. While LMOD provides 

valuable insights into current VLMs’ capabiliMes, the results highlight substanMal limitaMons in 

ophthalmic image interpretaMon, parMcularly in clinically meaningfu l reasoning. In contrast, our work 

advances beyond benchmarking by developing an ophthalmology -specific VLM, with a focus on AMD -

related image assessment and explanatory conversaMonal output. 

Beyond quanMtaMve grading scores, addiMonal insights were obtained through ophthalmologist 

evaluaMon of model outputs. This evaluaMon idenMfied systemaMc semanMc misalignments in 

explanatory content that were not captured by predicMon accuracy alo ne. As illustrated in Figure 1,

although OcularChat correctly idenMfied the absence of advanced AMD, its explanaMon emphasized 

drusen size and pigmentary abnormaliMes rather than the defining features of advanced disease, i.e., 

geographic atrophy and neovascular disease. While accur ate idenMficaMon of all stages of AMD is 

clinically important, parMcularly for risk straMficaMon and iniMaMon of AREDS2 supplementaMon, this 

pauern highlights a misalignment between the diagnosMc target and the explanatory focus. Specifically, 

co nflaMng features of earlier disease stages with criteria for advanced AMD may obscure the disMncMon 

between disease severity categories, which is of clinical concern for neovascular AMD, where Mmely 

detecMon is criMcal given the availability of effec Mve vision -preserving therapies. These findings indicate 

that correct diagnosMc labels do not necessarily correspond to clinically valid or stage -appropriate 

reasoning. 

This issue was more evident in the reference model, whose verbose outputs frequently combined some 

correct conclusions with inaccurate or irrelevant anatomical descripMons, including references to regions 

not central to the diagnosMc task. Clinicians consistently noted that this response style obscured the 

model’s final judgment. Although the referenc e model occasionally included appropriate guidance (e.g., 

recommending professional consultaMon or lifestyle modificaMon), it also increased suscepMbility to 

hallucinaMon and knowledge bias, which was reflected in consistently lower clinician scores for relevance 13 

and internal consistency. In contrast, OcularChat produced concise, task -focused responses that reduced 

the likelihood of contradictory explanaMons and were perceived as beuer aligned with clinical workflows, 

parMcularly in opportunisMc screening scena rios where clear idenMficaMon of pathologic findings is 

prioriMzed. In pracMce, OcularChat is intended to funcMon as a decision -support tool that complements 

clinician judgment by highlighMng salient image features and providing concise explanaMons, rather than 

replacing standard diagnosMc workflows .

This work has several limitaMons. Although OcularChat’s more concise outputs were generally preferred 

for their efficiency and clarity, some ophthalmologist s noted that highly simplified outputs insufficiently 

demonstrated image -grounded reasoning, raising concerns that predicMons parMally relied on learned 

priors rather than explicit visual evidence. This observaMon also reflects variability in clinician 

preferences, with some favoring concise, direcMve outputs and others valuing more detailed 

explana Mons to support exploratory reasoning or discussion. 

Another limitaMon is the reliance on CFPs, as it was trained exclusively on this single image modality. 

Although CFPs are central to reMnal evaluaMon, and reflect the reMnal appearance on clinical 

examinaMon, they do not always capture the full range of structural informaMon required for ophthalmic 

diagnosis in all semngs. OpMcal coherence tomography (OCT), in parMcular, provides high -resoluMon 3 -

dimensional imaging, especially for the early diagnosis of the neovascular form of late AMD 41 -44 .

Nevertheless, CFPs remain highly advantageous for large -scale screening applicaMons, as they can be 

acquired using cost -effecMve imaging devices, and are widely available across diverse clinical semngs .

In summary, this study demonstrates the feasibility of adapMng MLLMs for reMnal image interpretaMon, 

with clinician evaluaMon supporMng their potenMal role as interpretable decision -support tools in AMD 

assessment, with scope for further expansion to broader ophthalmic applicaMons. By integraMng image -

based diagnosis with clinically grounded explanaMons and interacMve dialogue, OcularChat illustrates 14 

how MLLMs can move beyond fixed -output classificaMon toward supporMng real -world clinical reasoning 

and paMent communicaMon. 

Methods 

This main study used publicly available data from the AREDS 35 , a mulMcenter 12 -year clinical trial 

conducted across 11 clinical sites in the United States. The original AREDS study was approved by the 

InsMtuMonal Review Board (IRB) at all parMcipaMng sites, and wriuen informed consent was obtained 

from all par Mcipants. Eligible parMcipants underwent stereoscopic color fundus photography (CFP; 30° 

field centered on the fovea) of both eyes at baseline and annually for up to 12 years of follow -up. 

ParMcipants were categorized into AMD severity groups, including individuals with no AMD. 

For the current study, CFPs and corresponding eye -level annotaMons (described below) were selected 

from the AREDS dataset. Data were split at the parMcipant level into training, validaMon, and test sets to 

prevent informaMon leakage, ensuring that imag es from the same parMcipant did not appear in other 

subsets. To ensure generalizability across eyes, images from both eyes were included .

Dataset Construc#on and Dialogue Genera#on 

To ensure clinical relevance and contextual accuracy during model training, simulated paMent -physician 

dialogues were generated using key clinical variables extracted from the AREDS dataset. These variables 

included demographic characterisMcs (age, gende r, diabetes status, and smoking history) and ophthalmic 

features (presence of advanced AMD, maximum drusen size, and presence of pigmentary abnormaliMes), 

defined by the AREDS simplified severity scale 35 .

Dialogue generaMon was performed using a large language model (GPT -4V 36 ), with structured prompts 

designed to ensure clinical coherence and to constrain dialogue content to the provided variables. The 

prompt design was physician -informed; clinicians were consulted to idenMfy key quesMons typically 15 

asked during paMent evaluaMon and to guide construcMon of the generaMon prompts so that simulated 

dialogues reflected realisMc paMent -physician interacMons. The dialogue generaMon workflow is 

illustrated in Figure 3, and the prompts used for generaMon are provided in Supplementary Tables S1 and 

S3. The generated dialogues contained two formats intended to reflect realisMc paMent -physician 

interacMons while guaranteeing the accuracy of AMD -related risk assessment (Supplementary Tables S2 

and S4). A total of 705,850 paMent -physician dialogues were generated through GPT -4V. Standard 

preprocessing followed Qwen2.5 -VL recommendaMons 37 with details presented in Supplementary S. 1. 

The generated dialogues, paired with their corresponding reMnal images, formed the training dataset.

these, 30 pairs were selected as a common subset, and were evaluated independently by all three 

ophthalmologists, enabling esMmaMon of inter -rater agreement. Th e remaining 90 pairs were parMMoned 

into three sets of 30 pairs and were assigned to a single ophthalmologist each. Thus, each 

ophthalmologist evaluated 60 pairs (30 common and 30 unique). Model performance was then

S. 1 Dataset Construction and Dialogue Generation 

In dialogue generation, a total of 705,850 patient -physician dialogues were generated through GPT -4V with 

temperature of 0.5 and 1024 maximum tokens. The generated dialogues, paired with their corresponding retinal 

images, formed the training dataset. Each sample consisted of a single pair of retinal images and associated 

dialogues formatted as alternating conversational turns. The generation strategy is shown in Fig ure S1. Retinal 

images were resized to 1024×1024 pixels and normalized following the pre -pro cessing pipeline recommended for 

Qwen2.5 -VL. No additional manual cropping or image enhancement was applied. 

S. 2 Training Details 

All parameters of Qwen2.5 -VL -32B, including the vision and language encoders and the multilayer perceptron 

(MLP), were optimized during training. Training was conducted using Pytorch (version 2.6) in a distributed setting 

using eight NVIDIA H100 GPUs, each with 80 GB of memory and per -GPU batch size of 2. An effective batch size 

of 128 was achieved through gradient accumulation over eight steps. Mixed -precision training with bfloat16 (BF16) 

was utilized to enhance memory efficiency and computational perform ance. The learning rate was initialized at 1e -6

and scheduled using cosine decay, with a warm -up phase comprising 3% of the total training steps to facilitate stable 

convergence. Optimization was performed using AdamW optimizer with default settings. The m odel was trained to 

minimize cross -entropy loss between predicted and target dialogue tokens, conditioned on both the fundus image 

and preceding conversational context. Model checkpoints were saved at regular intervals, and training was 

conducted for two e pochs. Unless otherwise specified, all remaining hyperparameters followed the official 

recommendations for Qwen2.5 -VL. 

S. 3 Evaluation of Model Accuracy 

To facilitate large -scale analysis, the model was instructed to return its outputs in a structured JSON format. To 

evaluate performance, these outputs were compared with the ground truth labels derived from previous reading 

center grading of the AREDS reti nal images. Specifically, performance was assessed using accuracy and F1 score. 

All analyses were conducted using Python (version 3.10.18), NumPy (version 2.1.2) and scikit -learn (version 1.6.1). 

All models were evaluated using the same prompts and decodin g parameters (including temperature and maximum 

token length) to ensure fair comparison .30

Table S7 . Likert Grading Rubric .

Grading Criteria - Retinal Findings (Core AMD Features) 

Q1. The model correctly identifies the presence or absence of advanced AMD. 

Introduction: The model provides a binary prediction (advanced AMD: Yes or No). Evaluate: 

- Is the diagnosis correct based on the fundus image? 

- Are hallmark features of advanced AMD (e.g., geographic atrophy, hemorrhage, subretinal fluid, 

disciform scars) visible to support the diagnosis? 

- If asked, does the model give a plausible rationale (even if incomplete or implicit)? 

Scoring Criteria 

• 1: Model prediction is incorrect (e.g., predicts advanced AMD when features are clearly absent, or 

misses clear signs). No visible features to support claim. 

• 2: Prediction may be borderline correct, but lacks clear visible justification (e.g., subtle atrophy or 

ambiguous signs). Reasoning unclear or absent. 

• 3: Prediction is plausible and some image -based evidence is present (e.g., early GA or fibrotic tissue). 

Minor uncertainty exists. 

• 4: Correct diagnosis with supporting visible features. Clinically coherent. Reasoning is plausible if 

provided. 

• 5: Clear and correct prediction with strong visual support (GA or nAMD signs clearly visible). Model 

would be trustworthy in clinical use. 

Q2. Pigmentary abnormalities are appropriately detected. 

Introduction: The model predicts presence/absence of pigmentary abnormalities. Consider: 

- Are hypo/hyperpigmented areas present and reasonably interpreted? 

- Does the model’s output align with what you see? 

- If asked, does the model give a plausible explanation (e.g., mentions of mottling, clumping, 

depigmentation)? 

Scoring Criteria 

• 1: Model missed clear pigmentary changes or falsely claimed them. No visual support. Reasoning (if 

any) is incorrect. 

• 2: Some pigmentary change may be visible, but model prediction is off or ambiguous. Reasoning 

absent or inaccurate. 

• 3: Prediction is plausible with some supporting visual features. Reasoning unclear but not obviously 

wrong. 

• 4: Correctly identifies pigmentary changes with visible support. Reasoning plausible if queried. 

• 5: Prediction matches clinical observation; pigmentation abnormalities are clear. Reasoning, if 

requested, aligns with clinical criteria. 

Q3. Drusen size is accurately categorized. 38 

Introduction: The model outputs a categorical decision: none/small, intermediate, and large. Consider: 

- Is the prediction correct based on the image? 

- Can you identify visible supporting features for the prediction? 

- If you asked the model for an explanation, was the reasoning (if any) correct, incorrect, or 

unavailable? 

Scoring Criteria 

• 1: Prediction is incorrect, and no clear soft drusen are visible (or falsely claimed). Reasoning (if 

available) is incorrect or absent. 

• 2: Prediction may be correct or borderline, but supporting features are unclear or weak. Reasoning 

not available or inaccurate. 

• 3: Prediction is plausible, and visible evidence (e.g., well -defined, yellowish deposits) is present, even 

if not explicitly highlighted. Reasoning (if requested) may be absent or partial. 

• 4: Correct prediction with supporting image features visible. Reasoning is plausible if provided. 

• 5: Correct prediction, supported by clearly visible soft drusen. Reasoning is accurate, or clinician 

could easily infer the same conclusion independently. 

Q4. Overall Assessment: The model’s outputs are useful for clinical decision -making in AMD 

and general retinal care. 

Introduction: Consider: 

- Whether the model’s diagnostic outputs (e.g., AMD stage, systemic risks) are correct or clinically 

reasonable. 

- Whether it missed any obvious or important findings visible in the image. 

- Whether it hallucinated findings (i.e., reported conditions do not present in the image). 

Scoring Criteria 

• 1: Outputs are mostly incorrect, miss key findings, or include hallucinated diagnoses. Clinical utility is 

low or potentially misleading. 

• 2: Some outputs are reasonable, but the model either misses important features or makes 

unsupported claims. Use would require significant clinician correction. 

• 3: Outputs are partly useful — major findings generally present, some minor omissions or 

questionable statements. Would use with caution. 

• 4: Most outputs are correct and relevant. Only minor omissions or borderline findings; enhances 

efficiency with limited oversight. 

• 5: Diagnoses are accurate, important findings are captured, and no hallucinations observed. Highly 

useful in supporting clinical decisions.
