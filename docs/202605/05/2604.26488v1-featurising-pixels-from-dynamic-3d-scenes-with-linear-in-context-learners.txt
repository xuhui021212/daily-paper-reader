## Abstract 

One of the most exciting applications of vision models in-volve pixel-level reasoning. Despite the abundance of vision foundation models, we still lack representations that effec-tively embed spatio-temporal properties of visual scenes at the pixel level. Existing frameworks either train on image-based pretext tasks, which do not account for dynamic el-ements, or on video sequences for action-level reasoning, which does not scale to dense pixel-level prediction. We present a framework that learns pixel-accurate feature de-scriptors from videos, LILA. The core element of our train-ing framework is linear in-context learning. LILA leverages spatio-temporal cue maps — depth and motion — estimated with off-the-shelf networks. Despite the noisy nature of those cues, LILA trains effectively on uncurated video datasets, em-bedding semantic and geometric properties in a temporally consistent manner. We demonstrate compelling empirical benefits of the learned representation across a diverse suite of vision tasks: video object segmentation, surface normal estimation and semantic segmentation. 

## 1. Introduction 

Most computer vision tasks require pixel-level feature repre-sentations that embed both semantic and geometric proper-ties of a visual scene. Although state-of-the-art foundation models, such as the DINO family [ 1– 3], already encode a remarkable degree of these properties, these encoder-only models yield feature maps at a low patch -level resolution. To achieve pixel -fine feature grids directly, one could up-sample the input by a factor of the patch size. However, this approach is neither computationally efficient, nor task-effective in practice, since it introduces a discrepancy in in-put resolution between training and inference. In this work, we introduce a novel training strategy for encoder-decoder architectures, which natively computes a feature vector for each input pixel, as shown in Fig. 1 (top).

In this work, we consider two cue modalities — depth and optical flow — which we complement with self-distillation of upsampled encoder representations. Off-the-shelf networks for estimating depth and optical flow already exhibit impressive generalisation to in-the-wild videos. Since the training data for these networks typically comprises a mixture of synthetic and real-world data captured with inexpensive hardware ( e.g . RGB-D cam-eras), the overall annotation effort is substantially lower compared to methods using mask supervision [ 4, 7]. How-ever, a crucial challenge in learning from the cues predicted by pre-trained models is the inherent noise and imperfections in the training signal. We show that LILA learns effectively despite the imperfect cue maps. Although trained on videos, LILA requires only a single image as the input at inference. This sets LILA apart from the prior work on video-based pre-training that operates on spatio-temporal inputs both at training and inference [ e.g . 8, 9]. Our work makes two core contributions. (i) We develop a novel training technique, linear in-context learning, for encoder-decoder pre-training from videos. Our model yields dense feature maps per image with powerful geometric, se-mantic and temporal qualities. (ii) We rigorously evaluate LILA on a diverse suite of tasks distinct from the cue modal-ities used during training. Specifically, LILA yields consis-tent and significant improvement over established baselines across three benchmarks: video object segmentation, surface normal estimation and semantic segmentation. 

## 2. Related work 

Our work contributes to representation learning from unla-belled videos. Therefore, LILA is related to the literature of self- and weakly supervised learning. 

Self- and weakly supervised learning. The most widely used representations stem from pre-training on image data [1 , 10 ]. Contrastive learning [ 11 ] maximises the similarity of a sample to its positive counterpart w.r.t. a set of negative samples [ 12 ], and underpins a large family of frameworks [ 13 –15 ]. For example, MoCo [ 14 ] applies the contrastive loss by deriving the negative samples from a dynamic queue. Non-contrastive methods, such as DINO [ 2] and its scaled-up variants [ 1 , 3], implement clustering via self-distillation, where the mean teacher creates categorical targets for the student. BYOL [ 16 ] is another example of a non-contrastive approach with an asymmetric architecture, which dispenses with the negative samples without solution collapse. Masked autoencoder [MAE, 10 ] implements masked image mod-elling (MIM) , i.e . learning to reconstruct the input from its partial observation. Instead of the colour space as in MAE, I-JEPA [ 17 ] realises MIM in the embedding space, by predicting the representation of masked patches. BEiT [ 18 ] and iBOT [ 19 ] leverage a pre-trained tokeniser to en-code the targets for the partially masked input. Training on paired text-image data, CLIP [ 20 ] and SigLIP [ 21 , 22 ]produce semantically aligned representations by learning a joint embedding space shared by the two modalities. Given the diversity of foundation models, feature aggregators, such as AM-RADIO [ 23 ], aim at creating “superset” models by distilling multiple foundation models into one. A practical limitation of the works above is the low res-olution of the feature grid, comprising only patch features. While there are upsampling strategies, such as FeatUp [ 5]and LoftUp [ 4], we take an approach that yields pixel-level 

representations naturally and complementary (i.e . with dif-ferent properties) to those obtained from the encoder. 

Dense (pixel-level) representation learning. Previous work has approached dense representations with pixel-level contrastive learning [ 24 ] and clustering with multi-crop [ 25 ]and cross-image consistency [ 26 ]. Other dense pre-text tasks include creating targets for relative position prediction (either for patches [ 27 ] or entire crops [ 28 ]), and colorisation [ 29 ,30 ]. CroCo [ 31 ] learns to complete the second view given the context of an adjacent view. 

Dense representation learning from videos. Videos are abundant, but learning dense representations from videos has proved challenging. Jabri et al . [ 32 ] combine contrastive learning with cycle consistency by propagating a patch-level label set forward and backward. Araslanov et al . [ 33 ] create the learning signal with a batch-level set of clusters spread uniformly across multiple videos. VITO [ 34 ] uses attention to spatially aggregate a representation most likely predic-tive of the future state. Leveraging pre-trained optical flow networks, FlowE [ 35 ] extends the BYOL framework to pixel-level training. Specifically, FlowE encourages representation similarity of temporally corresponding pixels. PooDLe [ 36 ]augments the dense objective of FlowE with temporally co-herent crop pairs. DORA [ 37 ] uses a student-teacher frame-work by distilling object-patch correspondences. Leveraging slot attention and sparse depth supervision, SAVi++ [ 38 ]encodes a video into a set of temporally invariant slots. In contrast to these works, we explore geometrically grounded signals for supervision — depth and motion — with a simple novel technique, linear in-context learning .

We bootstrap our model from a pre-trained vision trans-former, such as DINOv2 [ 39 ], and equip the backbone with a DPT decoder [ 40 ]. The DPT decoder has skip connections to four intermediate feature blocks in the encoder, which allows it to effectively upsample the patch-level tokens to a pixel-level feature map. Our framework trains only the decoder, while keeping the encoder parameters frozen. While we estimate the depth and flow maps using frames 

(It, I t+∆ ), we provide only their crops as the input to our encoder-decoder network. We randomly generate aspect-preserving crops c0 := C0(It) and c∆ := C∆(It+∆ ), and feed them to LILA in parallel. We aim to train our network to produce useful pixel-dense representations by imposing a training loss on the corresponding outputs x0, x ∆ ∈ RN ×d.

Cue maps: motion, depth and self-distillation. To super-vise the network, we use pixel-level cue maps, illustrated in Fig. 2b. Specifically, we use optical flow computed between 

(It, I t+∆ ) in the forward U↑ and backward directions U↓,and depth estimates Dt and Dt+∆ for frames It and It+∆ .Note that U↑ is defined in the reference frame of It, warp-ing it to frame It+∆ (conversely for U↓). Furthermore, we complement the flow and depth with low-resolution encoder features. We refine the features with the image statistics using pixel-adaptive map refinement (PAMR) [ 41 ]. PAMR implements a variant of mean-field inference for a Condi-tional Random Field (CRF) using local affinity-based kernels. Training LILA. Using the cue maps Gcontext , G query ∈

RN ×m, we develop an in-context training objective. Con-cretely, we aim to obtain the feature maps x0, x ∆ such that an optimal linear projection of x0 approximating Gcontext 

also works for x∆ to approximate Gquery , i.e . network pre-dictions remain consistent under the linear transformation. More formally, for depth maps and encoder features, the consistency implies that if a linear operator W optimally approximates these cues for frame It, it must approximate them for frame It+∆ . −W must approximate the backward flow. We formulate this idea into a learning framework. Given x0, produced by our encoder-decoder model for crop C0(It), we find the optimal projection W ∗ w.r.t. Gcontext

Discussion. Linear in-context learning imposes a non-trivial training task on the encoder-decoder network. The optimal linear map W ∗, derived via ridge regression, must capture the visual elements of the context frame that remain 

invariant across the entire temporal window. The modalities embedded by the cue maps further encourage the network to learn a spatially diverse structure, specifically recognising dynamic objects (due to optical flow) and encoding scene geometry (due to depth maps). By embedding the refined encoder maps, LILA simultaneously performs a variant of 

self-distillation , which preserves the encoder’s original se-mantic properties. Fig. 3 visualises the target cues and their approximations during training of LILA. Despite the in-herent noise and inaccuracies present in all cue modalities, LILA learns an effective representation, as we demonstrate experimentally in the next section. 

Inference. At test time, LILA operates in a standard fash-ion, discarding the depth and optical flow networks. It ac-cepts a single image as input and produces a feature map of the same spatial resolution. 

## 4. Experiments 

Implementation details We train the LILA models with AdamW [ 42 ] using the learning rate 10 −4 and weight decay 

10 −5. The decoder architecture is based on DPT [ 40 ]. We define the output feature dimensionality from the decoder as 128 , 192 and 256 for the LILA variants with backbones ViT-S14, ViT-B14, ViT-L14, respectively. We set γ = 1 

throughout all experiments; LILA is not sensitive to moder-ate deviations of this hyperparameter. For the experiments below, we initialise the backbones from DINOv2 [ 1]. How-ever, we show that LILA generalises effectively to backbones with other pre-training strategies (see the supp. material). 

Training datasets. We experiment with two datasets for training LILA: YouTube-VOS (YT-VOS) [ 43 ] and Kinetics-700 [ 44 ]. The YouTube-VOS (2019) dataset comprises 

3, 978 (train and val ) high-resolution video sequences of everyday scenes. Kinetics-700 contains up to 650 K video clips of human actions. We do not use any annotation accom-panying these datasets. We train the smaller LILA variants on YouTube-VOS (ViT-S14, ViT-B14), while for the larger backbone (VIT-L14) we experiment with both training sets. 

Evaluation datasets. We test LILA on three distinct tasks: video object segmentation (VOS), surface normal estima-tion and semantic segmentation. DAVIS-2017 [ 45 ] is the standard semi-supervised VOS benchmark with 30 video sequences in the validation set. For semantic segmenta-tion, we use COCO-Stuff [ 46 ] with 27 coarse categories. NYUv2 [ 47 ] provides the testbed for surface normal estima-tion, focusing primarily on indoor environments. 

4.1. Video object segmentation 

We evaluate LILA on the downstream task of video object segmentation. The goal is to estimate correct segmentation of a video sequence based on the ground-truth annotation provided for the first frame. The validation sequences feature camera motion as well as motion of one or multiple objects. As the standard metrics on this benchmark, we report the mean Jaccard index (IoU), Jm and the boundary-based F-score, Fm. Following previous work [ 6 ], we test LILA with two probing methodologies: linear probing and local k-NN.

4.2. Surface normal estimation 

Following previous work [ 48 ], we use NYUv2 dataset [ 47 ]for estimating normals per pixel. We train a linear probe to map the feature representation to a 3D normal vector. For the high-resolution feature grids of LILA (and the recent work FlowFeat [ 41 ]), we implement the linear mapping using a convolutional layer with a 5 × 5 kernel. We augment the 3D representation from our decoder with the low-resolution output from the encoder, obtained by a 1 × 1 convolution and bilinearly upsampled to the original image resolution. Tab. 2 (left section) reports the results in terms of four standard metrics: root mean squared error (RMSE) and the fraction of pixels with the angular error below three pre-defined thresholds δ1 = 11 .25 ◦, δ2 = 22 .5◦, δ3 = 30 ◦.LILA outperforms all baselines and the recent FlowFeat 6across all metrics with a confident margin. We observe that the surface normal accuracy significantly improves with model capacity. For example, RMSE of DINO2-L14 is lower than that of DINO2-S14 by around 5 points. Despite the stronger baseline, LILA improves the normal accuracy further. By training LILA with DINO2-L14 on the larger Kinetics, we achieve a modest, though still tangible boost. Observing the visual results in Fig. 5a, we note a remark-able degree of detail in the feature maps yielded by LILA. Furniture and household items are especially prominent in the representation. This is surprising, since LILA was trained on dynamic scenes with a strong bias for outdoor scenes and dynamic objects, not static environments. Overall, the results suggest that LILA encodes geometric scene properties, while also embedding the semantic struc-ture of complex scenes, which we analyse further next. 

4.3. Semantic segmentation 

We use COCO-Stuff [ 46 ] for probing experiments on seman-tic segmentation. The task is to predict categorical distri-bution for each image pixel. Following previous work [ 5], we use the coarse set annotation with C = 27 semantic cat-egories. We use the train split of COCO-Stuff for training the probe. Adapting the strategy from FlowFeat [ 6], LILA uses an attention-based probe. Specifically, we pass C query tokens via cross-attention through the spatial feature grid to obtain class prototypes. For efficiency, we downsample the high-resolution feature grid in the cross-attention block. The logits for each pixel derive from a scalar product of the class prototypes and the original feature map. We complement the high-resolution logits obtained from the decoder by a sum with the low-resolution logits from the encoder, which we obtain via a concurrently trained 1 × 1 convolution. In Tab. 2 (right section), we report the results on the vali-dation set of COCO-Stuff in terms of the mean IoU ( mIoU )and the average pixel accuracy ( pAcc ). LILA consistently and notably improves the segmentation quality across all baselines and the recent FlowFeat [ 6]. The absolute improve-ment tends to increase with the model size. For example, LILA boosts the mIoU of DINO2-S14 by +3 .0 % , whereas for DINO2-L14 the benefit amounts to +4 .1 % . This ob-servation suggests that LILA succeeds at leveraging more complex patterns available in the encoder network (via skip connections). To test the scalability further, we evaluate a LILA variant trained on Kinetics. Here, we observe a tangi-ble improvement across both mIoU and pAcc in comparison to the small-scale pre-training ( +0 .5 % / +0 .3 % , respec-tively). This suggests that LILA has the capacity to scale its semantic representation from uncurated videos. From the qualitative results in Fig. 5b, we note a dramatic improvement of the feature quality in terms of the spatial detail and semantic granularity. Limitations. LILA relies on cue maps, which derive from state-of-the-art off-the-shelf models (SEA-RAFT [ 50 ] for op-tical flow; DepthAnythingV2 [ 51 ] for depth). ## 5. Conclusion 

We presented linear in-context learning, an effective train-ing strategy for encoder-decoder networks. Learned from videos and imperfect cue maps, LILA effectively embeds temporal, semantic and geometric aspects into its pixel-level representation. ## A. Backbone generalisation 

We train LILA with different backbone families. Concretely, we experiment with a Masked Autoencoder (MAE-B16) [ 10 ], a DINOv2 variant equipped with registers (DINO2-B14-Reg) [ 52 ] and DINOv3 [ 3]. ## B. Practical details 

Training LILA is computationally inexpensive, a benefit we achieve through several practical considerations. For exam-ple, we downsample the context cue grid, Gcontext , by a factor of 7. This measure significantly improves the efficiency of ridge regression without effecting the downstream perfor-mance. We train all models on YouTube-VOS with a batch size of 32 on a single A100 GPU with 40GB of memory. The training typically reaches convergence after 30K iterations, which translates to approximately 24 hours. 

PAMR. We apply PAMR [ 41 ] in a coarse-to-fine fashion. Specifically, we define an image pyramid with three levels corresponding to the downsampling ratios of 4, 2 and 1 (i.e .the original resolution). We define the PAMR parameters identically at each level. The local affinity kernel consists of three 3 × 3 kernels with dilation ratios of 1, 3 and 5. At the coarsest resolution, the refinement runs for 20 iterations, and we reduce this number by a factor of 2 for each consecutive, finer resolution. This results in a total of 35 refinement itera-tions. Notably, this coarse-to-fine strategy is approximately four times more efficient than running all 35 iterations at the original image resolution.
