# PIDNet: Progressive Implicit Decouple Network for Multimodal Action Quality Assessment 

Qiqi Li, Pengfei Wang, and Nenggan Zheng, Senior Member, IEEE 

Abstract —Action quality assessment (AQA) aims to automat-ically quantify the execution quality of human actions in videos and is of great value in applications such as competitive sports judging. In multimodal AQA, quality evidence from different modalities is inherently heterogeneous, while quality cues evolve progressively over time. Existing methods largely rely on coarse-grained fusion or unified temporal modeling, which may blur modality-specific cues, preserve cross-modal redundancy, and weaken stage-specific quality evidence. To address these issues, we propose a progressive implicit decoupling and fusion net-work (PIDNet), which progressively integrates modality-specific information, cross-modal complementary cues, and global quality semantics to learn more discriminative representations for accu-rate quality assessment. Specifically, we design an iMambaWave module that projects RGB, optical flow, and audio features into a shared latent space, and disentangles them through a Bi-Mamba branch and a wavelet-transform branch to capture long-range temporal dependencies and local perturbation details, respectively. A gated aggregation mechanism is then introduced to adaptively fuse temporal and frequency domain information. Building on this, we construct a three-stage progressive fusion network with Group3M blocks as the core units. A modality complementary attention mechanism retrieves complementary evidence across modalities while suppressing redundancy, and multi-scale convolutions further enrich feature representations. Extensive experiments on the Rhythmic Gymnastics and Fis-V datasets show that, compared with existing unimodal and multi-modal methods, PIDNet achieves highly competitive score corre-lation while maintaining favorable error control. Ablation studies further confirm the effectiveness of each component. Moreover, iMambaWave consistently improves visual representation and temporal modeling across multiple backbones, demonstrating good generalization and plug-and-play capability. 

Index Terms —Action quality assessment, multimodal fusion, video understanding

In recent years, AQA has demonstrated significant application value in scenarios such as competitive sports scoring [3], [4], medical-assisted diagnosis [5], and skill training [6]. By reducing reliance on conventional evaluation methods that depend heavily on human expertise, it offers more objective and efficient technical support while helping to alleviate concerns related to subjectivity and fairness. The difficulty of accurate AQA lies in the fact that action quality cannot be reliably judged from a single frame or a few images alone, but instead requires modeling and understanding the entire action sequence. For long-term tasks such as figure skating and gymnastics [7], [8], the quality differences be-tween performers are often subtle, while the key evaluative cues are sparsely distributed throughout the sequence and must be accurately mapped to nonlinear professional scoring standards, such as gymnastics judging rules or rehabilitation criteria. Meanwhile, the final score depends not only on instan-taneous technical details, but also on the overall fluency and stability of the complete performance. Therefore, AQA places high demands on both fine-grained feature representation and temporal dynamic modeling. Early AQA methods relied almost exclusively on visual in-formation. Backbone networks such as video swin transformer (VST) [9], inflated 3D convnet (I3D) [10], and convolutional 3D (C3D) network [11] were widely used to extract spatiotem-poral appearance features from video sequences, capturing body pose, apparatus position, and spatial configuration, and were directly employed for quality score regression. Sub-sequently, optical flow was introduced as a complementary visual modality to RGB [12], offering greater sensitivity to motion magnitude, velocity variation, and local dynamic patterns. Most later multimodal approaches were built upon this visual foundation. Furthermore, graph-based modeling has been introduced into AQA, including skeleton-based graph convolutional networks (GCNs) that explicitly model joint topology to enhance pose representation [13], [14], as well as graphs constructed directly in the video feature space, where clips are treated as nodes to capture cross-clip semantic rela-tions and long-range contextual dependencies [15]. However, a common limitation of these methods is that all cues are confined to the visual domain, leaving non-visual signals such as audio and textual knowledge entirely unexplored [16]– [18]. We note that audio provides an irreplaceable source of quality-related information for long-term actions performed with background music. It conveys rich temporal structure, including action-music synchronization, variations in rhythmic intensity, and action boundaries, thereby offering complemen-tary evidence beyond vision and representing a promising direction for improving long-term AQA.

Accordingly, multimodal AQA research has gradually be-gun to incorporate audio information. Representative work extended the MLP framework to the audio-visual modalities and employed memory recurrent unit feature descriptions to model long-range representations, demonstrating the effec-tiveness of joint audio-visual modeling for figure skating assessment [19]. However, the fusion granularity remains rela-tively coarse, as features from different modalities are directly mixed, which can dilute modality-specific discriminative cues during fusion. Subsequent work [20] further introduced a progressive multimodal fusion network, which alleviates cross-modal interference by separately modeling unimodal branches and a mixed-modal branch, yet the fine-grained dynamic interaction between audio and video remains limited. More recently, language modality has also been exploited to inject domain-specific action knowledge for guiding audio-visual learning [16], [21], thereby enhancing cross-modal semantic association while reducing parameter dependence. Neverthe-less, these methods still tend to treat long-term actions as rela-tively homogeneous temporal processes and fail to adequately capture the stage-wise evolution of quality cues. In fact, long-term actions often involve multiple temporal structures simultaneously, with local short-term disturbances reflecting momentary instability or local technical deviations, and global long-range dependencies being essential for judging overall rhythm, coherence, and stability [22]. When a unified or one-shot temporal modeling strategy is applied to the entire sequence, key quality variations at different stages are likely to be averaged out, thereby weakening the expression of quality evidence. We argue that effective quality cues in AQA reside in both intra-modal representations and cross-modal correlations. Therefore, within each modality, the model should preserve and strengthen modality-specific discriminative information, so that critical details are not prematurely smoothed out during fusion. Across modalities, different modalities should not be treated as equally important at all times. Instead, the model should selectively suppress redundant and interfering information, enhance truly complementary interactions that contribute to quality judgment, and progressively form a stable global quality representation over time. Based on the above observations, we propose a progressive implicit decoupling network (PIDNet). Specifically, (i) we de-sign an iMambaWave module. For the RGB, optical flow, and audio modalities of an input video, we first project them into a unified latent space through an embedding layer. A portion of the split features is then routed to an identity branch to ef-fectively preserve native modality information while reducing computational overhead. The remaining features are decoupled and fed into the Bi-Mamba and wavelet-transform branches, respectively, enabling the model to capture both the global evolution of action patterns throughout the entire sequence and subtle transient motion variations. A gated aggregation mech-anism is further introduced to adaptively balance the contribu-tions of temporal and frequency domain information, thereby substantially enhancing feature representation capability. (ii) In addition, as illustrated in Fig. 2, we design a progressive fusion network consisting of three stages. At each stage, the core fusion unit, namely the Group3M block, facilitates effective information exchange among modalities. In particular, the modal complementary attention (MoCA) mechanism uses the fused-modality feature as the query to retrieve modality-specific evidence from each modality branch, and employs differential attention to inject complementary information into the fused-modal representation while effectively suppressing redundant cross-modal expressions. Meanwhile, MKConv is used to perceive information at different scales so as to enrich feature representations. (iii) Finally, experimental results on the Rhythmic Gymnastics and Fis-V datasets demonstrate that the proposed model outperforms existing unimodal and multimodal methods. Ablation studies further verify the ef-fectiveness of the key designs in enhancing quality repre-sentation capability and final scoring performance. Notably, our method builds upon PAMFN [20] and further extends it by introducing progressive implicit decoupling and adaptive multimodal fusion for quality-aware temporal modeling. A. Vision-Based Action Quality Assessment 

Vision-based AQA primarily relies on observable visual cues in videos, including appearance, pose, and motion dynam-ics, to automatically model and quantify action performance. As one of the earliest research paradigms in AQA, vision-based methods have continuously evolved around the central question of how to extract truly quality-relevant discriminative cues from visual sequences. Accordingly, their focus has gradually shifted from coarse-grained score regression over holistic video representations to deeper modeling of relative quality differences, fine-grained action structure, and long-range temporal dependencies [14], [23], [24]. Early AQA studies mainly focused on short-term actions such as diving [25], [26], typically using 3D convolutional or VST backbones to extract spatiotemporal features from full videos and directly regress quality scores. However, absolute score regression often encourages models to fit score distribu-tions rather than capture true quality differences, resulting in limited generalization. To this end, later studies reformulated AQA as a relative score learning problem, using pairwise comparison to highlight discriminative evaluation cues and group-aware regression decomposing predictions into coarse-to-fine hierarchical sub-problems [27], [28]. Building on this, procedure-aware methods further modeled the internal tem-poral structure of actions by decomposing performances into JOURNAL OF L ATEX CLASS FILES, VOL. 14, NO. 8, AUGUST 2021 3

semantically coherent steps and aligning corresponding stages across query and exemplar videos [4], [29]. Further, learnable-query-based temporal parsing eliminated the need for explicit step-level supervision by automatically discovering temporal structures, combined with contrastive regression to strengthen fine-grained quality understanding [30]. While these studies establish a solid methodological foundation for AQA, their applicability to long-term action sequences remains limited. Long-term actions typically comprise multiple sub-actions spanning several minutes, as exemplified by figure skating and rhythmic gymnastics, imposing heightened demands on models’ long-range temporal modeling and multi-dimensional scoring comprehension. To address non-uniform quality distri-bution in long videos, a grade-decoupling method based on the Likert scoring paradigm extracts grade-aware representations from video features via learnable grade prototypes, which are then explicitly modeled and aggregated into a final score [31]. To tackle the difficulty of localizing key technical actions, an uncertainty-aware score decoupling framework [32] disen-tangles features into presentation score and technical score representations, further incorporating a weakly supervised localization module and a temporal interaction encoder to integrate cross-segment context. Additionally, combining hier-archical grade representations with pre-training alignment ob-jectives has proven effective in enhancing the generalization of long-term models [33]. Nevertheless, vision-only approaches overlook non-visual quality cues such as accompanying audio, inherently limiting further performance gains. 

B. Multimodal Action Quality Assessment 

Recent efforts have incorporated non-visual cues, such as audio and language, into AQA to overcome the performance ceiling of vision-only approaches in long-term action mod-eling [3], [34]. Multimodal AQA exploits complementary cues from vision, audio, language, and domain knowledge to support more comprehensive quality judgment. As AQA has progressed from action observation to a deeper understand-ing of rhythm, style, rules, and judging logic, multimodal modeling has become increasingly important for improving quality perception and evaluation reliability. Existing work has advanced primarily along two directions, namely audio-visual fusion and vision-language knowledge guidance. In audio-visual fusion, the central challenge lies in pre-serving modality-specific discriminative information while establishing effective cross-modal complementarity. Skating-Mixer [19] introduced FS1000, an audio-visual benchmark for figure skating assessment, and demonstrated the advantage of joint audio-visual modeling over vision-only baselines. Sub-sequent work [20] mitigated inter-modal interference through separate modality-specific and mixed-modality branches, with an adaptive fusion module to dynamically adjust fusion across action phases. Wang et al. proposed a two-stream Mamba pyramid network for figure skating, modeling TES and PCS with a visual stream and an audio-visual stream, respectively, and employing a multi-scale Mamba pyramid to strengthen long-range temporal modeling and technical element localiza-tion [35]. However, audio-visual interactions in these methods remain confined to shallow feature alignment, failing to cap-ture the implicit, non-linear correspondences between musical attributes and visual motion. LMAC-Net [36] addressed this via an attention-driven cross-modal alignment mechanism that explicitly models deep temporal audio-visual collaboration, yet fine-grained dynamic interaction remains an open challenge. In vision-language knowledge guidance, the advent of large-scale pretrained language models including BERT [37], GPT [38], and DeepSeek [39] has endowed AQA with rich semantic priors and knowledge-driven reasoning capabili-ties [33]. Relevant work [40] incorporates language as an auxiliary supervisory signal through joint score-description modeling or language-prompted visual attention, establishing a foundational semantic-visual association paradigm. However, coarse-grained video-text matching falls short of AQA’s fine-grained temporal modeling demands. To this end, subsequent work [41] encodes action steps and scoring criteria into directed acyclic graph structures, enabling step-level cross-attention alignment between action execution and evaluation standards. Further, recognizing that general vision-language models lack effective encoding of professional judging knowl-edge, recent work [17] injects domain-specific expertise, such as sport judging rules and surgical skill criteria, in a learnable manner to enhance evaluation-aware reasoning. Nevertheless, a substantial semantic gap between abstract rule descriptions and low-level visual features remains a persistent challenge. III. M ETHOD

A. Datasets and Evaluation Metric 

We conduct experiments on two public long-term AQA benchmarks, including Rhythmic Gymnastics (RG) [42] and Fis-V [43]. We follow the criteria established by the datasets and prior works. 

Datasets. The RG dataset includes 1000 videos covering four gymnastics routines, namely ball, clubs, hoop, and ribbon, with 250 videos for each routine. Each video is annotated with difficulty score, execution score, and total score, all given by judges according to official rules. The total score is the sum of the difficulty and execution scores, minus penalties. The Fis-V dataset consists of 500 videos from international ladies’ singles short program figure skating competitions. Each video is approximately 2 minutes and 50 seconds long, with a frame rate of around 25 frames per second, capturing the entire performance of a single skater. All videos are annotated with the total element score and the total program component score.

B. Implementation Details 

We implement PIDNet in PyTorch and conduct both training and evaluation on an NVIDIA V100 GPU. The model takes temporal feature embeddings from three modalities, namely RGB, optical flow, and audio, as input. Specifically, RGB features are extracted by VST [47] pretrained on Kinetics-600 [10], optical flow features are extracted by I3D net-work [10] pretrained on Kinetics-400 [10], and audio features are extracted by audio spectrogram transformer (AST) [48] pretrained on AudioSet [49]. The corresponding feature di-mensions dV, dF, and dA are 1024, 1024, and 768, respec-tively. To ensure temporal alignment across modalities, all modality sequences are unified to a fixed segment length during training via random cropping or zero padding, with the segment length set to 70 for the RG dataset and 130 for the Fis-V dataset. We use AdamW as the optimizer, with an initial learning rate of 8 × 10 −4, a weight decay of 1 × 10 −4,and a batch size of 64, while gradient clipping is applied. For the model configuration, the MKConv kernel parameter 

K is set to 3, modality splitting is enabled with a ratio of 0.25, the number of 1D wavelet decomposition levels q is set to 1. Different training epochs are adopted for different action categories. On the RG dataset, ball/clubs/hoop/ribbon are trained for 400/300/300/400 epochs, respectively. On the Fis-V dataset, both TES and PCS are trained for 300 epochs. 

C. Comparisons with State-of-the-arts 

Comparisons on the Rhythmic Gymnastics dataset. The RG dataset is characterized by complex human–apparatus interactions and pronounced long-range temporal dependen-cies, posing substantial challenges to the representation ca-pacity and cross-modal modeling ability of AQA methods. Table I reports the comparison between PIDNet and existing approaches on the RG dataset. As can be seen, PIDNet achieves the best overall performance, with an average Sp. Corr. of 0.848 and an average MSE of 4.53, outperforming all competing methods. Compared with current state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods, PIDNet surpasses both PAMFN and QGLV in terms of average Sp. Corr. and average MSE. In particular, compared with PAMFN, which adopts the same multimodal input setting, PIDNet improves the average Sp. Corr. from 0.819 to 0.848 and significantly reduces the av-erage MSE from 6.64 to 4.53. These results indicate that the performance gain arises not merely from the use of multimodal features, but more importantly from the proposed architecture, which more effectively exploits and fuses complementary information across modalities. For the individual apparatus categories, PIDNet achieves the best Sp. Corr. on clubs, hoop, and ribbon, reaching 0.831, 0.879, and 0.863, respectively. Notably, although PIDNet (0.808) yields a slightly lower Sp. Corr. than QGLV (0.828) on ball, it achieves a better MSE (4.81 of PIDNet and 4.83 of QGLV). A possible reason is that quality differences in ball are reflected more heavily in apparatus control, which weakens the advantage of PIDNet in modeling cross-modal dynamic complementarity. In addition, under the MSE metric, PIDNet achieves the best results on ball, clubs, and ribbon, and the second-best result on hoop, indicating strong stability and generalization across most ap-paratus categories. A further comparison across different types of methods shows that early models based on single visual features, such as C3D-SVR and MS-LSTM, exhibit limited overall performance. With the introduction of a stronger visual backbone, VST, the performance of single-modality methods improves substantially. However, PIDNet still achieves better average performance than these single-modality approaches, suggesting that visual representation alone is insufficient to fully characterize rhythmic variation and temporal details in AQA, whereas combining RGB, optical flow, and audio pro-vides a more complete basis for quality assessment. Overall, these results strongly validate the effectiveness and superiority of PIDNet for multimodal AQA. 

Comparisons on the Fis-V dataset. Table II reports the comparison between PIDNet and existing methods on the Fis-V dataset. Overall, PIDNet demonstrates strong compre-hensive performance on this dataset, with both the average Sp. Corr. and MSE reaching leading or second-best levels among current SOTA methods. Specifically, on PCS, PIDNet achieves the highest Sp. Corr. of 0.877 , surpassing QGLV (0.868 ), PAMFN ( 0.872 ), and VATP-Net ( 0.863 ), which indi-cates that the proposed method is more effective at capturing program-component-related attributes such as choreography, expressiveness, and execution quality. On TES, PIDNet at-tains the second-best result of 0.744 , while still substantially outperforming previous methods such as CoFInAI ( 0.716 )and GDLT ( 0.685 ). It is worth noting that QGLV adopts “VST+AST+CLIP” combination, which introduces additional vision–language representations from CLIP, whereas both PIDNet and PAMFN are based on “VST+AST+I3D” features. Therefore, the superior performance of PIDNet over PAMFN more directly reflects the effectiveness of the model archi-tecture itself under a comparable input setting. In particular, PIDNet improves the PCS Sp. Corr. from 0.872 to 0.877 and reduces the average MSE from 15 .33 to 13 .21 , demonstrating that the proposed progressive implicit decoupling strategy can more effectively improve prediction accuracy while reducing regression error. Moreover, even when compared with the advanced QGLV method that leverages CLIP representations, PIDNet still remains highly competitive, which further vali-dates its effectiveness for long-term AQA.

In contrast, adding Bi-Mamba yields larger improvements than adding Wavelet alone, suggesting that bidirectional temporal modeling plays a more critical role in capturing long-range quality dynamics. When Bi-Mamba and Wavelet are jointly used, the model consistently achieves the best results across most action categories, demonstrating their strong complementarity. Effects of Stage-wise Progressive Modeling . Table VII compares the performance of the one-stage, two-stage, and all-stage settings on the RG dataset. Overall, all-stage achieves better performance than both one-stage and two-stage in terms of average Sp. Corr. and average MSE. This is because, as more stages are involved in modeling, the model can aggregate longer-range contextual information and form higher-level semantic representations, thereby better capturing the rhythmic structure, long-range temporal dependencies, and overall exe-cution quality required for AQA. It should be noted, however, that although increasing the number of stages introduces richer information, it also strengthens the redundancy and diversity among features. Effects of Fusion Strategies . Multi-stage modeling en-riches feature representations, while the fusion strategy further determines whether such hierarchical information can be ef-fectively utilized. Table VII reports the ablation results of dif-ferent fusion strategies on the RG dataset. Under the all-stage setting, the average Sp. Corr. of sum and concat is 0.820 and 0.810, respectively, whereas gated substantially improves it to 0.848. Meanwhile, the average MSE is reduced to 4.53, clearly outperforming the other two fusion schemes. This is because, as more stages are involved in fusion, feature redundancy and heterogeneity across different levels and modalities also increase. Simple summation or concatenation cannot adapt to the varying requirements of different time steps, whereas gated fusion can adaptively select and reweight features from different sources, yielding more flexible and discriminative enhanced representations than fixed fusion strategies. 

Effects of Modality Combinations . Table VIII presents the ablation results for various modality combinations on the RG dataset. The full three-modality PIDNet achieves the best performance, with both the average Sp. Corr. and average MSE clearly outperforming all single-modal and bimodal settings. Among single-modal, RGB performs the best, with an average Sp. Corr. of 0.759, followed by optical flow, while audio alone yields much weaker results. This indicates that pose appearance and human-apparatus spatial relationship information from RGB serve as the primary cues for AQA, while optical flow provides useful dynamic complements. By contrast, audio has limited discriminative power when used alone and is better suited as an auxiliary modality in multimodal fusion. Among bimodal settings, “RGB+Flow” achieves the best performance, reaching an average Sp. Corr. of 0.803 and an average MSE of 4.81, clearly surpassing the other two bimodal combinations. This suggests that RGB and optical flow offer the strongest synergy. In contrast, the gains from audio-involved bimodal settings remain limited, indicating that the rhythmic and temporal cues from audio are more effective when jointly modeled with both appearance semantics and motion dynamics.

Split Ratio . To evaluate the effect of the split ratio α

Computational Efficiency Analysis . Table XI compares the computational efficiency and performance of different methods on the RG dataset. With the further introduction of the optical-flow modality, the model performance improves markedly. Notably, PIDNet achieves the best result under the same trimodal input setting, indicating that its performance gain is not merely due to the use of more input modalities, but also to the effectiveness of its architectural design. PIDNet is able to model complementary relationships among multimodal features more thoroughly and capture score-relevant temporal dynamics more effectively. Although its parameter count and inference time increase to 25.89M and 52ms, respectively, the additional overhead remains acceptable and yields the best evaluation performance, demonstrating the stronger practical potential of PIDNet in AQA scenarios where high accuracy is required.

These observations suggest that iMambaWave performs adaptive branch selection across both stages and modalities, enabling more flexible temporal modeling for multimodal AQA. V. C ONCLUSION 

In this paper, we address the limitations of multimodal AQA in modeling long-range temporal dependencies, capturing fine-grained quality cues, and fully exploiting multimodal repre-sentations by proposing a cross-modal interaction and feature enhancement network, termed PIDNet. The proposed iMam-baWave module achieves implicit feature disentanglement through a splitting strategy, and effectively integrates identity features, the bidirectional temporal modeling capability of Bi-Mamba, and the multi-scale frequency-aware enhancement of Wavelet, thereby enabling more comprehensive capture of lo-cal details, global dynamics, and discriminative patterns across temporal scales in action sequences. In addition, we design a progressive fusion network that can be efficiently integrated with iMambaWave to progressively enhance modality-specific information exchange during cross-modal interaction while re-ducing information redundancy. Extensive experiments on the RG and Fis-V multimodal datasets demonstrate the superior performance of the proposed method, validating the accuracy, robustness, and generalization ability of PIDNet in complex action scenarios.
