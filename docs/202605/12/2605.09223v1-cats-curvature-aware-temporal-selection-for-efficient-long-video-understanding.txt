## Abstract 

Understanding long videos with multimodal large language models (MLLMs) requires se-lecting a small subset of informative frames under strict computational budgets, where ex-haustive processing is infeasible and optimal selection is combinatorial. We propose CATS 1,a curvature-aware frame selection method that explicitly models the temporal geometry of query–frame relevance to identify salient events and their surrounding context. By leverag-ing temporal curvature to adapt selection density, CATS captures both abrupt transitions and gradually evolving content while suppressing redundant frames. Under a fixed back-bone and frame budget, CATS consistently outperforms prior lightweight approaches such as AKS on LongVideoBench and VideoMME. While multi-stage methods such as MIRA achieve higher absolute accuracy, they incur substantial computational overhead; in con-trast, CATS retains approximately 93–95% of MIRA’s performance while requiring only 3–4% of its preprocessing cost, yielding a favorable efficiency–accuracy trade-off. Beyond answer accuracy, we evaluate description generation using an LLM-as-a-judge protocol and the obtained results show that CATS produces more coherent and informative outputs, indicating improved grounding in visual evidence. These results position CATS as a com-putationally efficient and principled approach to long-video understanding.

## 1 Introduction 

Long-video understanding has emerged as a central challenge in multimodal reasoning, where models are required to analyze extended video sequences and answer queries or identify relevant events (Bai et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023). Despite recent progress, these models remain fundamen-tally constrained by input budget, as directly processing all frames of a video is computationally prohibitive due to the large number of visual tokens (Tang et al., 2025a). Consequently, effective video understanding depends on selecting a small subset of frames that preserves information relevant to the input query. This selection process is inherently combinatorial, since the number of possible frame subsets grows exponentially with video length, rendering optimal selection intractable. Designing efficient and principled frame selection strategies is therefore critical for enabling scalable and accurate long-video reasoning, while balancing the need to capture salient content and avoid redundant selections across time. Existing methods approach this problem using a spectrum of strategies with varying complexity. Early approaches rely on simple heuristics such as uniform sampling (Zhang et al., 2024a), top-K relevance-based selection (Tang et al., 2025a), or restricting the model to a small number of frames (Lin et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024b). While computation-ally efficient, these methods treat frames largely in isolation and fail to capture temporally localized and context-dependent information. More structured approaches attempt to balance relevance and coverage. For instance, Adaptive Keyframe Sampling (AKS) (Tang et al., 2025a) formulates frame selection as a trade-off between query-frame relevance and temporal coverage, using a recursive judge-and-split strategy to allo-cate keyframes across the video. More recently, MIRA (Hao et al., 2026) introduces a multi-view retrieval framework that combines relevance and causality-based scoring with adaptive routing for keyframe selection, achieving strong performance through richer modeling of video content. Despite these advances, existing approaches exhibit a fundamental limitation in how they model temporal relevance. Heuristic methods lack query-aware temporal reasoning, while AKS relies on recursive partitioning and multiple control parameters, making its behavior sensitive to hyperparameter choices and prone to coverage-dominated allocations that resemble near-uniform sampling in certain regimes. Moreover, it does not explicitly model local variations in query-conditioned relevance, limiting its ability to adapt to rapidly changing semantic content. ## 2 Related Works 

Recent advances in multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have enabled unified reasoning over visual and textual inputs. Early and mainstream works such as LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023), Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023), MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023), InternVL (Chen et al., 2023), GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024), and NVILA (Liu et al., 2025b) extend large language models to the visual domain by encoding images as visual tokens. While effective for image-level understanding and broad multimodal reasoning, these approaches assume limited visual inputs and do not directly address the challenges of long video reasoning. To extend MLLMs to videos, subsequent methods adopt frame sampling strategies while reusing image-based architectures. Video-LLaVA (Lin et al., 2023) employs sparse uniform sampling, which often misses temporally localized events, while LLaVA-Video (Zhang et al., 2024b) increases sampling density but remains constrained by context length and redundancy. PLLaVA (Xu et al., 2024b) improves parameter efficiency, yet its performance remains highly dependent on the quality of selected frames. The importance of effective frame selection becomes more pronounced in long-video benchmarks such as LongVideoBench (Wu et al., 2024), VideoMME (Fu et al., 2024), EgoSchema (Mangalam et al., 2023), Ego4D (Grauman et al., 2022), and MLVU (Zhou et al., 2024), which require reasoning over extended temporal contexts. However, many of these benchmarks rely on multiple-choice question answering, where correct predictions may arise from partial cues, limiting their ability to fully evaluate temporal reasoning and contextual understanding. Existing approaches for frame selection can be broadly categorized into training-based and training-free paradigms. Training-based methods learn frame importance or temporal reasoning through supervision, as exemplified by Frame-Voyager (Yu et al., 2024), ViLaMP (Cheng et al., 2025), and BIMBA (Islam et al., 2025), which incorporate learned scoring or redundancy reduction mechanisms. Related efforts train lightweight selectors using pseudo spatial and temporal labels (Hu et al., 2025b), distill effective training and inference practices for long-range temporal understanding as in Apollo (Zohar et al., 2025), or combine supervised fine-tuning with preference optimization to better align reasoning with human judgments (Li et al., 2025b). TSPO (Tang et al., 2025b) further explores lightweight policy adaptation for long-video reasoning. While effective, these approaches require additional training, hyperparameter tuning, and computational resources. In contrast, training-free methods operate entirely at inference time. Pre-processing methods select infor-mative frames before reasoning: VideoTree (Wang et al., 2025a) performs hierarchical search but relies on clustering heuristics that may overlook fine-grained events, CoS (Hu et al., 2025a) performs pseudo temporal grounding via compact video coding, and BOLT (Liu et al., 2025a) and related methods use proxy relevance signals from pre-trained encoders that may not align with query-specific semantics. Other retrieval-oriented methods perform multi-round temporal search with object-centric cues (Ye et al., 2025b) or construct in-terleaved image-text streams to preserve both relevance and temporal continuity (Fang et al., 2025b). Acomplementary line of work improves efficiency through built-in compression: SF-LLaVA (Xu et al., 2024a) uses parallel token transmission with different sampling rates, AdaRETAKE (Wang et al., 2025b) performs adaptive compression across time and transformer layers, and APVR (Gao et al., 2025) combines query-aware 4Q: How many kinds of chords are played in the video?

## 3 Methodology 

We formalize the problem of query-guided frame selection in long videos and introduce CATS , a curvature-aware temporal selection method that explicitly models the temporal structure of query–frame relevance as a signal over time. 

3.1 Preliminaries 

We consider video question answering, where a model is given a video V and a textual query Q, and is required to generate a textual response. A video is represented as a sequence of frames V = F1, F 2, . . . , F T ,where T denotes the total number of frames. 5Modern multimodal large language models (MLLMs) encode selected frames into visual tokens and process them jointly with the query. Due to limited context capacity, only a subset of frames can be processed. This necessitates a keyframe selection function that selects indices I ⊂ 1, . . . , T with |I| = M , where M is constrained by the model. The objective is to select frames that maximize their utility for answering the query:

U({Ft | t ∈ I} , Q),

where U(·) denotes query-dependent utility. Direct optimization is intractable due to the combinatorial search space and the absence of explicit supervision, motivating efficient approximations based on proxy signals. 

3.2 Principles of Keyframe Selection 

A natural proxy for utility is query–frame relevance. Let s(Q, F t) denote a relevance score between the query and frame Ft, computed using a pre-trained vision-language model (e.g., CLIP) without additional training. A straightforward strategy is to select the top-K frames with the highest scores; however, this often yields redundant selections, as temporally adjacent frames tend to exhibit similar content and comparable relevance. To mitigate redundancy, prior methods (Tang et al., 2025a) introduce diversity or coverage constraints. While such mechanisms improve temporal spread, they typically rely on heuristic assumptions about the distribution of informative content. In practice, the temporal relevance signal is highly non-uniform and query-dependent: informative content may manifest as sharp peaks corresponding to short, critical events, or as extended regions where relevance evolves gradually. Consequently, effective frame selection requires modeling the temporal geometry of the relevance signal rather than relying solely on score magnitude or predefined allocation schemes that we are going to address in the section that follows. 

3.3 CATS: Curvature-Aware Temporal Selection 

We propose CATS , an iterative keyframe selection algorithm that operates on the temporal relevance sig-nal. Conceptually, CATS can be interpreted as an adaptive sampling strategy over this signal, where local geometric properties guide the allocation of the frame budget. Given frame-level relevance scores, CATS fol-lows a greedy selection procedure with adaptive suppression to balance informativeness and redundancy. At each iteration, the frame with the highest remaining relevance score is selected, and a temporal suppression mechanism is applied to its neighborhood. ## 4 Empirical Evaluation 

This section presents the empirical evaluation of CATS. We first compare against prior methods on bench-mark datasets, namely VideoMME and LongVideoBench, and then examine efficiency, robustness, and the contribution of each component, namely frame budget, curvature, and decay, with a focus on evaluating whether explicitly modeling the temporal structure of relevance enables efficient and effective frame selec-tion under limited computational budgets. 

4.1 Experiment Design 

We evaluate CATS on standard long-video understanding benchmarks, focusing on both answer accuracy and efficiency. Specifically, we report results on LongVideoBench (LVB) and VideoMME, which require reasoning over extended temporal contexts. Table 1 summarizes the overall performance compared to prior methods. As shown in the table, CATS consistently outperforms prior lightweight, training-free frame selection methods under the same backbone and frame budget. In particular, compared to AKS, CATS achieves +0.45% on LongVideoBench and +0.56% on VideoMME, demonstrating that explicitly modeling the temporal structure of the relevance signal leads to more informative and context-aware frame selection. The results exhibit deterministic behavior across multiple runs. Compared to larger or closed-source MLLMs these results further highlight that improved frame selection (not increased model scale) can drive performance gains, enabling competitive accuracy with significantly fewer visual tokens and a smaller backbone. We further analyze this trade-off from a computational perspective. While multi-stage retrieval approaches such as MIRA achieve higher absolute accuracy, they incur substantially higher computational cost due to multi-stage retrieval and iterative scoring pipelines. In contrast, CATS is designed as a training-free, low-compute method that directly operates on the temporal relevance signal. A.1 A.1 Description Generation Prompt 

To assess whether selected frames support faithful and comprehensive video understanding, we generate structured descriptions using a standardized prompt. The prompt is designed to separate (i) question-specific visual evidence and (ii) broader domain coverage, ensuring that the generated descriptions capture both immediate relevance and contextual completeness. The exact prompt used for description generation is as follows: 

You are an expert visual anthropologist analyzing a video clip frame-by-frame. 

Below is a multiple-choice question and its options. 

YOUR TASK IS NOT TO CHOOSE A, B, C, or D. 

Instead, generate a detailed, standalone two-part description of what the question is really asking about, based ONLY on what you perceive in the provided video frames. 15 Part 1 – Direct Frame Evidence (question-specific) 

Describe exactly what you see in the frames that relates directly to the question: objects, actions, sequence of events, timeline, text overlays, labels, diagrams, animations, people, environments, tools, or any other visual elements. Be exhaustive — list every relevant visual detail and how they connect to the exact topic of the question. Imagine you are writing precise field notes that another researcher could use to reconstruct the exact scene without watching the video. 

Part 2 – Full Domain Coverage (everything related that might be questioned later) 

Now describe everything related to the question’s domain or topic that you can see anywhere in the frames. Cover every single aspect, sub-topic, variation, step, component, related object, concept, sequence, or idea that belongs to the same broader domain — even if it is not directly mentioned in the question. Be completely exhaustive so that any future question on any part of this domain can be answered from this description alone. Include all key principles, methods, alternatives, applications, or connections that are visible in the video. Write as if you are creating a detailed encyclopedia entry that fully maps the entire topic shown in the video. 

Rules: 

• Base everything strictly on visuals you actually see — never invent or assume content. 

• Use precise, descriptive, and educational language. 

• Do NOT mention the options, the correct answer, or say “the question is testing...”. 

• Do NOT summarize; be as detailed and comprehensive as possible in both parts. 

• Structure your response clearly with Part 1 and Part 2 headings.

A.2 A.2 LLM-as-a-Judge Evaluation Prompt 

To compare the quality of generated descriptions, we adopt an LLM-as-a-judge protocol. The evaluation focuses on two criteria with equal weight: (i) question-specific visual grounding and (ii) coverage of additional relevant context for future queries. The judge is instructed to make a strict binary decision between two descriptions, without allowing ties, and to focus exclusively on the defined evaluation criteria. The exact prompt used for evaluation is as follows: 

You are a strict, impartial judge evaluating two already-generated descriptions from a video understanding benchmark. 

You will be given: 

• The original multiple-choice question 

• The ground-truth correct answer (A, B, C, or D) — used ONLY to define the scene boundary 

• Description 1 (Part 1 + Part 2 from Model 1) 

• Description 2 (Part 1 + Part 2 from Model 2) These descriptions were produced using a frame-grounded generation prompt. You MUST assume every statement in both descriptions is already strictly limited to visible frames. Your job is NOT to check grounding again. 

YOUR ONLY JOB: Decide which description is BETTER overall, using exactly these two rubrics (equal weight): 

Rubric 1 – Question-Specific Details (Part 1) 

Evaluate how precise, observable visual details are provided that directly relate to answering the given question. Score higher for: 16 • More exhaustive listing of objects, actions, people, tools, text, labels, spatial relation-ships, motion, and timeline elements that are relevant to the question. 

• Higher precision and reconstructability of the exact scene for this specific question. 

Rubric 2 – Related/Future-Question Coverage (Part 2) 

Evaluate how additional visible elements from the same scene/domain (question topic or related topic) are included that could support future questions on the same topic. Score higher for: 

• More exhaustive coverage of other objects, steps, contextual elements, variations, or supporting details inside the exact same scene (even if not needed for the current question). 

• Better readiness for follow-up questions without rewatching the video. 

Rules: 

• Judge ONLY on the two rubrics above — nothing else. 

• Use the correct answer only to confirm the scene boundary. 

• Ignore anything that is not related to the question at all. 

• Be extremely critical: the description with clearly more details on BOTH rubrics wins. No ties allowed. 

• Penalize any detail that drifts outside the current question’s scene (as defined by the correct answer). 

Response format (exactly like this):
