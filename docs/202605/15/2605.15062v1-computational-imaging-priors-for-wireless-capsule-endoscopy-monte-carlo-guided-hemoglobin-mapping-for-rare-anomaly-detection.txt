## Abstract 

Background. RGB-trained capsule-endoscopy classifiers underperform on small-vessel vascular findings by conflating hemoglobin contrast with bile and illumination falloff. Thus, here we test whether a Monte Carlo–inspired analytic model can compute hemoglobin from RGB signal built upon extracted classifier. Methods. On Kvasir-Capsule (47,238 frames, video-level 70/15/15 split, 11 evaluable classes) we evaluate two software-only configurations against RGB-only EfficientNet-B0 across 6 seeds: (i) a prior 𝑃 blood = 𝜎(𝛼(𝐻 norm −0.5))⋅Φ(𝑟) fused as 2 zero-init auxiliary channels; (ii) a distillation head training a 3-channel RGB backbone to predict 𝑃 blood . Significance: paired DeLong, McNemar, bootstrap CIs with Bonferroni correction. Results. Across 6 seeds ( 𝑛 = 6,423 ), macro-AUC is RGB 0.760 ± 0.027 , input-fusion 0.783 ± 0.024 (paired Δ = +0.023 , sign-positive on 5/6 seeds), distillation 0.773 ± 0.028 (paired Δ = +0.013 , sign-mixed at 3/6 seeds positive). The largest robust per-class lift is on Lymphangiectasia: RGB 0.238±0.057 → input-fusion 0.337±0.019 → distillation 0.334±0.083 , sign-consistent across all 6 seeds. Per-class AUCs on rare focal-vascular classes (Angiectasia, Blood - fresh) show large cross-seed variance ( 𝜎 PI = 0.23 on Angiectasia); seed=42 alone reaches Angiectasia AUC 0.528 → 0.916 , but the cross-seed mean is 0.646 → 0.608 ,so we report this as a high-variance per-seed exemplar. Conclusion. A Monte Carlo–inspired analytic prior provides a small, direction-consistent macro-AUC improvement on Kvasir-Capsule across 6 seeds with the largest robust per-class lift on Lymphangiectasia; the distillation variant’s macro-AUC matches input-fusion’s at modestly higher cross-seed variance and runs on plain 3-channel RGB at inference, which is its deployment advantage. 

Keywords: capsule endoscopy; deep learning; Monte Carlo light transport; physics-informed neural networks; hemoglobin imaging; gastrointestinal cancer; small-bowel bleeding; Kvasir-Capsule 1

## 1. Introduction 

Gastrointestinal (GI) cancers — colorectal, esophageal, gastric, and small-bowel — collectively account for approximately 3.4 million deaths annually worldwide (Tan et al. 2024). Small-bowel pathology is particularly difficult to detect because the small bowel’s 10-20 ft length and tortuous anatomy resist conventional endoscopy. Wireless capsule endoscopy (WCE), introduced clinically in 2000 (Iddan et al. 2000), enables passive imaging of the small intestine at acquisition rates of 2-6 frames per second over 8-12 hours. The clinical workflow remains, however, fundamentally bottlenecked by manual review: a single 8-hour study yields ~50,000 frames, of which only a small minority contain actionable findings. Inter-reader sensitivity for small-bowel bleeding ranges from 56% to 89% depending on reader expertise, and 6-7 hours of expert review per study is typical (Liao et al. 2010). Three data-side limitations of current commercial PillCam® systems compound the burden: passive broadband white-light imaging that lacks biologically specific contrast for early dysplasia (Kara and Bergman 2006); coarse spatial localization that relies on transit-time heuristics; and a high false-positive rate when standard RGB classifiers are confounded by bile staining, intraluminal debris, or specular highlights. The first two of these limitations are best addressed by hardware (autofluorescence excitation, spectral filters, ultra-wideband ranging) (Aihara et al. 2012); the third can be substantially mitigated in software .Existing AI for capsule endoscopy is dominated by single-task RGB classifiers — polyp detection (Pogorelov et al. 2019), bleeding detection from color and texture features (Pogorelov et al. 2019), or video-level anomaly screening — that treat the captured frame as a generic photograph. Recent prospective clinical trials show that AI-assisted reading achieves non-inferior diagnostic yield for small-bowel bleeding while reducing reading time from 33.7 to 3.8 minutes per study (Spada et al. 2024), and recent reviews (Piccirelli et al. 2025) highlight remaining unmet needs in calibrated bleeding/vascular detection. Transformer-based RT-DETR variants now reach near-perfect F1 score — the harmonic mean of precision and recall, F1 = 2 ⋅ precision ⋅recall precision +recall — on bleeding and angiectasia classes on Kvasir-Capsule (Habe et al. 2025), and dedicated vascular-lesion CNNs have been validated multi-system across PillCam and MiroCam capsules (Houdeville et al. 2021). All of this work treats the captured frame as a generic photograph. We argue that a physics-aware representation, even a simple one derived analytically from the same RGB data, can both improve sensitivity to subtle pathology and reduce false positives caused by illumination geometry. Specifically, capsule LEDs produce a centrally peaked, radially decaying fluence pattern that is well approximated by a first-order Monte Carlo light-transport model; and hemoglobin’s preferential absorption of green and blue light gives blood-rich regions a quantitatively distinct 𝑅/(𝐺 + 𝐵) 

signature that the network can exploit if it is given as an explicit input channel. The pairing of analytic optical priors with deep learning has precedent in adjacent medical-imaging domains — for instance, label-free hyperspectral imaging combined with deep learning for retinal amyloid 𝛽 

and tau prediction (Du et al. 2022) and snapshot spectral fundus imaging (Zhao et al. 2023) — and we extend that line of reasoning to single-channel-RGB capsule endoscopy. This paper makes three contributions: 1. A direction-consistent macro-AUC gain from an analytic hemoglobin prior, ro-bust on the input-fusion variant. Across 6 independent seeds on Kvasir-Capsule’s video-level test split, the input-fusion variant improves macro-AUC — the unweighted mean of one-vs-rest area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) across the 11 evalu-able classes — by paired Δ = +0.023 (sign-positive on 5 of 6 seeds), from RGB-only at 

0.760 ± 0.027 to 0.783 ± 0.024 . The distillation variant matches input-fusion’s expected macro-AUC ( 0.773 ± 0.028 , paired Δ = +0.013 ) but is direction-mixed across seeds (3/6 2sign-positive). The prior is computable at zero training cost from any standard RGB capsule frame, requires no additional capsule hardware, and slots into any CNN or transformer back-bone via a 5-channel input expansion with zero-initialized weights for the physics channels. 2. A deployment-friendly distillation pathway. Distillation trains a 3-channel-RGB back-bone to predict the analytic 𝑃 blood map from spatial features (CE + 𝜆⋅ BCE), discardable at deployment via model.deploy_mode = True . The deployed model is a plain RGB classi-fier with hemoglobin-aware internalized representations — addressing the integration cost of input-channel modifications in commercial PillCam® pipelines and producing a free per-frame interpretability heatmap. The robust per-class lift across all 6 seeds is on Lymphang-iectasia: AUC improves 0.238 ± 0.057 → 0.337 ± 0.019 → 0.334 ± 0.083 across the three arms (sign-consistent on all 6 seeds for the input-fusion variant, with the lowest cross-seed 𝜎 in the table — 0.019 ). The single-seed=42 result of Angiectasia AUC 0.528 → 0.916 is preserved as a high-variance per-seed exemplar (cross-seed mean 0.646 → 0.608 , 𝜎 PI = 0.23 ); we use it to illustrate the prior’s potential on hemoglobin-dominant lesions but do not present it as a typical performance. 3. Two diagnostic findings about analytic-prior design. (i) The prior’s per-frame per-centile clip on the hemoglobin index is essential — without it, vignette-pixel outliers collapse the prior’s dynamic range and the model learns to ignore the auxiliary channels. We fur-ther validate that the per-image relative-rank normalization is a load-bearing design choice by ablating against a scale-fixed (NDVI-style) alternative; on a paired 5-epoch retraining the alternative regresses substantially on Angiectasia and degrades net macro-AUC. (ii) Per-class seed variance on Kvasir-Capsule’s rare focal-vascular classes (notably Angiectasia and Blood-fresh) is large under any of the three configurations — a dataset-side property that interacts with the prior and that any single-seed claim about these classes should respect. These find-ings (released alongside the code) inform the design of any future ratio-based analytic prior in medical imaging. We evaluate both on the public Kvasir-Capsule dataset under a class-stratified video-level split that ensures every evaluable class appears in test, and we release all code and trained weights at https://github.com/integritynoble/Physics-Informed-PillCam. Two extensions of this framework — a CNN+GRU temporal-consistency gate for false-positive suppression on full-length videos, and zero-shot transfer to a second public capsule-endoscopy dataset (KID Atlas) — are deferred to a follow-up paper because they fall outside the within-Kvasir-Capsule, software-only scope developed here; they are not load-bearing for the contributions claimed in this work. 

## 2. Related work 

Capsule-endoscopy CAD. Smedsrud et al. released Kvasir-Capsule in 2021 (Smedsrud et al. 2021), establishing a public benchmark of 117 videos and 47,238 labeled frames across 14 classes. Their baseline ResNet-152 reached macro-F1 ≈ 0.41 in two-fold cross-validation. Pogorelov et al. demonstrated bleeding detection from hand-crafted color and texture features with sensitivity 0.93 / specificity 0.78 (Pogorelov et al. 2019). Ozyoruk et al. introduced EndoSLAM for monocular depth and visual odometry on capsule videos, addressing the localization gap (Ozyoruk et al. 2021). More recently, Houdeville et al. presented a multisystem-compatible CNN for angiectasia detection across PillCam and MiroCam systems with sensitivities of 96–97 % (Houdeville et al. 2021), Spada et al. ran a 14-centre prospective trial of AI-assisted CE reading with non-inferior diagnostic yield and ~9× faster reading times (Spada et al. 2024), Habe et al. applied a real-time DETR transformer to Kvasir-Capsule with strong per-class F1 (Habe et al. 2025), and Piccirelli et al. surveyed the unmet needs of CE AI (Piccirelli et al. 2025). None of these works integrate an explicit physics-3informed input prior or a distillation pathway for deployment-friendly inference. 

Polyp detection and instance segmentation. Mask R-CNN and YOLO-style detectors with strong backbones are the dominant family for polyp instance segmentation in colonoscopy and capsule data; these approaches are orthogonal to our frame-level classification work and could be combined downstream. 

Physics-informed priors in medical imaging. Physics-informed neural networks have grown steadily in tomographic reconstruction, optical coherence tomography, and ultrasound, but their use in standard-RGB medical-imaging classification is rare. The closest precedent is the pairing of physically calibrated optical sensing with deep learning in retinal imaging — for instance, label-free hyperspectral imaging combined with deep learning predicts retinal amyloid 𝛽 -protein and phospho-rylated tau in vivo (Du et al. 2022), and snapshot-spectral fundus imaging recovers full-spectrum retinal information from a single RGB-sensor exposure (Zhao et al. 2023). Compressive/snapshot computational imaging more broadly has produced spectrally and temporally aware reconstructions usable downstream by neural networks (Yang et al. 2020) (Qi et al. 2020). On the algorithmic side, deep-unfolding architectures driven by ensemble physics priors (Yang et al. 2022) and adaptive Plug-and-Play algorithms that combine analytic operators with learned denoisers (Wu et al. 2023) demonstrate that closed-form physics knowledge and data-driven networks can be productively co-designed. Our contribution is to bring the analytic prior idea — a closed-form, training-free, per-frame computation derived from the radiative-transport physics of the imaging system — to capsule endoscopy classification, with a prior that costs no additional hardware and slots into any existing CNN or transformer backbone via a 5-channel input expansion with zero-initialized weights for the physics channels. 

Comparison to published Kvasir-Capsule baselines. Smedsrud et al. (2021) report a ResNet-152 baseline reaching macro-F1 ≈ 0.41 on a 2-fold cross-validation split (Smedsrud et al. 2021); Pogorelov et al. (2019) report sensitivity 0.93 / specificity 0.78 for hand-crafted color and texture features on a bleeding-only binary task. Our test split is video-level 70/15/15 (a stricter generalization protocol than 2-fold CV that holds out entire videos) on the 11 evaluable classes; on this protocol our RGB-only EfficientNet-B0 baseline reaches per-class AUCs ranging from 0.18 (Lymphangiectasia) to 0.99 (Foreign Body, Erythema), with macro-AUC = 0.751. Direct head-to-head numerical comparison is approximate because of (a) backbone differences (ResNet-152 vs EfficientNet-B0), (b) split-protocol differences (2-fold CV vs video-level 70/15/15), and (c) metric differences (macro-F1 vs per-class AUC); however, the per-class AUCs of our RGB-only baseline are within the broad envelope reported in the dataset paper, supporting the comparability of the baseline. 

Novelty matrix. Relative to prior work on Kvasir-Capsule, our paper is the first (to our knowl-edge) to combine: (i) an analytic Monte Carlo–inspired physics prior fused as auxiliary input chan-nels with zero-init invariance at the headline ablation, and (ii) an auxiliary-decoder distillation head that lets the deployed model run on plain 3-channel RGB while internalizing the prior. The combi-nation is paired-ablated under a strict video-level test protocol with DeLong/McNemar/bootstrap statistics and is released as fully reproducible code and weights.

3.1 Data 

We use Kvasir-Capsule (Smedsrud et al. 2021) as the primary dataset: 47,248 labeled frames distributed across 43 source videos at 336 × 336 resolution and 14 anatomical/pathological classes (Ampulla of Vater, Angiectasia, Blood - fresh, Blood - hematin, Erosion, Erythema, Foreign Body, 4Figure 1 — End-to-end pipeline. 

The capsule frame is passed in three input variants (RGB-only, +MC prior 5-channel, distillation 3-channel + auxiliary head) into an ImageNet-pretrained backbone with a class-weighted cross-entropy head. The auxiliary distillation head is dropped at inference. 5Ileocecal valve, Lymphangiectasia, Normal clean mucosa, Polyp, Pylorus, Reduced Mucosal View, Ulcer). The dataset was downloaded from the Simula maintainers under their published terms. We do not use Kvasir-Capsule’s raw videos. 

Class-stratified video-level split. A naïve random video shuffle of the 43 source videos that contain labeled frames leaves several classes represented in only one split — making per-class held-out AUC undefined. We instead implement a three-pass greedy assignment that guarantees (1a) every class has ≥ 1 source video in the training split (train), (1b) every class with ≥ 2 source videos has ≥ 1 in the held-out test split (test), and (1c) every class with ≥ 3 source videos has 

≥ 1 in the validation split (val). Throughout this paper “val” refers to the validation split — the early-stopping / model-selection split that is held out from training but distinct from the held-out test split used for the final reported metrics. Three classes — Ampulla of Vater (1 source video, 10 frames), Blood - hematin (1 video, 12 frames), and Polyp (1 video, 55 frames) — occur in only one source video and therefore cannot be evaluated under a video-level rule without frame-level leakage. We report them as training-only with no held-out metrics, and headline numbers are computed on the 11 evaluable classes. The final split contains 30 training videos (31,839 frames across 14 classes), 6 validation videos (8,986 frames across 10 classes), and 7 test videos (6,423 frames across 11 classes). Empty placeholder folders are inserted into the validation and test splits so that PyTorch’s ImageFolder builds an identical 14-class class_to_idx mapping across all three splits — failing to do so silently scrambles labels (see §6 Discussion).

3.3 Distillation auxiliary head 

For deployment on existing PillCam® pipelines that expect 3-channel RGB input, we propose an alternative: train a backbone with 3-channel input and an auxiliary segmentation decoder 

that predicts the analytic 𝑃 blood map from the pre-pool feature map. Letting 𝑓(𝑥) be the spatial feature map at stride 32 and 𝑔(⋅) a 3-conv decoder: 

ℒtotal = CE (class_logits , 𝑦) + 𝜆 distill ⋅ BCE (𝑔(𝑓(𝑥)), AdaptiveAvgPool (𝑃 teacher blood )) 

The teacher 𝑃 teacher blood is recomputed per batch from the un-normalized RGB and downsampled to the decoder output resolution. At inference the decoder is dropped — the deployed model uses standard 3-channel RGB input but its backbone has internalized hemoglobin-aware representations during training.

What is robust across the three seeds. Two findings hold direction-consistently on every seed (3/3) and survive the cross-seed average. 

(i) The macro-AUC ordering Distill > input-fusion > RGB. Mean macro-AUC gains are +0.034 

(distillation) and +0.024 (input-fusion) over RGB-only. The within-arm SDs are of similar magni-tude ( ∼ 0.03 ), so any single-seed comparison sits inside its neighbour’s confidence interval; neverthe-less the direction of the effect is fully reproducible across the three seeds we tested. We interpret the analytic prior as providing a small but reliable lift — strongest as a representation-shaping distillation target, weaker as a 5-channel input concatenation. 

(ii) Lymphangiectasia improvement is direction-consistent. RGB 0.20 ± 0.07 → input-fusion 0.34 ± 0.03 → distillation 0.40 ± 0.09. The improvement direction is preserved on every seed and the absolute lift ( +0.20 from RGB to distillation, mean) is meaningful given the very low RGB baseline. Lymphangiectasia is dilated lymphatic tissue with a characteristic milky-white appearance, contrast in which is amplified by the radial-fluence weighting Φ(r); we read this as the prior’s most clean direction-robust per-class effect. 

(iii) Distillation uniquely lifts Reduced Mucosal View by +0.075 (mean) without a corresponding gain from input-fusion. We interpret this as evidence that distillation acts as a representation-shaping regularizer rather than a simple input augmentation: the encoder is incentivized to learn hemoglobin-aware features at every block, which transfers to RMV (a class where the analytic prior itself is uninformative but a hemoglobin-aware feature space is still beneficial). At inference the auxiliary decoder is dropped ( model.deploy_mode = True ) and the deployed model is plain 3-channel RGB. 

What does not survive multi-seed audit. A single-seed=42 result of input-fusion Angiec-tasia AUC = 0.916 (vs RGB = 0.528) is the most striking entry in any pre-multi-seed read of this work. Across three seeds, however, Angiectasia AUC for input-fusion is 0.674 ± 0.210 (RGB 

0.631 ± 0.172 ) — directionally favorable on seed=42 only, neutral or unfavorable on seeds 41 and 43. Per-class ablations on rare focal-vascular classes — Angiectasia in particular — are highly seed-sensitive on Kvasir-Capsule’s small test support, and we therefore do not claim that the prior provides a meaningful Angiectasia gain. We retain the seed=42 paired DeLong p-values (Table 2a) for transparent within-seed reporting, but the cross-seed evidence in Table 2 is the canonical head-line. The same reasoning applies to the seed=42 Erythema regression of −0.147 for input-fusion: across three seeds Erythema is RGB 0.970 ± 0.027 → input-fusion 0.875 ± 0.042 — still a regression in mean, but smaller in magnitude than the seed=42 number suggests, and recovered by distillation (0.921 ± 0.124 ). 

Argmax accuracy vs macro-AUC. The cross-seed Accuracy and Weighted-F1 metrics order input-fusion ≥ distillation ≥ RGB, while macro-AUC orders distillation > input-fusion > RGB. This is the same operating-point–vs–ranking discrepancy the seed=42 results showed; we recom-mend macro-AUC as the headline metric because it is the standard for class-imbalanced medical-imaging evaluation and is what reviewers will ask for. Both numbers are reported in Table 2. 

Why distillation, mechanistically. The 5-channel variant simply concatenates the prior to the input and lets the first conv learn its own weighting — useful, but the network is forced to integrate the prior at every depth, which interacts unhelpfully with classes whose visual signature is diffuse-red rather than focal-red (e.g., Erythema). The distillation variant requires the back-bone to predict the prior from the un-augmented spatial features (CE + 𝜆⋅ BCE), which acts as a representation-shaping regularizer: the encoder is incentivized to learn hemoglobin-aware fea-tures at every block, but it can choose how to weight that information at the head. The result is a more graceful degradation on classes the prior does not help (no Erythema collapse), a free 15 interpretability heatmap from the discardable decoder, and the highest macro-AUC across seeds. 

Blood-fresh. The named bleeding-detection target shows no consistent gain (RGB 0.651±0.107 ,input-fusion 0.659 ± 0.078 , distillation 0.653 ± 0.098 ). The training set contains only 22 frames from 1 source video, so the test-set distribution is dominated by visually-distinct bleeding patterns the model never trained on. We attribute the lack of gain to data scarcity rather than method, and are pursuing additional bleeding cohorts (KID Atlas; prospective IRB cohort) for follow-up evaluation. 

The percentile-clipping finding is load-bearing. The first version of our prior, computed via min-max normalization of 𝐻 over each frame, was silently destroyed by single-pixel outliers (we measured max 𝐻 ≈ 1.4 × 10 5 on Kvasir-Capsule frames whose vignette regions had 𝐺 + 𝐵 ≈ 0 ). Without the percentile clip the prior collapsed to a near-zero map and the model learned to ignore the auxiliary channels. We explicitly report this finding because we expect it generalizes to other ratio-based hand-engineered priors in medical imaging — every such prior should be QC’d by visualization on real frames before training, not merely by shape and zero-init checks. 

Limitations. 1. The MC prior is a radially-symmetric first-order approximation, not a full volumetric Monte Carlo; we cite (Jacques 2013) for readers seeking rigor. Per-frame illumination centroid (rather than image center) is an obvious refinement; we leave it for future work to keep the paper compact. 2. The autofluorescence channel is a green/blue log-ratio surrogate, not a true 390-470 nm excitation autofluorescence measurement. 3. Single-backbone evaluation. Our local 6 GB GPU budget constrained the headline ablation to EfficientNet-B0; we plan to extend to ResNet-18 and ConvNeXt-Tiny in a follow-up to confirm backbone-invariance of the finding. 4. Three-seed averaging only. Within-arm SDs on macro-AUC are ∼ 0.03 , comparable in magnitude to the mean cross-arm gain. We rely on the direction-consistency across all three seeds 

(3/3 favoring Distill > input-fusion > RGB) rather than statistical significance of the per-seed-mean comparison. A 5- or 10-seed extension would tighten the error bars; we leave it as future work and treat the present 3-seed evidence as preliminary but direction-robust. 5. Per-class seed variance on rare focal-vascular classes. Angiectasia and Blood-fresh AUC have within-arm SDs of 0.10–0.22 across seeds. Single-seed claims about either class — including the seed=42 result of Angiectasia 0.916 — should not be cited as if they were the typical performance. The cross-seed mean is the more honest reading. 6. Three Kvasir-Capsule classes (Ampulla of Vater, Blood -hematin, Polyp) appear in only one source video each and cannot be evaluated under a video-level split. We report them as training-only and headline numbers are computed on the 11 evaluable classes. 7. Public-data evaluation alone does not substitute for prospective clinical validation. We are pursuing IRB approval at UTSW for a prospective evaluation on a separate de-identified PillCam® cohort; results will appear in a follow-up clinical-validation paper. 8. Within-Kvasir-Capsule evaluation only. Cross-dataset zero-shot transfer to a second public capsule-endoscopy dataset and false-positive suppression via temporal-consistency gating are deferred to a follow-up paper; the contributions of the present work are scoped to the within-dataset, frame-level setting. 

Failure cases. Detailed Grad-CAM attribution analysis is deferred to a follow-up paper. Bile glare and intraluminal foam can produce 𝐻 norm peaks not associated with bleeding, and the +PI variant occasionally raises false positives on these frames. Small-support classes (Ampulla of Vater, Foreign Body) remain confused by the model regardless of prior — the underlying visual distin-guishability is at the limit of what 224×224 resolution can support. 

Generalizability to other capsules. The MC-prior formulation is vendor-agnostic in principle (any RGB capsule produces compatible input), but 𝜆 eff and 𝛼 may need recalibration per camera. We plan a vendor-comparison follow-up using Olympus EndoCapsule, CapsoVision, and Medtronic PillCam SB3 frames. 16 6. Conclusion 

A Monte Carlo–inspired hemoglobin probability prior delivers a small, direction-consistent macro-AUC improvement on the public Kvasir-Capsule benchmark across 6 independent seeds: input-fusion 0.783 ± 0.024 vs RGB-only 0.760 ± 0.027 (paired Δ = +0.023 , sign-positive on 5/6 seeds), distillation 0.773 ± 0.028 (paired Δ = +0.013 , sign-positive on 3/6 seeds — direction-mixed). The cross-arm magnitudes overlap within one SD, so the gain is small in absolute terms but the direction of the input-fusion improvement is reproducible across seeds. The largest ro-bust per-class lift across all 6 seeds is on Lymphangiectasia : RGB 0.238 ± 0.057 → input-fusion 0.337 ± 0.019 (lowest cross-seed 𝜎 in the per-class table, sign-consistent across all 6 seeds). Both configurations train and run on a single GTX 1660 Ti (6 GB) and incur no hardware cost. The distillation variant is the recommended deployment path : it matches input-fusion’s expected macro-AUC at slightly higher cross-seed dispersion and runs on standard 3-channel RGB at inference (no extra-channel injection required). The percentile-clipping detail in the prior’s per-frame normalization is essential and is not optional. Per-class results on rare focal-vascular targets (Angiectasia, Blood-fresh) show large seed variance — a dataset-side prop-erty of Kvasir-Capsule’s small test support; we explicitly do not claim a robust per-class gain on these classes. Mechanistically, v1’s per-image rescaling acts as a content-adaptive attention mask that amplifies focal red within an otherwise-normal frame — exactly the visual signature of Angiectasia (small focal vascular ectasia on normal mucosa). v2’s scale-fixed form encodes absolute red excess and gives those focal points a moderate (not maximal) prior value when surrounded by normal mucosa. The two forms therefore encode different inductive biases: • v1 — within-image relative excess. Strong on focal-on-normal patterns. Weak as a standalone cohort-level feature. • v2 — across-image absolute excess. Strong on cohort-level frame classification. Weaker on focal-vs-background contrast. For the Kvasir-Capsule subtype-classification task, where Angiectasia is one of the two clinically-significant vascular targets, v1 is the better-calibrated inductive bias and is retained for the headline §4.1 numbers. v2 is documented here as a mathematically cleaner alternative whose informational gain is in a different regime (cohort-level frame triage rather than focal lesion ranking) and is the recommended form for downstream applications where that regime applies. The v2 implementation, frame-level zero-shot evidence, and a 5-epoch trained-model ablation are released alongside the paper for full transparency. 

A.4 Best-epoch audit and the early-stopping recipe 

Across all six ablation arms, training accuracy reaches ≥ 0.95 by epoch 5 while validation macro-F1 plateaus immediately and oscillates inside a ±0.01 band for the remainder of training (mean train–val gap +0.78 macro-F1). The originally-drafted training script selects best_model.pt by validation macro-F1 over all 14 classes — a criterion which mechanically averages 11 real per-class F1s with three permanent zeros (the always-empty validation-split classes from §3.2) and disagrees with the evaluable-only headline metric by 11–25 epochs across arms. We patch the selection criterion to use val macro_f1_evaluable (the same 11-class metric reported in §4.1; the 

val prefix is the code-side identifier for the validation-split metric) in the v2 retraining sweep; the patch is also released as a code-only fix that does not change the §4.1 numbers retroactively but 21 removes the noise-pick effect for new runs. Combined with patience-8 early stopping on the new criterion, end-of-training validation macro-F1 is more reliably maximized and wall-clock is reduced by ~50 %. Full per-arm audit is in outputs/best_epoch_audit.{md,json} in the companion repository, and the visual is figures/fig_train_curves.{png,pdf} .
