## 1. Introduction 

A B S T R A C T

The quality of diabetic retinopathy (DR) screening relies on the ability to correctly grade 

severity; however, many deep -learning (DL) classifiers cannot be easily interpreted in the 

clinical context. This study presents a methodology that combines strong discriminative 

models with multimodal explanations, converting retinal pixels into clinically 

interpretable outputs. Using the APTOS 2019 benchmark, we evaluated six 

represent ative CNN - and transformer -based backbones under a controlled protocol 

with strat ified five -fold cross -validation. We then compared ensembling strategies 

(hard voting, weighted soft voting, stacking) and investigated a hybrid class -level fusion 

variant to exploit grade -specific advantages. For interpretability, we produced Grad -

CAM++ v isual attribution maps and short textual rationales using vision –language 

models (VLMs) conditioned on the fundus image and classifier outputs under 

conservative prompting constraints. Modern CNN backbones (ResNet -50 and 

ConvNeXt -Tiny) provided the stronge st single -model baselines, with cross -validated 

QWK up to 0.919 and 0.914, respectively. Ensembling improved ordinal agreement, and 

weighted soft voting was the most consistent across folds (QWK 0.934 , 0.017). Hybrid 

class -level fusion was competitive but did not yield a statistically reliable improvement 

over standard fusion in paired fold comparisons (Holm -adjusted p ≥ 1.000). For 

explanation quality, Grad -CAM++ offered plausible but coarse localization, and VLM 

rationales were generally grade -consistent. Quantitatively, VLM variants showed a 

trade -off between clinical completeness and template -level semantic similarity (coverage 

0.700 vs. BERTScore 0.072), while image –text alignment was comparable (CLIPScore 

ù 0 .34). Overall, the study sho ws that DR grading performance and multimodal 

explainability can be evaluated jointly with explicit protocols, motivating external 

validation and expert assessment.

Deep learning has become the dominant approach 

for grading DR from retinal fundus images over the last 

decade, owing to improved representation learning and 

large datasets. Despite high predictive performance, 

most models are difficult to interpret. A predicted grade 

in clinical workflows is rarely sufficient on its own; 

clinicians and screening programs must understand 

whether the model relies on plausible retinal evidence 

(e.g., lesion patterns) or confounding cues (e.g., imag -

ing artifacts, illumination changes, or camera -specific 

biases). This is reflected in studies that focus on strong 

assessments and ordinal metrics that have clinical sig -

nificance, such as quadratic weighted kappa (QWK). In 

cases in which there is a significant difference between 

the predicted and true grades, it leads to stronger 

penalization of disagreements ( Chilukoti et al. , 2024 ;

Akhtar et al. , 2025 ). 

In practice, grading is difficult because of subtle 

class boundaries and imbalanced data: mild DR is 

underrepresented in many screening datasets, whereas 

advanced stages are less prevalent owing to treat -ment 

and referral processes. This encourages models to 

optimize overall accuracy while underperforming on 

minority grades, which are often the most clinically 

significant. 

Ensemble learning has remained attractive for DR 

because complementary decision boundaries can re -

duce grade -specific failure modes. A recent study on 

DR grading using transfer learning and ensembles 

demonstrated that combining multiple backbones can 

improve ordinal agreement across standard benchmarks 

(Chilukoti et al. , 2024 ; Akhtar et al. , 2025 ). In addition, 

modern architectures encompass both convolutional 

networks and vision transformers, motivating hybrid 

combinations that can combine local texture sensitivity 

(CNNs) with stronger global context modeling (trans -

formers) in one pipeline (Boulaabi et al. , 2025 ; Aburass 

et al. , 2025 ). 

In such systems, explainability is commonly added 

using post hoc visual attribution methods, such as 

Grad -CAM and its variants. Grad -CAM provides an 

intuitive heatmap overlay showing where the model 

"looked" to support a decision by backpropagating gra -

dients to a chosen convolutional layer. When DR is per -

formed, heatmaps are often used to determine whether 

the model focused on lesion -rich regions. However, 

recent interpretability analyses on DR classification 

have shown that different attribution methods can yield 

inconsistent or conflicting explanations for the same 

image, and that explanation behavior can be strongly 

affected by the architecture and implementation details 

(Zhang et al. , 2025a ). A visually appealing heatmap 

does not necessarily translate into a clinically usable 

rationale for DR lesions, and practitioners often have 

difficulty converting diffuse saliency into a concrete 

interpretation of DR lesions because of their small size 

and spatial dispersion. These observations highlight 

three important limitations of explainability in current 

DR grading systems. 

First, heatmap -based explanations may vary sub -

stantially across attribution methods, reducing 

their consistency and reliability. 

Second, their behavior can depend strongly on 

the underlying architecture and implementation 

choices, making interpretation less stable across 

models. 

Third, even when visually plausible, saliency 

maps often remain too diffuse to provide a con -

crete and clinician -readable rationale for small 

and spatially scattered DR lesions. 

As a result of these limitations, multimodal expla -

nations are becoming more common, bringing them 

closer to clinical reporting. Vision -language models 

(VLMs) can translate visual evidence into short textual 

explanations, potentially bridging the gap between 

model internals and clinician -facing interpretations. 

Recent reviews of medical vision -language foundation 

models have noted rapid progress in multimodal in -

struction following and biomedical image understand -

ing. However, medical VLMs must be carefully eval -

uated and domain -specifically grounded. In particu -

lar, robustness remains a key issue: VLM results are 

susceptible to artifacts and changes in distributions, 

and systematic studies have demonst rated that medical 

VLMs can be adversely affected by realistic imaging 

perturbations and do not perform well in real -world 

images (and other images) (Cheng et al. , 2025 ). These 

findings underline that generating text is insufficient; 

explanation quality must be measured, and reliability 

risks must be explicitly addressed. 

Motivated by these limitations, our work moves 

beyond heatmap -only explainability by combining vi -

sual attribution with vision -language -based textual ra -

tionales, with the goal of providing explanations that 

are not only visually grounded but also more clinically 

interpretable and explicitly evaluable. 

Motivated by these trends, we propose a unified DR 

grading and explainability pipeline that moves from 

pixels to explanations . We first build discrimi -native 

graders by benchmarking representative CNN and 

transformer backbones on APTOS 2019 and then

## 2. Related Work 

For years, researchers have worked on automating 

the detection of diabetic retinopathy using color fundus 

images. This push has come from the growing demand 

for wider screening that does not depend solely on hu -

man experts. Standardized public datasets have shaped 

much of this development, offering a common ground 

for defining tasks and fairly measuring results. Owing 

to these resources, assessing how well different systems 

classify disease stages has become more reliable. They 

also allow clearer contrasts between various models and 

methods for training them ( Chilukoti et al. , 2024 ). 

Recently, greater attention has been paid to building 

systems resilient to differences in image clarity and 

uneven case distributions. At the same time, accuracy 

in matching clinical assessments remains a key measure 

of success ( Chilukoti et al. , 2024 ). 

Although older, convolutional neural networks hold 

up well because of their built -in assumptions about 

nearby pixel relationships and subtle anomaly detec -

tion. Because medical image sets often lack size or 

balance, practitioners continue to rely on pretrained 

models from vast photo collections to speed train -

ing and boost performance. Recently, efforts targeting 

diabetic retinopathy have shifted toward reshaping 

model structures, for example, using attention lay -

ers, to reflect varied lesion appearances and navigate 

complex classification edges. For example, CRA -Net 

introduces transformer -guided category -relation atten -

tion to model long -range dependencies while improving 

within -class representation ( Zang and Ma , 2024 ). In 

addition to such architectural contributions, several 

recent grading pipelines have reported strong ordi -

nal agreement using modern CNN families and task -

specific refinements, including EfficientNet -based de -

signs with attention or gating ( Dharrao et al. , 2025 )

and transfer -learning frameworks designed explicitly 

for stage grading ( Akhtar et al. , 2025 ). Comparative 

evidence also suggests that modern model families can 

differ meaningfully in their error profiles and decision 

behavior. For instance, vision transformers have been 

reported to outperform CNN baselines for referable

In parallel, vision transformers and hybrid CNN 

Transformer architectures have become increasingly 

prominent in fundus -based DR research. The main 

motivation is to combine CNN sensitivity to fine -

to model global structure and long -range dependen -

DR systems and hybrid designs aimed at improving 

classification performance and computational efficiency 

(Huang et al. , 2025 ). Hybridization has also been 

explored in practical grading pipelines. For example, 

hybrid EfficientNet –Swin designs have been evaluated 

to combine local lesion sensitivity with contextual 

modeling (Xu et al. , 2024 ). Comparative work has also 

moved beyond reporting accuracy alone by examining 

how CNNs, ViTs, and hybrid models behave in practice, 

including their error patterns and explanation stability. 

These studies suggest that architectural choice can 

affect not only grading performance but also the type 

and consistency of the explanations produced (Zhang 

et al. , 2025b ). Overall, the literature suggests that 

hybridization can be beneficial; however, the magnitude 

of gains varies across experimental settings, reinforcing 

the need for careful benchmarking and grade -wise 

analysis rather than reliance on a single best backbone. 

In addition to single models, ensemble learning 

remains a common strategy for improving robust -ness 

under label noise, acquisition variability, and subtle 

boundaries between adjacent grades. Standard 

ensemble approaches include hard voting, probabil -ity 

av eraging (soft voting), and stacking -based meta -

learning. Recent work reports that combining multiple 

models can improve severity -grading performance com -

pared with individual backbones, supporting the view 

that ensembles can reduce grade -specific failure modes 

(Chilukoti et al. , 2024 ). Related directions have also 

explored alternative fusion strategies beyond classic 

ensembling, such as representation -fusion approaches 

that combine complementary feature signals (Mohsen 

et al. , 2025 ) and multi -scale attention -based grading 

networks designed to improve fine -grained staging (Sun 

et al. , 2025 ). This broader body of work aligns with 

guidance in medical imaging that highlights pitfalls in 

metric interpretation and encourages transparent 

reporting across complementary measures matched to 

the clinical objective ( Kocak et al. , 2025 ). 

Explainability has also become increasingly impor -

tant because clinical stakeholders typically require not 

only a predicted grade but also evidence supporting 

the decision. Visual attribution methods are the most 

common, with Grad -CAM and its variants frequently 

used to highlight influential regions ( Selvaraju et al. ,

2017 ). However, DR lesions can be small and spatially 

dispersed, and post -hoc heatmaps are not guaranteed 

to be stable, specific, or clinically faithful. A DR -

focused interpretability study comparing multiple ex -

planation techniques reported substantial variability 

across methods and architectures, including sensitivity 

to modeling choices and potential emphasis on non -

lesion structures or artefacts ( Zhang et al. , 2025a ). 

These observations motivate explanation pipelines that 

go beyond heatmaps alone and provide more explicit, 

human -readable rationales while maintaining a clear 

evaluation protocol. 

More recently, VLMs have enabled multimodal ex -

planations by generating textual rationales conditioned 

on images and auxiliary signals, such as predicted prob -

abilities or localization cues. LLaVA -Med demonstrates 

that instruction -tuned VLMs can produce clinically 

styled responses when adapted to biomedical image –

text data ( Li et al. , 2023 ). Nevertheless, reliability 

concerns remain central, as medical VLM outputs can 

degrade under common image artifacts, raising practi -

cal concerns for deployment ( Cheng et al. , 2025 ). The 

evaluation of ophthalmology -focused multimodal large 

language models is also becoming more systematic. 

Recent benchmarking studies of fundus interpretation 

have shown that these models still struggle with sev -

eral basic ophthalmic tasks, highlighting the need for 

domain -specific training and more rigorous assessment 

frameworks ( Wei et al. , 2025 ). In the same direction, 

specialized training curricula have been proposed to 

improve vision –language model performance in retinal 

image analysis, suggesting a broader shift from generic 

prompting strategies toward ophthalmology -adapted 

multimodal learning (Holland et al. , 2025 ). Meanwhile, 

recent retinal diagnostic pipelines have begun to incor -

porate vision –language components directly into end -

to -end systems designed for transparency, reflecting 

growing interest in explanation generation as a core 

output rather than a secondary illustrative feature (La 

Rosa et al. , 2026 ). 

These reliability concerns have strengthened the 

case for a quantitative evaluation of generated expla -

nations instead of relying only on selective qualitative 

examples. Metrics such as BERTScore enable semantic 

comparisons between generated and reference texts, 

whereas CLIPScore offers a reference -free measure of 

image –text consistency (Zhang et al. , 2020 ; Hessel 

et al. , 2021 ). In parallel, community benchmarks in 

medical captioning and interpretable report genera -

tion have increasingly emphasized structured evalua -

tion schemes, including concept -level assessments and 

explainability -oriented criteria ( Damm et al. , 2025 ). 

Taken together, these developments support the need 

for pipelines that combine accurate grading models 

with multimodal explanatory outputs while evaluating 

those explanations explicitly, rather than treating them 

as supplementary illustrations.

## 3. Materials and Methods 

This section presents the dataset, preprocessing 

pipeline, model backbones, ensembling strategies, ex -

plainability framework, and evaluation protocols used 

in this study. It includes the proposed hybrid class -level 

ensembling scheme, Grad -CAM -based visual evidence 

localization, VLM -generated textual rationales, and the 

evaluation of both grading and explanation quality. 

Research Questions 

To structure the experimental design around clear 

objectives, we addressed the following research ques -

tions: 

These three research questions follow a clear and 

logical progression. RQ1 first establishes a baseline by 

comparing representative CNN and transformer back -

bones under the same training and evaluation settings, 

allowing us to see how architectural choices influence 

ordinal DR grading. Building on this, RQ2 investigates 

whether combining these models can reduce their in -

dividual weaknesses, especially in the presence of class 

imbalance and confusion between adjacent grades. RQ3 

then takes the analysis a step further by moving beyond 

prediction accuracy to interpretability, asking whether 

the final grading pipeline can also provide clinically 

meaningful multimodal explanations. In this way, the 

study progresses from backbone comparison to robust -

ness through ensembling, and finally to explanation 

quality. 

Dataset and Severity Labels 

We conducted five -class DR severity grading using 

the APTOS 2019 fundus image benchmark, which con -

tains 3,662 labeled images annotated into five ordinal 

stages: no DR, mild, moderate, severe, and proliferative 

DR ( Karthik et al. , 2019 ). The images were collected 

under heterogeneous acquisition conditions (different 

cameras, illumination, and fields of view), which intro -

blur, and background artifacts that could confound 

automated grading ( Karthik et al. , 2019 ). As shown in 

Figure  1, the class distribution was markedly imbal -

anced, with early stages dominating and severe classes 

occurring far less frequently; we explicitly accounted 

for this imbalance during training and evaluation. 

APTOS 2019 is well suited to the aims of this study 

for several reasons. First, its five -class ordinal label 

structure closely reflects the clinical grading setting 

addressed by our research questions. Second, the strong 

class imbalance makes it a useful benchmark for testing 

whether different backbone architectures and ensemble 

strategies can maintain reliability on underrepresented

Cross -Validation Protocol 

We employed stratified five -fold cross -validation to 

obtain performance estimates that are less sensitive to 

a single split and remain representative under class 

imbalance. The dataset was partitioned into five folds 

using stratified sampling on the DR grade labels, 

ensuring that the class distribution was approximately 

preserved within each fold. For each cross -validation 

run, four folds were used for training, and the remaining 

fold was used for validation; this was repeated five times 

so that each fold served as the validation set exactly 

once. Fold assignment was generated with shuffling 

enabled and a fixed random seed (random_state = 42) 

to ensure reproducibility. 

To minimize the risk of information leakage, model 

selection and hyperparameter tuning were performed 

separately for each training -validation split. Specifi -

cally, checkpoint selection is based only on the val -

idation performance for the corresponding fold, and 

the selected checkpoint is then used to compute the 

reported metrics for that fold. Th e final results are 

reported as the mean and standard deviation across 

folds. This protocol provides a controlled and repeat -

able evaluation framework, supports fai r comparisons 

across backbones and ensembles, and enables grade -

wise performance assessment in the presence of imbal -

anced class distributions. 

Image Preprocessing and Augmentation 

Fundus photographs exhibit substantial variability 

owing to acquisition conditions, including illumination 

changes, contrast variation, focus or blur, and sensor -

specific artifacts, all of which can confound automated 

grading. To ensure a controlled comparison across back -

bones, we applied the same preprocessing pipeline to all 

models. The images were resized to a fixed resolution 

of 224 İ 224 pixels, consistent with the standard input 

size commonly adopted by the selected pretrained 

architectures, including ResNet, ConvNeXt, DeiT, and 

Swin Transformer variants ( He et al. , 2016 ; Liu et al. ,

2022 ; Touvron et al. , 2021 ; Liu et al. , 2021 ). Local 

contrast was enhanced using contrast -limited adaptive 

histogram equalization (CLAHE), a technique com -

monly used in retinal image preprocessing to improve 

local contrast and lesion visibility (Imran et al. , 2019 ), 

with a clip limit of 2.0 and an 8 İ 8 tile grid. To avoid 

introducing a systematic mismatch between training 

and evaluation, CLAHE was applied stochastically 

during training ( p = 0.5) and deterministically during 

validation ( p = 1 .0). 

To improve generalization and reduce sensitivity to 

acquisition variability, we employed on -the -fly data 

augmentation during training. Geometric augmenta -

tions included random horizontal and vertical flips (p

= 0 .5 each), random rotations by multiples of 90 (p =

0.5), and mild affine perturbations (translation up to 

5%, scaling up to 5%, and rotation up to 30; p =

0.7). Photometric augmentation was implemented via 

random brightness and contrast adjustments up to 

,0 .2 ( p = 0 .7). In addition, to promote robustness to 

localized occlusions and spurious artifacts, we applied 

coarse dropout by removing up to eight rectangular 

regions per image. 

Finally, the images are normalized using ImageNet 

channel statistics (mean = [0.485, 0.456, 0.406], stan -

dard deviation = [0.229, 0.224, 0.225]) and converted 

into PyTorch tensors. The validation pipeline includes 

resizing, deterministic CLAHE, and normalization; 

however, geometric and photometric augmentations 

are excluded to guarantee a stable and comparable 

evaluation setup.

be better at capturing broader retinal contexts and

Training Objective and Model Selection 

To address class imbalance and reduce overconfident 

predictions, we trained all backbones using a class -

weighted cross -entropy objective with label smoothing 

and focal reweighting. Label smoothing was applied 

with a smoothing factor ϵ = 0.1, and focal modulation 

used a focusing parameter ц = 2 .0 to upweight hard 

(low -confidence or misclassified) samples. Class weights 

were computed from the training labels in each fold 

using a balanced weighting scheme and were applied 

consistently during optimization. 

To ensure a fair comparison across architectures, all 

backbones were trained under the same training proto -

col within each cross -validation fold. Model selection

Ensembling Strategies 

To improve robustness under class imbalance and 

reduce adjacent -grade errors, we evaluated multiple 

standard ensembling schemes and a proposed class -

level variant. For all ensembles, we used the same set 

of base models and operated on their predicted class 

probabilities and labels. The ensemble methods were 

selected to represent increasingly flexible ways of com -

bining complementary models, an approach that has 

been widely adopted to mitigate model -specific error 

patterns and improve robustness in medical image clas -

sification ( Chilukoti et al. , 2024 ; Akhtar et al. , 2025 ). 

Hard voting serves as a simple label -level baseline, 

weighted soft voting examines whether combining class 

probabilities can produce more stable predictions in 

the presence of ordinal ambiguity and class imbalance, 

and stacking tests whether a learned meta -model can 

improve fixed fusion rules ( Kocak et al. , 2025 ). The 

proposed hybrid class -level approach further explores 

whether different models are more reliable for specific 

DR grades, which is particularly relevant when adjacent 

classes are difficult to distinguish and minority severity 

stages are less represented ( Mohsen et al. , 2025 ; Sun 

et al. , 2025 ). 

Hard Voting: Each base model outputs a class 

label, and the final prediction is obtained by majority 

voting. In the event of a tie, we select the tied class with 

the highest mean confidence across the models 

involved in the tie. This tie -breaking rule is fixed and 

appl ied consistently across all experiments. 

Weighted Soft Voting: We combine models at 

the probability level by averaging their predicted class -

probability vectors using non -negative weights. The 

weights are estimated on the validation split using 

a predefined criterion (e.g., macro -F1 or QWK) and are 

then fixed during evaluation. This approach allows 

stronger base models to contribute more to the final 

prediction while maintaining a transparent, determin -

istic fusion rule at test time. 

Stacking: We implement stacking as a two -level 

scheme in which a multinomial logistic regression meta -

learner is trained to combine base model outputs. The 

meta -learner input is the concatenation of base model 

probabilities (or logits) for each class. To avoid 

information leakage, the meta -learner is trained only on 

validation predictions generated from base models not 

trained on those samples (i.e., fold -consistent out -of -

sample predictions). The learned meta -learner is then 

applied to the held -out evaluation data. 

Hybrid Class -Level Fusion: In addition to global 

weighting, we evaluate a class -level fusion strategy that 

assigns model weights separately for each class. Class -

specific weights are derived from validation behavior 

(e.g., class -wise recall/F1 or confusion tendencies), 

allowing models that are empirically stronger for 

particular severity stages to contribute more to those 

stages. Importantly, the class -wise weighting policy 

is estimated using only validation data and fixed for 

evaluation, ensuring that class -level specialization does 

not exploit test set information. Interpretable DR Grading with Vision -Language Models

Multimodal Explanation Pipeline 

To support both visual and textual interpretability, 

we employ a two -stage explainability pipeline that com -

bines gradient -based localization (Grad -CAM) with 

vision language model (VLM) rationales. Grad -CAM 

and VLM -generated rationales are combined because 

they serve different but complementary interpretability 

purposes. Grad -CAM helps to show where the model 

is focusing in the retinal image; however, it does not 

explain this evidence in a manner that is easy to 

interpret clinically. In contrast, VLM -generated text 

can express the models likely reasoning in more familiar 

clinical language without any visual groun ding, but it is 

more difficult to assess whether that explanation is tied 

to plausible image regions. Used together, these two 

components provide a more informative multimodal 

explanation by linking coarse visual evidence with 

concise textual justification, in line with the aim of 

RQ3 .

Visual Explanations with Grad -CAM++: 

We generated gradient -based class activation maps 

(Grad -CAM) for the predicted DR class (Selvaraju 

et al. , 2017 ). Grad -CAM produces a class -specific 

saliency map by backpropagating the prediction signal 

to a selected late -stage feature representation, high -

lighting image regions that contribute the most to the 

decision. The resulting map was resized to the input 

resolution and overlaid on the corresponding fundus 

photograph to provide an intuitive visualization of 

model attention. We computed Grad -CAM overlays for 

both correctly classified samples and representative 

failure cases across severity levels to qualitatively 

assess whether the model focused on plausi ble retinal 

evidence. 

VLM -Based Rationale Generation: To gener -

ate clinician -oriented textual rationales, we employed 

a vision –language model (VLM) conditioned on the 

fundus image and classifier outputs. We evaluated two 

VLM variants: a lightweight general -purpose model 

(llava -hf/llava -1.5 -1b -hf) and a biomedical -adapted 

model (llava -med -v1.5 -mistral -7b) ( Li et al. , 2023 ). 

For each image, the VLM input consisted of (i) the 

original RGB fundus photograph and (ii) a structured 

textual context containing the predicted DR grade 

and the corresponding class -probability vector. A fixed 

prompt template was used to standardize the output 

format (2 –4 sentences) and limit stylistic variance 

across samples. 

Prompt Design and Constraints: To reduce 

hallucination risk and encourage image -grounded re -

porting, we incorporate explicit constraints in the 

system instructions. The VLM is instructed to (i) 

describe only clearly visible findings, (ii) explicitly 

state “not clearly visible” when unce rtain, (iii) avoid 

inventing lesions, (iv) mention relevant image -quality 

limitations (e.g., blur, glare, low contrast), and (v) 

avoid treatment recommendations. If image -grounded 

findings cannot be stated, the model is instructed to 

output a criterion -based generic note using conditional 

language (e.g., “may/typically/often”) and to label the 

output as GENERIC . This design enforces a

conservative explanation style and makes uncertainty 

explicit.

Evaluation Metrics 

Grading Performance Metrics: We evaluated DR 

severity grading using a set of complementary met -rics 

that capture both overall correctness and class -

sensitive behavior under imbalance. Overall accuracy 

is reported as a coarse summary of correct predic -

tions. Precision and recall are re ported to character -

ize false -positive and false -negative tendencies, respec -

tively, whereas macro -F1 treats all classes equally and 

emphasizes performance on minority grades. Weighted 

F1 additionally accounts for class prevalence to reflect 

the expected p erformance under the observed label 

distribution. Because DR grades are ordinal, we also 

report quadratic weighted kappa (QWK), which mea -

sures the agreement between the predicted and refer -

ence grades while penalizing larger grade discrepancies 

more strongly than adjacent -grade errors (Karthik 

et al. , 2019 ). 

Textual Explanation Quality Metrics: We assessed 

textual rationales using three complementary criteria 

designed to measure semantic fidelity, image grounding, 

and clinical completeness. 

(i)  Semantic Similarity (BERTScore). We 

compute BERTScore to quantify the semantic overlap 

between the generated explanation and a reference 

text using contextual token embeddings (Zhang et al. ,

2020 ). Because APTOS does not provide human -

written rationales, we adopt grade -specific reference 

templates that describe canonical findings for each 

severity level (0 –4) and use them as reference targets for 

scoring. The templates are as follows: Class 0: “Normal 

retina with clear macula and intact vasculature. No 

visible microaneurysms, hemorrhages, or exudates.” 

Class 1: “Mild NPDR: few microaneurysms with 

minimal vascular changes. No significant hemorrhages 

or exudates.” Class 2: “Moderate NPDR: multiple mi -

croaneurysms and dot -blot hemorrhages with scattered 

hard exudates, mainly around the posterior pole.” Class 

3: “Severe NPDR: numerous hemorrhages with venous 

bleading and cotton -wool spots; ischemic changes may 

be present near the posterior pole.” Class 4: “PDR: 

neovascularization with possible preretinal/vitreous 

hemorrhage and widespread ischemic changes.” When 

the VLM explicitly labels its output as generic , we score 

it against the template of the predicted grade. 

(ii)  Image –Text Grounding (CLIPScore): To 

quantify how well a generated rationale is aligned with 

the associated fundus image without requiring reference 

text, we compute the CLIPScore using the CLIP ViT -

B/32 model ( Hessel et al. , 2021 ). Before scoring, the 

generated text is sanitized to remove prompt/control 

tokens (e.g., “USER:”, “ASSISTANT:”, and image 

placeholders), markdown artifacts, and bracketed prob -

ability strings, and it is truncated to a fixed length 

to satisfy CLIP token limits. The processed caption and 

the corresponding image are embedded using the CLIP 

text and image encoders, respectively. Both 

embeddings are ‡2-normalized, and the final CLIP -

Score is computed as their cosine similarity. A higher 

CLIPScore indicates stronger image –text compatibility, 

providing a reference -free complement to reference -

based semantic scoring. 

(iii)  Clinical Completeness (Coverage): We 

define a lightweight coverage metric to measure whether 

explanations include clinically relevant terms expected 

in DR reporting. Let f be a predefined vocabulary of 

ten retinal descriptors: { microaneurysm, hemorrhage, 

exudate, macula, vessel, retina, optic disc, cotton wool, 

lesion, neovascularization }. For a generated explana -

tion, we compute the fraction of terms in f that appear 

in the lowercased text via exact substring matching. 

Coverage is the average across the evaluation set. This 

metric acts as a proxy for completeness by indicating 

whether clinically important concepts are mentioned in 

the generated explanation. Although it does not assess 

correctness or the appropriateness of localization, it 

complements BERTScore and CLIPScore by explicitly 

rewarding the inclusion of clinically meaningful descrip -

tors.

## 5. Discussion 

The results show a consistent pattern across back -

bone comparison, ensembling, and multimodal expla -

nation analysis. Modern CNN backbones, particularly 

ResNet -50 and ConvNeXt -Tiny, provided the strongest 

overall balance between class -balanced performance 

and ordinal agreement, while transformer -based models 

remained competitive but somewhat less consistent on 

minority grades. Ensembling further improved robust -

ness, with weighted soft voting producing the most 

stable gains across folds, whereas the hybrid class -

level fusion strategy was competitive but did not 

demonstrate a statistically significant advantage in 

paired testing. At the explanation level, Grad -CAM++ 

provided plausible but coarse evi dence localization, and 

VLM -generated rationales were generally consistent 

with the predicted grade under constrained prompting. 

Quantitatively, the evaluated VLM variants revealed a

trade -off between clinical term coverage and semantic 

similarity to reference templates, while showing similar 

CLIPScore values and leaving classification perfor -

mance unchanged. 

The backbone comparison suggests that convolu -

tional inductive bias remains highly effective for DR 

severity grading, where the most informative cues often 

appear as small, localized lesions distributed across the 

retina. ResNet -50 and ConvNeXt -Tiny likely benefit 

from strong local feature extraction together with 

stable optimization under transfer learning and aug -

mentation. ConvNeXt, in particular, combines modern 

training practices with preserved convolutional locality, 

which may help maintain performanc e when lesion 

evidence is sparse or partly obscured by acquisition 

artifacts. 

Transformer -based models provide a complemen -

tary perspective. Swin and DeiT are well suited to 

capturing global context and long -range dependencies, 

which can be important when disease severity is re -

flected in broader vascular changes, diffuse hemor -

rhages, or more global structural patterns. However, 

the gap between their ordinal agreement and macro -

averaged performance is consistent with the challenge 

of distinguishing minority grades. Even when the over -

all severity ordering is learned reasonably well, separat -

ing adjacent stages remains difficult when high -grade 

findings are subtle, rare, or inconsistently represented 

in a single image. This is also consistent with the 

broader pattern observed across models, where most 

errors occurred between neighboring grades rather than 

as extreme grade jumps. From a clinical standpoint, 

this is expected, as the boundaries between mild, 

moderate, and severe DR depend on lesion counts and 

spatial distribution, which are difficult to infer reliably 

from heterogeneous images without explicit lesion 

counting or multi -view information. 

Ensembling is often motivated by the idea that 

different models make different mistakes, especially 

near class boundaries. In this study, global probability 

fusion through weighted soft voting produced the most 

consistent improvement in ordinal agreement across 

folds. This is a plausible outcome for an imbalanced 

ordinal task, as probability averaging can reduce vari -

ance, smooth unstable predictions near grade bound -

aries, and limit overconfident errors from any single 

backbone. The similar performance of stacking suggests 

that a learned meta -combiner does not necessarily offer 

additional benefits beyond well -calibrated probability 

fusion, particularly when the base learners are already 

strong and the amount of fold -specific validation data 

is limited. 

The hybrid class -level ensemble was designed to 

exploit grade -wise complementarity by allowing the 

preferred model to vary across classes. The fold -wise 

results suggest that this approach can match or, in 

some splits, exceed standard fusion; however, it can also 

underperform when class -wise competence estimates 

are unstable. This is especially relevant for minority 

grades, where small changes in fold composition can 

substantially affect the reliability of class -specific statis -

tics. The paired Wilcoxon test s with the Holm correc -

tion did not show statistically significant differences be -

tween hybrid fusion and the baseline methods, and the 

confidence intervals for the mean differences included 

zero. Under the current cross -validation setting, this 

indicates that hybrid class -level fusion does not provide 

a consistent advantage over global probability fusion.

A quantitative evaluation of explanations is es -

sential if multimodal interpretability is to move be -

yond illustrative examples; however, such evaluations 

must be interpreted carefully in medical applications. 

In this study, we used complementary metrics that 

capture different aspects of explanation quality: se -

mantic similarity to grade -specific templates through 

BERTScore, image –text alignment through CLIP -

Score, and keyword -based clinical term coverage. The 

results demonstrate that the two VLM variants behave 

differently in predictable ways. The biomedical -adapted 

model achieved higher coverage, suggesting that it 

more consistently included clinically relevant descrip -

tors from the predefined vocabulary. The lightweight 

model achieved a higher BERTScore against the grade 

templates, indicating closer alignment with canonical 

reference phrasing. The CLIPScore was similar for both 

models, implying that image –text compatibility was 

broadly comparable , and that the main differences were 

linguistic rather than visual. 

These findings highlight two important caveats. 

First, template -based semantic similarity is sensitive 

to phrasing and does not necessarily reflect clinical 

correctness. A model may achieve a higher BERTScore 

simply by producing language that more closely resem -

bles the template, without necessarily providing richer 

or more image -specific reasoning. Second, coverage 

serves only as a proxy for completeness and not as 

a guarantee of faithfulness. The inclusion of clinical 

terms does not ensure that the referenced findings are 

truly present or correctly localized. For this reason, the 

three metrics should be interpreted together: semantic 

similarity provides a reference -based signal, CLIPScore 

offers a reference -free grounding signal, and coverage 

reflects whether the explanation includes clinically 

meaningful concepts. 

The fact that the classification metrics remained 

identical across the two VLM settings is also important. 

This confirms that the grading model itself was un -

changed and that the evaluation isolates differences at 

the explanation stage. This supports the methodologi -

cal claim that explanation generation can be treated as 

a separate and measurable output layer, one that can be 

improved independently of classification performance. 

Clinical and Practical Implications 

From a clinical and screening perspective, these 

findings suggest that the proposed framework is most 

useful as a decision -support tool rather than as a re -

placement for expert judgment. In a practical workflow, 

the models predicted DR grade could support case 

prioritization by helping graders or ophthalmologists 

identify images that may require faster review, referral, 

or closer follow -up. For example, when the system pre -

dicts severe or proliferative DR with high confidence, 

this may help flag a case for urgent specialist attention. 

Conversely, in images predicted as no DR or mild DR, 

the output may support routine review, while still 

allowing the clinician to verify that subtle lesions have 

not been overlooked. In this sense, the main value of 

the system is not only in producing a grade, but also in 

providing additional visual and textual cues that can 

help experts assess whether the prediction appears 

clinically plausible. 

The explainability component is especially relevant 

in this regard. Grad -CAM(++) visualizations can help 

indicate whether the model is focusing on lesion -rich 

retinal regions rather than on irrelevant structures or 

image artefacts. For instance, if a moderate DR predic -

tion is accompanied by activation over areas containing 

hemorrhages or exudate -like regions, the explanation 

may increase the clinicians confidence that the model 

is attending to meaningful evidence. Similarly, VLM -

generated rationales can tr anslate the model output 

into short clinician -readable descriptions, which may be 

useful during image review or documentation -oriented 

workflows. For example, a rationale stating that the 

prediction is associated with multiple hemorrhagic or 

exudative findings may be easier to inspect and 

interpret than a heatmap alone, particularly for non -

technical graders who are less familiar with saliency 

maps. In this way, the combination of grade prediction, 

visual localization, and textual rationale may help 

bridge the gap between algorithmic output and expert 

review.

At the same time, our results also clarify the limits 

of such assistance. Because Grad -CAM(++) remains 

coarse, it should not be interpreted as lesion -level 

confirmation, and because VLM -generated text is eval -

uated only indirectly through similarity, coverage, and 

image –text alignment metrics, it cannot be assumed 

to be fully faithful in every case. This is particularly 

important in borderline images, in minority severity 

grades, or in cases where disease staging depends on 

subtle lesion counts and spatial distr ibution. In such 

settings, a clinician should treat the explanation as 

a prompt for closer inspection rather than as direct 

evidence. For example, if the model predicts severe DR 

but the highlighted regions are diffuse or the generated 

rationale is generic , this would signal the need for 

careful manual reassessment rather than immediate 

trust in the automated output. 

More broadly, the results suggest that the frame -

work could be useful in settings where screening volume 

is high and interpretability matters. In a teleophthal -

mology or population -screening context, the model 

could assist first -pass triage, while the accompanying 

explanations help human reviewers decide whether the 

case appears straightforward or whether the prediction 

should be questioned. For expert users, the system may 

serve as an additional second reader that highlights 

potentially relevant regions and summarizes possible 

retinal findings. For less specialized graders, the textual 

explanation may improve transparency by making the 

model decision easier to interpret. However, the system 

is not yet suited to autonomous clinical reporting. Its 

most realistic role, based on the present results, is to 

support expert -centered review by combining compet -

itive grading performance with explanation outputs 

that can be inspected, questioned, and validated by 

clinicians.

To place the reported performance in context, 

we compare our results with representative recent 

studies on DR severity grading and explainability, as

comparison should be interpreted cautiously, as prior 

work often relies on different train/test splits, DR -

specific external pretraining, additional preprocessing 

steps, or different evaluation sets, whereas our results 

are reported as the mean , SD under stratified five -fold 

cross -validation. With this caveat, our best -performing 

ensemble (weighted soft voting) achieved strong ordinal

emphasize transfer learning and ensemble strategies on 

APTOS -like benchmarks. For example, Dharrao et al.

beyond standard ImageNet initialization. 

From an interpretability standpoint, much of the 

DR grading literature relies mainly on post hoc heatmaps 

such as CAM and Grad -CAM as qualitative evi -

dence. Hybrid CNN –Transformer approaches often 

include attribution maps to improve transparency but 

do not systematically quantify explanation qual -ity 

(Xu et al. , 2024 ). In contrast, our study treats 

explanation generation as a measurable output by 

combining Grad -CAM(++) localization with VLM -

generated rationales and by reporting complementary 

quantitative measures that capture semantic fidelity to 

grade templates, image –text alignment, and clinical 

term coverage. This complements emerging retinal 

vision –language directions that aim to couple predictive 

models with language -based accountability; however, 

these approaches are typically evaluated on different 

datasets or tasks ( La Rosa et al. , 2026 ; This et al. ,

2025 ). 

Taken together, the comparison with prior work 

reinforces the practical picture emerging from our 

results. Strong CNN baselines and weighted soft -voting 

ensembles provide a reliable foundation for DR grading 

under the present setting, whereas the explainabil -

ity pipeline adds value primarily as an interpretable 

support layer rather than as an autonomous clinical 

reporting mechanism.

Future Directions 

Several directions may help strengthen the clinical 

reliability and broader applicability of the proposed 

pipeline. First, the proposed pipeline should be vali -

dated on additional DR datasets and real -world screen -

ing data, especially under conditions of device shift and 

variable image quality. Second, class -wise ensembling 

could be made more stable through repeated cross -

validation, regularized weight estimation, competence 

measures based on calibrated probabilities, or ordinal -

aware fusion objectives that explicitly penalize large 

grading errors. Third, explanation grounding could be 

strengthened by incorporating lesion segmentation or 

detection modules or by linking attribution maps to 

explicit lesion concept extraction to reduce ambiguity. 

From an evaluation perspective, ophthalmologist -in -

the -loop assessment remains important for judging 

both grading reliability and explanation faithfulness 

and could be complemented by structured annotation 

of key findings rather than relying solely on template -

based references. Finally, practical deployment would 

require uncertainty monitoring and reporting; future 

work should therefore integrate calibrated uncertainty 

estimates and guardrails to reduce overconfident ex -

planations when the underlying evidence is limited or 

visually unclear. 

## 6. Conclusion 

This study presents a unified pipeline for five -

class diabetic retinopathy grading that combines strong 

image -based classifiers with multimodal explainability. 

Under a controlled evaluation protocol on the APTOS 

2019 dataset, modern CNN backbones, particularly 

ResNet -50 and ConvNeXt –Tiny, emerged as the most 

reliable single -model baselines, whereas transformer 

backbones remained competitive but were somewhat 

less consistent under class imbalance. Ensembling fur -

ther improved ordinal agreement, with weighted soft 

voting producing the most stable gains across folds. 

Although hybrid class -level fusion was also competitive, 

it did not show a statistically consistent advantage 

within the present cross -validation setting. Beyond 

grading performance, this study also treated explana -

tion generation as a measurable output rather than 

a purely illustrative add -on. Grad -CAM and Grad -

CAM++ provided visually plausible, though coarse, 

evidence localization, whereas constrained prompting 

enabled VLMs to generate textual rationales that were 

generally consistent with the predicted grade. A 

quantitative comparison of the two VLM variants 

revealed a practical trade -off between clinical com -

pleteness, reflected in term coverage, and template -

level semantic similarity, reflected in BERTScore, while 

image -text alignment remained similar across models. 

Overall, these findings support the use of multimodal 

explainability as a complementary layer for improving 

transparency in DR grading. At the same time, they 

highlight the need for external validation and expert 

assessment before clinical faithfulness and deployment 

readiness can be established.
