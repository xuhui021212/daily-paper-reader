1 Introduction 

Humans effortlessly perceive motion; we recognize when a person walks leftward, when a ball accelerates, or when a vehicle follows a smooth arc. This capacity for motion understanding is so fundamental that it often goes unnoticed, yet it underpins our ability to predict, explain, and reason about the dynamic visual world. By contrast, today’s vision-language models (VLMs) still lack an explicit mechanism to reason about how objects move between observations. As a re-sult, models frequently rely on static priors or textual interpolation to “fill in” dynamics, producing motion statements that are plausible but ungrounded and difficult to verify. As illustrated in the bottom example of Figure 1, many video reasoning models produce free-form rationales that are not required to cite explicit spatial or temporal evidence (e.g., where an object is, and when). This makes the rea-soning trace weakly grounded: correct answers may rely on static priors rather than verifiable observations. Recent advances in video understanding [5, 13, 20] has substantially improved when and where models can ground evidence in video. A growing line of work trains models to emit structured evidence chains: sequences of timestamped bounding boxes that localize objects across frames. As illustrated in the top example in Figure 2, spatio-temporal evidence chains provide grounded snapshots —i.e ., an <obj> with <box> coordinates at times-tamps [13]—but they leave the inter-frame dynamics implicit. In other words, they specify where an entity is at a few moments, while the model must implic-Motion-o 3

itly interpolate how it moved between observations. The missing link is motion: a structured relation across time involving direction, velocity, and scale change. Without an explicit motion representation, models must infer trajectories implic-itly, which makes trajectory understanding hard to interpret, easy to shortcut with priors, and difficult to evaluate directly [12, 17, 24]. This gap matters more than it might appear. Full video comprehension re-quires what we term Spatial-Temporal-Trajectory (STT) Reasoning: the joint ca-pacity to reason about where objects are (spatial), when they appear (temporal), and how they move between observations (trajectory). Existing evidence-based frameworks largely address the first two dimensions but leave the third entirely implicit. Yet trajectory reasoning is not merely interpolation between boxes; it requires reasoning about direction, velocity, and acceleration. Knowing that a person appeared at position A at time t1 and position B at time t2 is necessary but insufficient. What is often needed is an explicit articulation such as: moved leftward at moderate speed while approaching the camera, or remained station-ary, or followed a smooth arc. These descriptors provide the semantic glue that connects observations into a coherent trajectory and enables downstream capa-bilities such as trajectory prediction, anomaly detection, and motion-sensitive question answering (e.g., “Which direction did the person walk?”). We introduce Motion-o , a motion-centric extension to grounded video rea-soning that makes trajectory understand-ing explicit and rewardable, without re-quiring architectural changes. Motion-o builds on spatio-temporal evidence chains and adds a lightweight but crucial op-erator: a structured Motion Chain of Thought (MCoT) step that forces the model to summarize motion between grounded observations. Concretely, after the model grounds an object at mul-tiple timestamps it must emit a self-closing ⟨motion /⟩ tag that describes the trajectory connecting those obser-vations. Each motion tag contains dis-crete, interpretable attributes includ-ing direction, speed, and scale change. For example, “<motion obj="person" dir="W" speed="slow" scale="stable"/> .” This creates an ex-plicit intermediate step that bridges spa-tial snapshots to continuous dynamics, turning implicit motion into a structured statement. ifiable intermediate into grounded video reasoning, while Figure 2 isolates the key mechanism: the <motion/> tag that bridges successive spatio-temporal obser-vations, converting a snapshot-based evidence chain into a spatial-temporal-trajectory reasoning trace. A key challenge is supervision; existing grounding datasets provide sparse key-frames, while motion is inherently defined over continuous time [11, 13, 21]. To address this, we introduce a trajectory-grounding dataset artifact that ex-pands sparse keyframe supervision with denser bounding box tracks suitable for motion supervision. From these tracks, we compute discrete motion prim-itives (direction, speed, and scale-change) automatically and inject them into reasoning traces during supervised fine-tuning, teaching the model the MCoT schema. We then reinforce motion reasoning with trajectory-grounded rewards during reinforcement learning. Motion-o uses two complementary signals: (i) a trajectory reward that measures agreement between predicted motion tags and ground-truth motion derived from tracks, and (ii) a visual grounding reward based on dual-chain verification that penalizes motion descriptions that remain unchanged when temporal motion cues are removed (e.g., by freezing frames). Together, these rewards suppress two common failure modes in video under-standing: producing motion narratives that do not align with the visual trajec-tory, and defaulting to motion claims that remain unchanged when temporal evidence is removed. Our contributions are as follows: (1) Motion-o , a motion-centric extension for grounded video reasoning that makes object trajectories explicit and ver-ifiable by augmenting evidence chains with structured motion descriptors. (2) We formalize Spatial-Temporal-Trajectory (STT) reasoning , identifying trajectory understanding—how objects move between observations—as a miss-ing capability in current video reasoning frameworks. (3) We introduce Motion Chain of Thought (MCoT) , a structured reasoning step that summarizes direction, speed, and scale change through discrete <motion/> tags, enabling in-terpretable trajectory reasoning without modifying model architectures. (4) We develop a trajectory-grounded training framework that expands sparse keyframe supervision with dense bounding box tracks and trains motion reasoning using trajectory-aware rewards, improving spatial–temporal grounding and trajectory prediction while remaining fully compatible with existing VLM pipelines. 

2 Related Work 

Spatial and Temporal Video Reasoning. Recent advances in LLMs and VLMs equip them with mechanisms to build and manipulate structured inter-mediate representations, allowing them to reason through multi-step transforma-tions rather than relying solely on pattern matching or next-token prediction. A growing body of work seeks to extend visual reasoning into video understanding, with particular focus on identifying when and where relevant evidence appears within a sequence. These efforts approach the problem from complementary Motion-o 5

angles, reflecting the central role of temporal and spatial localization in video reasoning. On the temporal side, Time-R1 [21] introduces verifiable rewards for temporal grounding with strong generalization, and TVG-R1 [2] improves ro-bustness through curated datasets. On the spatial side, SpaceR [15] leverages RL for object-centric grounding and geometric reasoning. Open-o3 Video [13] unifies both dimensions, generating timestamped frames with localized bound-ing boxes as structured evidence. However, all of these methods focus on where objects are located rather than how they move . While motion metrics appear as rewards in some systems [13], the models themselves never articulate mo-tion patterns in their reasoning. Our work addresses precisely this gap, making trajectory reasoning an explicit part of the model’s output. 

Evidence-based Video Reasoning. Rather than directly reason over high-dimension visual patches, a parallel body of work [4,7,19,26] investigates how to utilize explicit visual operations such as detection, segmentation and tracking. DeepEyes [26] shows that end-to-end RL can incentivize image-tool reasoning, and TreeBench [18] provides methodology for traceable, box-level evidence in images. In the video domain, OpenAI-o3 [14] formalizes the notion of “thinking with images,” and Open-o3 Video [13] extends this idea to “thinking with frames,” emitting timestamped crops and bounding boxes, while STVG-o1 [7] introduces a bounding box chain-of-thought for spatio-temporal grounding. These approaches compellingly demonstrate the value of explicit evidence, yet they focus almost exclusively on spatial localization. Motion —the temporal evolution of spatial positions—remains implicit throughout. Our Motion Chain of Thought explicitly bridges successive temporal observations with trajectory reasoning, adding the missing dynamic dimension to evidence-based video understanding. 

The Motion Reasoning Gap. Despite parallel advances in temporal ground-ing (when), spatial grounding (where), and evidence-based reasoning (what), no existing method achieves full Spatial-Temporal-Trajectory Reasoning—explicitly reasoning about how objects move. Some works [13,15] incorporate motion met-rics as implicit training signals, but the models never learn to articulate the motion patterns they rely on. This makes trajectory reasoning opaque—both to users seeking explanations and to the models themselves during inference. 

Reinforcement Learning-based training. Existing works leverage RL to push video understanding beyond direct reasoning to spatio-temporal evidence reasoning. Video-R1 [5] demonstrates that temporal-aware GRPO with curated reasoning data substantially improves video understanding, while VideoChat-R1 [11] extends this to spatio-temporal perception without degrading QA per-formance. More recently, Video-RTS [23] combines RL with test-time scaling, and DeepVideo-R1 [16] introduces difficulty-aware regularization to better ex-ploit temporal structure. These works establish RL as a powerful paradigm for video reasoning but operate primarily over textual outputs. With RL, Motion-o reshapes video reasoning by making motion an explicit and structured element of the inference process, effectively closing the gap left by models that treat motion only as an implicit or secondary cue. 3 Introducing Motion-o 

We present Motion-o, a framework that extends grounded video reasoning with an explicit trajectory reasoning step (Figure 3). Existing evidence-based video reasoning methods [7, 13] generate verifiable spatio-temporal observations (ob-jects, boxes, timestamps), but typically leave the motion that connects the ob-servations implicit. Motion-o closes this gap by augmenting spatio-temporal ev-idence chains with a discrete <motion/> descriptor that summarizes how an object moves between grounded observations. Given a video-question pair (V, q ), the model outputs 

y = ⟨think ⟩R⟨/think ⟩⟨ answer ⟩A⟨/answer ⟩,

where R contains (i) grounded spatio-temporal evidence and (ii) explicit motion descriptors. Motion-o is trained in two stages: (i) supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on motion-augmented reasoning traces that teach the model the structured format Motion-o 7

and syntax, and (ii) reinforcement learning that directly optimizes motion-centric rewards to encourage accurate and visually grounded motion descriptions. Corresponding with Figure 3, we first summarize the spatio-temporal evi-dence format that we extend to Spatial-Temporal-Trajectory evidence (Sec. 3.1), then introduce MCoT and how we obtain supervision for motion tags (Sec. 3.2), and finally describe the training pipeline and trajectory-grounded rewards (Sec. 3.3). 

3.1 Spatial-Temporal-Trajectory (STT) Evidence Chains 

Following Open-o3 Video [13], we adopt a structured format for grounding rea-soning in visual evidence. Given a video V and question q, the model produces a response organized as ⟨think ⟩R⟨/think ⟩⟨ answer ⟩A⟨/answer ⟩, where R contains the reasoning trace and A the final answer. Within R, each grounded claim is written as: 

⟨obj ⟩o⟨/obj ⟩⟨ box ⟩b⟨/box ⟩ at ⟨t⟩t⟨/t⟩s, (1) where o is the object name, b = [ x1, y 1, x 2, y 2] is a normalized bounding box, and 

t is the timestamp in seconds. This representation creates an evidence chain that anchors language to concrete observations across time. For example, a model might write: “The man is visible holding a glass at 47 .5s, and later at 54 .2sis smiling,” each claim anchored by a precise spatial location and timestamp. However, even when the same object is referenced at multiple timestamps, the evidence chain remains a set of discrete snapshots: it specifies where the object was at t1 and t2, but leaves the trajectory dynamics between t1 and t2 implicit. Motion-o targets precisely this missing link. 

3.2 Motion Chain of Thought (MCoT) 

We extend spatio-temporal evidence chains with explicit motion reasoning via a self-closing <motion/> tag to create spatial-temporal-trajectory evidence chains. The core idea is: after the model generates multiple temporal observations of the same object, it must summarize the motion that connects them. This converts a sequence of grounded boxes into a minimal, structured trajectory descriptor that is both interpretable and rewardable. so for

3.3 Training Pipeline 

Our training follows the standard two-stage paradigm [5,13] with minimal mod-ifications, while introducing Motion-o as a format-level extension that explic-itly supervises and rewards trajectory transition. We first teach the model to emit well-formed motion descriptors as part of the reasoning trace through su-pervised fine-tuning (SFT) and then directly optimize motion correctness and visual grounding through reinforcement learning (RL). 

Stage 1: Supervised Fine-Tuning. We fine-tune Qwen2.5-VL-7B [1] on the motion-augmented STGR data described above. Using standard cross-entropy optimization, the model learns to produce <motion/> tags as a structured contin-uation of spatio-temporal evidence chains: after grounding multiple observations of an object with <obj><box><t> , it emits a self-closing <motion obj="..." dir="..." speed="..." scale="..."/> that summarizes the trajectory con-necting those observations. Beyond offline data augmentation, the only addi-tional requirement in this stage is a system prompt instruction that specifies the tag schema and allowed attribute values. This stage establishes (i) strict for-mat validity ( <think>/<answer> and tag well-formedness), (ii) spatio-temporal grounding syntax, and (iii) the motion descriptor schema, ensuring the policy enters RL with stable, parseable trajectory reasoning traces. Stage 2: Reinforcement Learning. We build upon the training recipe in [13], adopting Group Sequence Policy Optimization (GSPO) [25] to stably optimize long-form evidence grounded video reasoning. Given a video–question prompt x, we sample a group of K candidate completions {y(k)}Kk=1 ∼ πθ (· | x),evaluate each completion with a scalar reward r(x, y (k)) and compute a group-normalized advantage. GSPO then updates the policy using sequence-level im-portance ratios, which better aligns optimization with sequence-level rewards and improves stability for long-horizon reasoning. Following [13], we decompose the scalar reward into three components: 

r(x, y ) = racc (x, y ) + rthk (x, y ) + rfmt (x, y ). (3) where, racc encourages answer accuracy task-specific; e.g., exact match for MCQ, ROUGE for free-form QA), rfmt enforces strict structured output formatting in the reasoning chain (including well-formed <motion/> tags), and rthk incen-tivizes evidence-based reasoning. As in [13], we define the thinking reward as the sum of temporal and spatial grounding terms: 

rthk (x, y ) = rt(x, y ) + rs(x, y ) + rmotion (x, y ), (4) where rt provides temporal supervision (with adaptive temporal proximity) and 

rs provides spatial supervision with temporal gating to ensure spatial rewards are only computed when predicted timestamps are sufficiently close to ground-truth. 

Motion-o extension: motion supervision inside rmotion . Our key extension is an explicit motion component 

rmotion (x, y ) = rtraj (x, y ) + rground (x, y ), (5) which teaches the model to (i) describe trajectory dynamics consistently with underlying tracks, and (ii) rely on true temporal evidence rather than textual priors. 

Trajectory Reward. For each object tracked across at least two temporal observations, we compute ground-truth motion bins from the bounding box tra-jectory and score the model’s predicted <motion/> attributes via discrete bin matching with adjacency-aware partial credit: 

rtraj = 0 .4 · rdir + 0 .3 · rspeed + 0 .3 · rscale . (6) Each attribute receives 1.0 for an exact match, 0.5 for an adjacent bin (direc-tion: ±45 ◦; speed/scale: ±1 ordinal rank), and 0.0 otherwise. Ground-truth bins are derived from centroid displacement and box area dynamics: direction from the dominant displacement vector quantized to eight compass points plus sta-tionary, speed from displacement magnitude normalized by the object’s bound-ing box diagonal, and scale from the log-ratio of box areas between the first and last observation. Trajectory scores are computed per object identity to ensure consistency over a single track. 10 B. Galoaa, S. Moezzi et al. 

Visual Grounding Reward (Dual-Chain Verification). To encourage the model to derive motion descriptions from actual visual observation rather than textual interpolation, we introduce a visual grounding reward via dual-chain ver-ification (see Stage 2 inputs in Figure 3). For each sample, we generate a second reasoning chain from a motion-masked version of the video (created by freezing frames to remove temporal motion cues). We then compare the <motion/> tags produced from the original video against those from the motion-masked input: 

rground = 0 .5 1[d̸ = d′] + 0 .3 1[s̸ = s′] + 0 .2 1[c̸ = c′], (7) where d, s, c and d′, s ′, c ′ denote the direction, speed, and scale attributes from the original and masked outputs, respectively. If predicted motion changes when motion evidence is removed, the model exhibits dependence on temporal cues (rground → 1); if predictions remain unchanged, it likely relies on non-visual shortcuts ( rground → 0). Objects absent from the motion-masked output are treated as fully grounded. 

Synergy Between Trajectory and Visual Grounding Rewards. Both the trajectory and visual grounding reward enforce complementary requirements. The trajectory reward enforces correctness of motion descriptors with respect to ground-truth trajectories, while the visual grounding reward enforces evi-dence dependence on temporal visual information rather than textual anticipa-tion. Correct motion reasoning ( e.g ., predicting dir="W" when predicted boxes show leftward displacement) yields high rdir ; accurate speed and scale descriptors align with bounding box predictions that match ground-truth velocity and area change, increasing rspeed and rscale . Together they suppress two common failures: emitting <motion/> tags that are inconsistent with the underlying bounding box track, and emitting plausible <motion/> tags that could be produced without ob-serving motion. This yields a self-reinforcing training signal: as the model learns to ground objects consistently across time, it is rewarded or emitting matching motion descriptors (higher rtraj ); as it learns to rely on temporal evidence rather than priors, it is rewarded under motion masking (higher rground ). 

4 Experimental Results 

4.1 Dataset 

Dataset Augmentation: Trajectory Grounding with Dense Motion Annotations 

Existing spatio-temporal grounding datasets often provide bounding boxes at a small set of timestamps, yielding sparse trajectories that under-specify the motion connecting observations [13]. This sparsity is a bottleneck for motion reasoning: direction, speed, and scale are defined over continuous time, but su-pervision is provided only at isolated keyframes. We densify sparse keyframe supervision on the Perception-LM (PLM) subset [3] by converting each sparse set of timestamped boxes into a temporally denser trajectory. For each tracked object, we preserve all original annotated keyframes Motion-o 11 

and insert intermediate supervision points at a fixed temporal stride between ad-jacent annotated timestamps. Intermediate bounding boxes are generated from interpolating the ground truth dense masks provided in the PLM dataset. This process converts originally sparse annotations into a dense per-object sequence of (t, b t) pairs, substantially increasing temporal coverage for motion supervi-sion. From the expanded trajectories, we compute object-level motion descriptors (see section 3.2 for details) and store them alongside the augmented tracks. Con-cretely, for each object we derive discrete attributes capturing (i) direction (quan-tized compass bins plus stationary), (ii) speed (binned using scale-normalized displacement), and (iii) scale change (binned from the area log-ratio trend), pro-ducing a compact summary of trajectory dynamics over the observed window. We then inject these descriptors directly into the model’s reasoning trace: after the final grounded mention of each object in the <think> chain (e.g., <obj>... </obj><box>... </box> at <t>... </t>s ), we append a structured motion tag <motion obj="..." dir="..." speed="..." scale="..."/> that sum-marizes the object’s trajectory over the evidence window. This coupling ensures the model learns motion as evidence-linked reasoning rather than as an auxiliary label: the chain supervises not only where and when an object appears, but also 

how it evolves between observations. We release this augmentation as a stan-dalone trajectory-grounding resource, including densely expanded trajectories, motion descriptors, and evaluation splits designed to isolate STT reasoning.

5 Conclusion 

We introduced Motion-o, a lightweight framework that makes motion dynamics explicit and verifiable in grounded video reasoning. Motion-o augments spatio-temporal evidence chains into spatial-temporal-trajectory evidence chains with MCoT: a structured <motion/> tag that bridges timestamped observations with object-conditioned motion attributes (direction, speed, scale). To provide su-pervision for these dynamics, we release a trajectory-grounding artifact that ex-pands sparse keyframes into denser box tracks, and we train with complementary trajectory and visual grounding rewards that encourage motion tags to depend on temporal evidence. Across benchmarks, Motion-o improves trajectory-faithful reasoning without architectural changes, requiring only offline preprocessing and a small training-time overhead. More broadly, this work establishes a practical foundation for video understanding where reasoning is anchored in the explicit dynamics that make a video a video, enabling more reliable and interpretable motion-centric video models. Future work will explore extending MCoT to multi-object interactions, non-linear trajectories, and richer motion representations. Motion-o 15

6 Training Details 

6.1 Training Data 

Both Motion-o and Open-o3 + MCoT models reported in this paper are trained using data derived from the Spatio-Temporal Grounded Reasoning (STGR) dataset introduced in the Open-o3 paper [13]. Following that formulation, our training data is organized into SFT and RL stages, with each sample associ-ated with a source dataset and a task type. The two models in this work, share the same RL data, but differ in their SFT mixtures to reflect different training priorities. 

Shared RL data. For both Motion-o and Open-o3 + MCoT, the RL stage uses the same file, containing 34k training samples. This shared RL set includes five task categories: temporal-spatial free-form QA (12,047), General video QA MCQ 

(12,998), visual QA (5,000), temporal QA (2,279), and general video QA free-form 

(2,000). Using the same RL corpus for both models ensures that differences in final behavior are primarily attributable to the SFT data design rather than to different reinforcement learning supervision. 

Motion-o SFT data. For Motion-o, the SFT stage uses a subset of the STGR dataset containing 22k samples. This mixture is broader and more balanced, combining motion-relevant grounded examples with a substantial amount of gen-eral video QA. Its task composition is: general video QA free-form (2,000), Gen-eral video QA MCQ (13,000), temporal QA (491), and temporal-spatial free-form QA (7,047). This design preserves broad video reasoning coverage while still re-taining a strong spatio-temporal grounding component, allowing Motion-o to couple explicit motion reasoning with more general video understanding capac-ity. Figure S5 summarizes the distribution of motion labels in our augmented dataset. Examples are not dominated by a single direction: eastward and west-ward motions are closely matched, and the remaining directional bins are also populated across the compass. This indicates that the learned <motion/> tags are trained on a reasonably balanced set of trajectory orientations, rather than reflecting a strong directional skew. We use this more concentrated SFT mixture for the Open-o3 + MCoT vari-ant because the Open-o3 base model has already been exposed to the broader STGR-style train-ing recipe, including SFT and RL on this same corpus family, in its original training pipeline [13]. Ac-cordingly, for the MCoT variant we bias SFT more strongly toward motion-intensive grounded exam-ples, with the goal of adapting the already grounded base model to-ward more explicit trajectory rea-soning rather than repeating the full broad-coverage mixture used to establish its general grounded reasoning ability. 

Key difference in training emphasis. The main distinction between the two mod-els lies in the SFT stage. Motion-o is trained with a larger and more mixed SFT corpus that retains substantial general video QA supervision alongside grounded spatio-temporal data, whereas Open-o3 + MCoT uses a smaller but much more concentrated motion-oriented SFT set. Motion-o prioritizes a balance between broad video reasoning competence and explicit motion-aware grounding, while Open-o3 + MCoT prioritizes targeted adaptation of an already grounded base model toward trajectory-explicit reasoning. This also explains why Open-o3 + MCoT achieves higher overall results: the Open-o3 base model has already been trained on the STGR corpus [13], giving it a strong prior over spatio-temporal grounding tasks. Our SFT stage therefore does not need to re-establish that general competence, and instead focuses narrowly on introducing <motion/> 

tag reasoning. The subsequent RL stage then builds directly on this founda-tion, using trajectory-consistency and visual grounding rewards to sharpen the model’s ability to produce track-consistent, explicitly grounded motion claims. The performance advantage of Open-o3 + MCoT thus reflects this compound-ing of a well-initialized base with targeted motion supervision, rather than any fundamental architectural difference. 8 Extended Benchmark Results 

Table S5 reports results on three additional benchmarks: TVGBench [2] for temporal video grounding, MVBench [10] for multi-modal video understand-ing, and MotionBench [9] (dev split) for motion-specific question answering. These complement the V-STAR, VideoMME, and WorldSense results in the main paper. 

Quantitative Analysis The extended benchmark results in Table S5 show a clear and consistent pattern: making motion explicit improves performance not only on motion-focused benchmarks, but also on broader video reasoning tasks that implicitly depend on dynamic understanding. Starting from the Qwen2.5-VL-7B baseline, Motion-o improves TVGBench from 16.3 to 37.6 (+21.3) and MotionBench from 35.0 to 60.0 (+25.0). When added on top of the Open-o3 base model, the Open-o3 + MCoT (Motion Chain-of-Thought) variant further raises TVGBench from 20.8 to 39.6 (+18.8), MVBench from 64.4 to 69.2 (+4.8), and MotionBench from 45.0 to 63.0 (+18.0). Notably, the best variant also surpasses 18 B. Galoaa, S. Moezzi et al. 

the strongest open-source MVBench baseline in our table (69.2 vs. 67.9) and exceeds GPT-4o result on MotionBench by a wide margin (63.0 vs. 33.0). These gains suggest that explicit trajectory reasoning is improving a capability that many modern video benchmarks increasingly require. This pattern is important because the three benchmarks probe different surfaces of the same underlying problem. TVGBench emphasizes when evidence occurs and whether the model can temporally align its reasoning to the relevant segment. MotionBench is more directly motion-centric, explicitly testing categories such as action order, camera motion, location-related motion, and motion recognition. MVBench is broader and more heterogeneous, but many of its subsets also depend strongly on dy-namics rather than static appearance alone, including action sequence, action prediction, and object interaction. Benchmarks are increasingly converging to-ward testing a model’s ability to reason about temporal change, transitions, and object evolution, rather than merely recognizing static content. The key point we want to emphasize here is that benchmark construction has already started moving in a motion-centric direction, but most models have not. Many recent methods are designed as generic video reasoners: they may improve temporal grounding, spatial localization, or chain-of-thought style inference, but they still do not explicitly model the trajectory that connects observations. Exist-ing evidence-based video reasoning frameworks have become increasingly strong at grounding where an object is and when it appears, yet they typically leave how it moved between those observations implicit. This forces the model to interpo-late dynamics internally, often relying on priors, textual smoothness, or weakly grounded heuristics rather than an explicit motion representation. Motion-o di-rectly targets this gap by introducing with the structured <motion/> tag that summarizes direction, speed, and scale change between grounded observations, turning trajectory reasoning into an explicit and rewardable intermediate step. The gains do not come from architectural novelty, but from changing what the model is required to represent. Motion-o changes the supervision target and the reward structure so that motion is no longer an implicit byproduct of spatio-temporal grounding, but an explicit reasoning primitive. Prior models may in-clude motion-sensitive metrics or temporal supervision, but the models them-selves do not articulate motion patterns in their reasoning traces. In Motion-o, the trajectory is part of the reasoning trace itself, and the RL stage further en-courages those motion claims to be both track-consistent and visually grounded under motion masking.
