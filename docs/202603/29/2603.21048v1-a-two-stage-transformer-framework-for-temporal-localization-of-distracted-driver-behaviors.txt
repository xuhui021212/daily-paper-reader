# ABSTRACT 

The identification of hazardous driving behaviors from in-cabin video streams is essential for enhanc-ing road safety and supporting the detection of traffic violations and unsafe driver actions. However, current temporal action localization techniques often struggle to balance accuracy with computa-tional efficiency. In this work, we develop and evaluate a temporal action localization framework tailored for driver monitoring scenarios, particularly suitable for periodic inspection settings such as transportation safety checkpoints or fleet management assessment systems. Our approach follows a two-stage pipeline that combines VideoMAE-based feature extraction with an Augmented Self-Mask Attention (AMA) detector, enhanced by a Spatial Pyramid Pooling-Fast (SPPF) module to capture multi-scale temporal features. Experimental results reveal a distinct trade-off between model capacity and efficiency. At the feature extraction stage, the ViT-Giant backbone delivers higher representations with 88.09% Top-1 test accuracy, while the ViT-based variant proves to be a practical alternative, achieving 82.55% accuracy with significantly lower computational fine-tuning costs (101.85 GFLOPs/segment compared to 1584.06 GFLOPs/segment for Giant). In the downstream localization task, the integration of SPPF consistently improves performance across all configurations. Notably, the ViT-Giant + SPPF model achieves a peak mAP of 92.67%, while the lightweight ViT-based configuration maintains robust results. 

Keywords Augmented Self-Mask Attention (AMA) · Driver behavior detection · Naturalistic driving · Spatial Pyramid Pooling–Fast (SPPF) · Temporal action localization · Vision Transformers

# 1 Introduction 

Ensuring driver attentiveness is a fundamental requirement for road safety, as human factors remain a dominant cause of traffic accidents across the globe. Among these factors, driver distraction—defined as any secondary activity that diverts visual, cognitive, or physical attention from the driving task—has been consistently identified as one of the most critical contributors to crash risk [ 1 , 2 ]. With the integration of in-cabin cameras in modern vehicles, the analysis of driver behavior has become increasingly feasible, enabling continuous monitoring of potentially unsafe actions such as mobile-phone manipulation, gaze aversion, eating, drinking, or interacting with in-vehicle devices. However, developing automated systems capable of reliably recognizing such behaviors is far from trivial. In-cabin videos typically suffer from illumination changes, shadows, occlusions generated by steering-wheel motion or hand movements, and significant inter-driver variability in posture and appearance [ 3, 4 ]. Many distraction-related actions are subtle, brief, or partially hidden, making them difficult to capture using traditional vision-based or frame-level classification methods. Classical CNN-based systems, including ensemble architectures [ 5 ], are inherently limited because they operate primarily on spatial cues and lack the temporal modeling capacity required to characterize continuous, fine-grained driver behaviors. Despite recent advances in driver behavior analysis, accurately localizing distraction-related actions in naturalistic in-cabin videos remains challenging. Many existing temporal action localization approaches still rely on sliding-window or anchor-based designs [ 6, 7, 8], which struggle to accommodate the large variability in action duration across long, untrimmed driving sequences. As a result, short and subtle behaviors may not be fully captured, while extended actions can be fragmented into redundant segments. Real-world driver-monitoring data further complicate this problem due to inconsistent video quality, sparse annotations, and frequent occlusions caused by steering-wheel motion or changes in driver appearance. These limitations underscore the need for a more robust and temporally adaptive framework capable of modeling fine-grained distraction behaviors under realistic driving conditions. Recent survey studies [ 9 ] further highlight that traditional CNN- and LSTM-based approaches lack the represen-tational power needed to capture long-range temporal dependencies and multi-scale context present in naturalistic driving videos. In response to these limitations, transformer-based architectures have emerged as powerful tools for temporal action localization, offering stronger modeling capacity for long-range dependencies compared to CNN-based or purely local methods. Early works such as Space-Time Attention [ 10 ] and Transformer-in-Transformer (TNT) [ 11 ] demonstrated that self-attention can effectively capture spatio-temporal relationships in video sequences. More specialized TAL frameworks—such as ActionFormer [ 12 ] and TriDet [ 13 ]—further improved temporal precision through hierarchical attention, boundary modeling, and feature calibration. However, these methods still rely primarily on local-window attention, which limits their ability to integrate global temporal context across distant segments of long in-cabin videos, especially when actions occur sparsely or exhibit highly variable durations. Parallel to architectural advances, masked autoencoding has recently shown exceptional potential for video represen-tation learning. VideoMAE [ 14 ] introduced a simple yet highly effective masked modeling strategy for self-supervised pretraining on large video corpora, enabling models to learn robust spatio-temporal features even under limited supervi-sion. Its successor, VideoMAE V2 [ 15 ], further scaled masked autoencoders with dual-masking strategies, achieving state-of-the-art performance across multiple video understanding benchmarks. These developments highlight the growing importance of transformer-based and self-supervised pretraining methods as foundational components for driver behavior recognition. Their strong temporal modeling capabilities and resilience to noisy video conditions make them particularly well-suited for naturalistic driving scenarios, laying the groundwork for more advanced and adaptive action localization frameworks. Collectively, these challenges indicate that existing approaches still fall short in modeling fine-grained distraction behaviors under real-world driving conditions. Although recent transformer-based and self-supervised advancements provide a promising foundation, further refinement is required to achieve robust temporal boundary precision. Building on these observations, we adopt and extend the Augmented Self-Mask Attention framework [ 16 ] to better capture long-range dependencies and enhance distracted action localization performance. To sum up, our approach aims to bridge the gap between theoretical research and practical deployment.

architectures, including 3D CNN variants and segment-based frameworks, as surveyed in [ 1, 9 ]. These models extract spatio-temporal features from short video clips, but are often designed primarily for clip-level classification. Recently, transformer-based models have gained significant attention in video understanding. Works such as TimeSformer [ 10 ] and Transformer-in-Transformer [ 11 ] extend the Vision Transformer (ViT) architecture to video data, leveraging self-attention to capture long-range dependencies in space and time. In parallel, masked autoencoding paradigms have emerged as powerful self-supervised learning strategies. Masked feature prediction and reconstruction approaches such as MaskedFeat [ 17 ] and Masked Autoencoders as Spatiotemporal Learners (MAE-ST) [ 18 ] demonstrate that reconstructing heavily masked video tokens encourages the model to learn robust and semantically meaningful representations. Among these methods, VideoMAE [ 14 ] proposes a simple yet effective masked video autoencoder with an asymmetric encoder–decoder architecture and a very high masking ratio, achieving strong performance in self-supervised video pre-training. VideoMAE V2 [ 15 ] further improves this framework using a dual-masking strategy and scaled model configurations. Despite their success, these models are largely designed for clip-level action recognition rather than temporal localization. Moreover, scaling up to very large ViT backbones can be computationally expensive, which is undesirable in resource-constrained in-cabin driver monitoring scenarios. In this work, we build on the VideoMAE family [ 14 , 15 ] and adopt a ViT-Base backbone distilled from a larger ViT-Giant model to balance accuracy and efficiency. This choice allows us to leverage strong spatio-temporal representations while maintaining feasible computational complexity for deployment. 

2.2 Temporal Action Localization 

Temporal Action Localization (TAL) focuses on identifying both the category and temporal boundaries of actions in untrimmed videos. Existing methods can be broadly categorized into sliding-window, two-stage, one-stage anchor-based, and transformer-based approaches. 

2.2.1 Sliding-Window and Proposal-Based Methods 

Early TAL approaches rely on fixed or multi-scale sliding windows across the temporal axis. Representative methods include S-CNN [ 6 ], TURN-TAP [ 19 ], and Cascaded Boundary Regression (CBR) [ 7]. These methods slide temporal windows over the input video and perform classification and boundary refinement within each window. Although conceptually straightforward, they suffer from high computational redundancy and struggle to handle variable-duration actions due to fixed window designs. Two-stage approaches, inspired by object detection pipelines, first generate candidate temporal proposals and then classify and refine them. Many of these methods are discussed in existing surveys [ 1, 9]. While these architectures can achieve strong performance, they introduce additional complexity and latency, making them less ideal for real-time driver monitoring. 

2.2.2 One-Stage Anchor-Based and Transformer-Based Methods 

Single-stage anchor-based detectors such as SSAD [ 8] and Gaussian Temporal Awareness Networks (GTAN) [ 20 ]directly predict action categories and temporal boundaries over a set of predefined multi-scale anchors. However, anchor design is often heuristic and may not generalize well to diverse temporal patterns. More recently, transformer-based TAL models have emerged as state-of-the-art. ActionFormer [ 12 ] leverages local self-attention and boundary-sensitive regression, while TriDet [ 13 ] introduces relative boundary modeling to improve localization robustness. Although these approaches perform well on standard benchmarks, they typically require long input sequences and substantial GPU memory, which limits their deployment in constrained environments. To address both efficiency and accuracy, the Augmented Self-Mask Attention transformer (AMA) [ 16 ] is proposed for naturalistic driving action recognition. AMA employs structured self-mask attention patterns to enhance multi-scale temporal modeling, making it well suited for complex in-cabin driver behaviors. Our work builds on this line by integrating AMA with VideoMAE-based features in a unified two-stage pipeline for temporal driver behavior localization.

Foundational work by Hurts et al. [ 2 ] analyzes the mechanisms and measurement of driver distraction from a human factors perspective, motivating the development of automatic monitoring systems. Early deep learning–based methods, such as the CNN ensemble of Eraqi et al. [ 3] and the genetic algorithm–based ensemble proposed by Kumar et al. While effective for detecting coarse categories of distraction, these models do not perform temporal localization and often fail to capture extended or subtle behaviors. Our work aligns with this video-only paradigm and focuses on modeling fine-grained, naturalistic driver behaviors using efficient transformer-based temporal action localization. 

2.4 Limitations of Existing Approaches 2.4.1 Model Design and Representation Limitations 

Sliding-window approaches such as S-CNN [ 6] and TURN-TAP [ 19 ] rely on fixed temporal windows, which can either miss long-duration actions or introduce redundancy for short actions. Anchor-based single-stage detectors like SSAD [8 ] and GTAN [ 20 ] depend on hand-crafted anchor configurations, limiting their adaptability across diverse temporal patterns. In the context of driver behavior recognition, CNN- and LSTM-based methods [ 3, 5] are optimized primarily for classification and lack explicit mechanisms for multi-scale temporal localization. Transformer-based TAL methods such as ActionFormer [ 12 ] and TriDet [ 13 ] address some of these issues but require long input sequences and large batch sizes, which can be prohibitive on resource-constrained hardware. From a representation learning standpoint, many backbone networks surveyed in [ 1 , 9] (e.g., TSN, 3D CNN variants) operate at the frame or short-clip level, making it difficult to capture extended temporal context. Masked video modeling approaches such as VideoMAE [ 14 ], VideoMAE V2 [ 15 ], MaskedFeat [ 17 ], and MAE-ST [ 18 ] improve representation quality, but they are not explicitly tailored for temporal boundary regression. 

2.4.2 Computational and Deployment Limitations 

Large ViT-based backbones used in recent video recognition and TAL models require substantial GPU memory and computational resources, especially when processing long temporal sequences. While such models achieve strong performance, their size and hardware demands make them impractical for many deployment scenarios, including post-trip driver behavior auditing systems where batch processing of long in-cabin video recordings is required. In these contexts, efficiency, throughput, and model footprint often take priority over marginal gains in accuracy. Multimodal systems, exemplified by datasets like the MDMD [ 4], further increase system complexity and deploy-ment costs by relying on additional sensors beyond cameras. These limitations motivate the design of a more compact and computationally efficient framework. In this work, we adopt a distilled VideoMAE-based backbone to significantly reduce model size while preserving strong spatio-temporal representational quality. Combined with an AMA-driven transformer for multi-scale temporal localization, the proposed system is well suited for high-throughput offline processing of naturalistic in-cabin driving videos.

3.1.1 AI City Challenge 2024 – Track 3 Dataset 

The AI City Challenge 2024 Track 3 dataset is designed for in-cabin driver behavior analysis, particularly focusing on distracted driving detection. The dataset contains 594 video clips, totaling approximately 90 hours of footage , captured from 99 drivers. Each driver performs 16 different tasks, including talking on the phone, eating, and reaching back, once in a random order. Videos are recorded simultaneously from three synchronized cameras mounted at different angles inside the vehicle. To introduce variability in appearance, each driver completes the task sequence twice: once without any appearance block and once with an appearance block (e.g., sunglasses, hats). Consequently, each driver contributes six videos, resulting in a total of 594 clips across all drivers [21]. The 90 hours of video are split into three subsets: A1, A2, and B, containing 69, 15, and 15 drivers, respectively. Dataset A1 includes ground-truth labels specifying the start and end times of each distracted behavior, along with its type, and is provided to teams for training and validation. Dataset A2 lacks labels and is intended for algorithm development and evaluation on the public leaderboard. Performance on dataset A2 allows teams to iteratively improve their systems, though rankings do not determine the final winners. Dataset B is reserved for final testing, where top-performing teams from the public leaderboard submit their functional code for training and inference. Organizers evaluate these submissions on dataset B to determine the official track winners, with teams required to make their code publicly available for verification [21]. Evaluation for Track 3 focuses on activity identification performance, quantified by the average activity overlap score. For a ground-truth activity g with start time gs and end time ge, the closest predicted activity p of the same class is matched based on the highest overlap score os, provided that the predicted start ps and end pe times fall within 

[gs − 10 s, g s + 10 s] and [ge − 10 s, g e + 10 s], respectively. The overlap score is computed as the ratio between the temporal intersection and union of g and p. Any unmatched ground-truth or predicted activities receive a score of 0. The final performance metric is the average overlap score across all matched and unmatched activities, providing a robust measure of model accuracy in detecting distracted behaviors [21]. AMA Temporal Action Detection Pipeline 

The Action Moment Adherence (AMA) temporal action detection stage accepts the feature matrix extracted by VideoMAE as input and generates temporally localized action segments, each associated with class labels and confidence scores. The AMA model utilizes a point-based detection framework integrated with multi-scale feature pyramid networks to effectively capture actions across varying temporal scales. The input to the AMA pipeline is a feature matrix of dimensions [Nchunks , D ], where Nchunks denotes the number of 16-frame video chunks (approximated as T / 16 for a video with T frames), and D represents the feature embedding dimension ( 768 for ViT-Base or 1408 for ViT-Giant). To align with the temporal sequence format required by the AMA backbone, this matrix is transposed to [D, N chunks ], where the temporal dimension corresponds to the sequence of feature chunks. The architecture comprises three primary components: a backbone network, a Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) neck, and parallel detection heads. The backbone processes the input sequence through a hierarchical structure with multiple stages, employing 1D masked convolutions and layer normalization to generate multi-level feature maps at varying 11 A T WO -S TAGE TRANSFORMER FRAMEWORK FOR TEMPORAL LOCALIZATION OF DISTRACTED DRIVER

3.2.5 Spatial Pyramid Pooling Spatial Pyramid Pooling Fast (SPPF) 

In the original AMA architecture [ 16 ], the neck is implemented as a simple Identity fusion module, which only applies layer normalization on top of the multi-scale features produced by the backbone. While this design is computationally efficient, the receptive field at each temporal position remains local, limiting the model’s ability to capture actions with highly variable duration. To enrich the temporal context at the neck stage, the Identity was replaced with neck with a Spatial Pyramid Pooling Fast (SPPF) module. 3.2.6 Ensemble Model 

Temporal action localization models typically generate a large number of predictions with varying confidence scores. These predictions often exhibit significant temporal overlap and redundant detections, especially in long-duration naturalistic driving videos where actions may occur closely in time or share similar motion patterns [1, 9]. To ensure fair scoring under tIoU-based evaluation protocols [ 6, 8], each ground-truth action should be matched with at most one prediction of the same class. Therefore, post-processing and refinement are essential for producing temporally accurate and non-redundant predictions. 4.3 Discussion Interpretation and Implications 

This section provides a deeper analysis of the quantitative results presented in Chapter 5, moving beyond statistical reporting to interpret the underlying causes of the observed behaviors and their practical implications for real-world driver monitoring systems. 

Interpretation of Results 

The experimental results presented in the previous chapter offer a comprehensive view of the factors influencing driver action localization. A primary observation is the stark trade-off between model capacity and computational efficiency. The ViT-Giant backbone, with its billion-scale parameter count, demonstrates a superior ability to capture nuanced spatiotemporal representations, resulting in a significantly higher accuracy compared to the ViT-Base model. This suggests that the complexity of driver behaviors—which often involve subtle interactions and occlusions—benefits substantially from the increased representational power of large-scale transformers. Furthermore, the structural comparison of the AMA module reveals that the Spatial Pyramid Pooling Fast (SPPF) neck consistently outperforms the Identity design. This performance gap can be attributed to the inherent temporal variance of driving activities; while some actions like phone usage are prolonged, others such as glancing are fleeting. The SPPF mechanism effectively addresses this by aggregating context at multiple scales, allowing the model to adapt to varying action durations. However, a granular analysis of class-wise performance exposes a critical limitation in the feature extraction process. While gross motor actions involving distinct body movements achieve near-perfect detection, the model struggles with subtle, localized behaviors such as "Yawning," "Talking," or "Singing." These actions rely heavily on facial micro-expressions and mouth movements, which may be overshadowed by global body features in the current video-based architecture, leading to ambiguity between semantically distinct but visually similar classes. 

Practical Implications 

These findings carry several significant implications for the deployment of driver monitoring systems. First, the efficiency trade-off suggests a bifurcated deployment strategy: while the ViT-Base model is the more practical choice for resource-constrained or embedded environments, the ViT-Giant model is better suited for server-side batch analytics or as a teacher network in knowledge distillation pipelines. Second, the demonstrated effectiveness of the SPPF module indicates that future architectural designs should prioritize multi-scale context aggregation mechanisms to ensure robustness against the heterogeneous temporal characteristics of human actions. Finally, the observed difficulty in distinguishing between vocalizing actions (e.g., singing, talking) and physiological behaviors (e.g., yawning) suggests that relying solely on RGB visual data may be insufficient for comprehensive safety assessment. Achieving high-precision recognition for these subtle categories will likely require multimodal approaches, potentially incorporating audio cues or fine-grained facial landmark features to resolve visual ambiguities. 22 A T WO -S TAGE TRANSFORMER FRAMEWORK FOR TEMPORAL LOCALIZATION OF DISTRACTED DRIVER

4.4 Limitations and Future Works Limitations 

Despite the promising performance of the proposed framework, several limitations must be acknowledged. First, the model was optimized and evaluated on a specific benchmark dataset with fixed environmental conditions, including camera angles and lighting. This reliance creates a potential vulnerability to domain shift, where the model’s performance may degrade when deployed in vehicles with different cabin geometries or unseen illumination scenarios. Second, computational constraints imposed by the available hardware restricted the training batch size for the backbone network, which may have introduced noise into the gradient estimation and limited the ultimate convergence of the model. Additionally, the massive computational cost of the ViT-Giant backbone currently renders it impractical for direct deployment on standard automotive embedded systems (real-time). Lastly, the current vision-only approach reaches a performance ceiling when differentiating between fine-grained actions that lack distinct body articulation, such as the subtle difference between singing along to music and talking to a passenger, which are critical distinctions for assessing driver distraction levels. 

Future Works 

To address these limitations, future research will focus on enhancing both the robustness and the efficiency of the system. To mitigate the risks associated with dataset dependency and domain shift, we plan to validate the model across heterogeneous datasets and explore Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA) techniques to ensure consistent performance in diverse real-world driving environments. Addressing the computational overhead will involve investigating model compression techniques, specifically leveraging knowledge distillation to transfer the rich representations from the ViT-Giant teacher to a lightweight student network suitable for edge deployment. Finally, to resolve the ambiguity among subtle facial actions, we aim to extend the framework into a multimodal system. By incorporating audio signal processing, the model could leverage acoustic features to distinctively classify vocal activities, thereby significantly improving the detection precision for yawning, singing, and talking. 

# 5 Conclusion 

5.1 Summary of Contributions 

This study addressed the critical challenge of temporal action localization in driver monitoring systems, a domain characterized by complex, untrimmed video sequences and diverse behavioral patterns. To tackle this, we proposed a robust two-stage framework that leverages the representational power of Masked Autoencoders (VideoMAE) for feature extraction, coupled with a specialized Action Localization (AMA) module. By decoupling the representation learning from the temporal boundary prediction, we were able to systematically evaluate how model capacity and architectural components influence detection performance. The core contribution of this work lies in the extensive benchmarking of self-supervised backbones—specifically ViT-Base and ViT-Giant—and the validation of multi-scale pooling mechanisms within the localization head. This approach effectively circumvents the limitations of traditional supervised pre-training, enabling the model to learn richer, more discriminative spatiotemporal features directly from the driving context. 

5.2 Key Findings 

The experimental results strongly validate the efficacy of the proposed framework. A definitive conclusion drawn from this study is that model capacity plays a decisive role in understanding complex driving behaviors. The ViT-Giant backbone achieved slightly better performance compared to the ViT-Base counterpart, proving that large-scale parameters are essential for capturing the subtle nuances of spatiotemporal dynamics in high-fidelity video data. Furthermore, the integration of the Spatial Pyramid Pooling Fast (SPPF) neck proved to be a critical architectural choice. By aggregating contextual information at varying scales, the SPPF design successfully mitigated the challenges posed by the heterogeneous duration of driver actions, allowing the system to robustly detect both fleeting glances and prolonged activities. However, this performance comes with a clear trade-off: while large-scale models offer high precision, their computational demands currently limit their applicability in resource-constrained edge environments. 5.3 Closing Remarks 

In conclusion, this study establishes a strong baseline for automated driver behavior analysis using transformer-based architectures. A pivotal achievement of this work is the successful bridging of the gap between theoretical academic research and practical application. Rather than limiting the scope to quantitative metrics, we have systematically aligned the model’s development with real-world deployment constraints, analyzing in detail the trade-offs between computational cost and accuracy. By demonstrating how high-capacity models can be architecturally optimized and evaluated under practical operating conditions, this work takes a concrete step toward operationalizing advanced computer vision systems for transportation safety. The resulting framework not only supports the detection of dangerous driving behaviors but also provides a foundation for identifying traffic violations in controlled inspection or monitoring environments. Furthermore, the accompanying web-based demonstration showcases the system’s applicability, making the methodology more accessible for integration into driver-monitoring workflows. Ultimately, this work contributes to the broader goal of enhancing road safety by delivering a reliable and extensible approach for detecting unsafe or non-compliant driving behaviors, paving the way for next-generation intelligent in-cabin monitoring systems.
