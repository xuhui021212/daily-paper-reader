## LVSum: A Benchmark for Timestamp-Aware Long Video Summarization 

Alkesh Patel ⋆, Melis Ozyildirim ⋆, Ying-Chang Cheng, and Ganesh Nagarajan

Abstract. Long video summarization presents significant challenges for current multimodal large language models (MLLMs), particularly in maintaining temporal fidelity over extended durations and producing summaries that are both semantically and temporally grounded. In this work, we present LVSum, a human-annotated benchmark designed specif-ically for evaluating long video summarization with fine-grained temporal alignment. LVSum comprises diverse long-form videos across 13 domains, each paired with human-generated summaries containing precise temporal references. We conduct a comprehensive evaluation of both proprietary and open-source MLLMs on LVSum, assessing performance using newly introduced LLM-based metrics for content relevance and modality co-herence, alongside standard evaluation metrics. Our experiments reveal systematic gaps in temporal understanding among existing MLLMs and offer insights that establish a new foundation for advancing temporal reasoning in long video summarization.

1 Introduction 

Video contents are increasingly created and consumed by millions of users, creating a need to effectively make them accessible at the right time and in the right form to reduce cognitive load and enable fast decision making. Long-form videos – such as lectures, documentaries, news reports, vlogs, and podcasts – have become ubiquitous across platforms. However, efficiently summarizing their content remains a challenge for both humans and machines. Long video summarization aims to condense extended video content into concise, coherent representations, often including temporally grounded summaries that highlight what happened and when. While recent advances in multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have shown promise in video understanding, temporal precision and scalability over long video sequences remain key limitations. Despite the rapid growth of multimodal large language models (MLLMs), existing methods fall short in several major ways:

1. Lack of temporal grounding. Most current MLLMs can summarize what happened in a video, but struggle to predict when important moments occurred. They lack explicit temporal supervision, such as timestamped key moments or event ranges. 2. Limited context window for long videos. Long videos (ranging from tens of minutes to several hours) pose significant scaling challenges due to context length limitations. As a result, MLLMs often truncate inputs or rely on sparse frame sampling, leading to loss of temporal continuity and context. 3. Weak instruction following for summarization tasks. Many MLLMs are trained on generic instruction-tuned corpora but are not fine-tuned for temporal video summarization. The lack of domain- or task-specific instruc-tion tuning often results in less targeted outputs. In particular, constraints such as summary length budgets and category-dependent guidance play a critical role in producing high-quality summaries. 4. Lack of holistic metrics. Existing automatic metrics (e.g., F1, Kendall’s τ ,Spearman’s ρ) inadequately capture semantic relevance, temporal coherence, and category-dependent preferences in video summaries. Human evaluation further introduces subjectivity and scalability challenges, highlighting the need for more comprehensive and reliable evaluation protocols. To address some of these challenges, we introduce LVSum, a benchmark focused on long-duration video summarization, featuring 72 videos with an average duration of 16 minutes and human-annotated summaries with fine-grained temporal alignment. LVSum spans over 13 categories ( e.g., Lectures/Tutorials, Documentaries, News Reports, TV Shows, Podcasts, Vlogs, Webinars, Comedy, Explainer Videos, Games, Memes, Reactions, Debates/Panel Discussions ), and is designed to rigorously evaluate a model’s ability to produce coherent, timestamp-anchored summaries from lengthy video inputs. Finally, we conduct a systematic evaluation of proprietary and open-source MLLMs — including models like Opus-4.5 [1], Gemini-2.5-Pro [6], and open-source variants like Qwen3-VL-235B-A22B-Instruct [4]– on LVSum. We assess them across multiple dimensions: semantic relevance, temporal accuracy, modality alignment, and scalability to long input sequences. Our results uncover critical gaps in current models’ abilities to track narrative flow over time. This paper makes three primary contributions: 

– LVSum 1, a new benchmark explicitly designed for long-duration video summarization, featuring 72 videos with up to 10 human-annotated summaries per video and fine-grained temporal alignment across diverse domains. 

– Two new evaluation metrics — Modality Coherence (MC) and Content Relevance (CR) — designed to quantify cross-modal grounding and semantic faithfulness in long-video summarization, addressing limitations of existing rank-based correlation metrics.

2 Related Work 

Video summarization tasks 

There are several task variants for video summarization. Video-to-video (V2V) summarization [3,7,8,22] condenses a long video into a shorter version preserving its most informative and diverse content. This is different from Video-to-text (V2T) summarization [5, 15, 16], which generates a compact textual summary for a video. Query-driven video (Q2V) summarization [9, 11, 17, 18, 21, 23] produces user-customized summaries. Video-to-video-text (V2VT) summarization [10, 14] provides both video and text summaries. In this paper, we focus on the V2VT task, where the input is a video with its transcript and the output consists of timestamped video intervals with corresponding descriptions. Unless otherwise specified, we use “video summarization” to refer to V2VT. 

Video Summarization Datasets 

SumMe [8] and TVSum [22] have remained classic datasets for over a decade, but they are dominated by shorter web/user videos compared to modern long-form videos across many categories. Moreover, their volume remains small (25-50 videos). More recent datasets such as VideoXum [14] provides large scale dataset repurposing the ActivityNetCap [12]. Instruct-V2Xum [10] is another large video summarization dataset created using large language model-based summaries; however, it lacks high-quality human annotations. Additionally, its summaries are represented as selected keyframes rather than contiguous temporal intervals. All prior datasets contain short videos ( ≤ 10 mins). In contrast, we focus on long 4 A. videos with high-quality human-annotated key-shots paired with short descrip-tions to better reflect real-world, summary-worthy content. 

Video Summarization with LLMs 

Video summarization has transitioned from extractive methods, which select keyframes based on visual saliency or diversity, to abstractive and semantic-aware approaches [2]. Recent progress leverages MLLMs for understanding and describ-ing video content. LLMVS [13] adopts an LLM-based frame selection strategy to generate human-aligned summaries, while V2Xum-LLM [10] introduces temporal prompt instruction tuning for cross-modal summarization across video-to-video, video-to-text, and mixed settings. While promising, these methods are primar-ily evaluated on short videos, leaving the capability of MLLMs for long-video summarization largely untested. To address this gap, we introduce LVSum, a long-video summarization dataset and benchmark designed to evaluate state-of-the-art MLLMs under realistic constraints. LVSum includes long videos with rich human annotations, summary budgets, and evaluation protocols, enabling systematic benchmarking of modern MLLMs for long-video summarization. 

Evaluation protocols and metrics 

Reliable evaluation of video summarization depends on annotation richness and appropriate metrics. Early benchmarks such as SumMe and TVSum provided 15–20 human annotations per video, enabling researchers to model perceptual variance, but their limited scale (25–50 videos) is inadequate for evaluating modern multimodal LLMs. Beyond annotation density, evaluation metrics have also evolved. [20] showed that the widely used F1 score is strongly biased by segment-length distributions and argued for rank-based measures such as Kendall’s τ and Spearman’s ρ to better capture importance. For cross-modal settings, VideoXum [14] introduced VT-CLIPScore to measure semantic consis-tency between video and text, and V2Xum-LLM [10] extended this direction with CLIP-based cross-modal metrics. However, these recent metrics primarily evaluate semantic relevance and modal similarity, and overlook a new challenge in-troduced by MLLM-based summarization: modality coherence between predicted timestamps and their generated descriptions. In practice, we observe that MLLMs often produce plausible textual descriptions that do not correspond to the visual content of the predicted interval, creating cross-modal hallucinations that current metrics fail to capture. To address this gap, we incorporate MLLM-as-judge metrics that assess content relevance and introduce a modality coherence metric to penalize timestamp–description mismatch in V2VT summarization. 

3 Dataset for Long Video Summarization: LVSum 3.1 Dataset Construction 

We begin by crawling approximately 4,000 videos from the web, each with a duration of at least 10 minutes. To ensure content diversity, we use an MLLM (Gemini-2.5-Pro) to assign open-ended semantic category labels to each video. These labels are then clustered, also using Gemini-2.5-Pro, into 13 higher-level categories to form a coarse taxonomy of video content. select 100 videos using weighted sampling proportional to the observed category distribution. This strategy preserves the natural long-tailed distribution of real-world video content while avoiding over-representation of dominant categories that would result from uniform sampling. The selected videos are then sent to human annotators for summarization. Annotators are required to be fluent in English, as the videos are in English, and all annotations are conducted by annotators based in the United States, Europe, and India to ensure linguistic proficiency and cultural diversity. Annotators were instructed to filter out videos containing personally identi-fiable or sensitive content before proceeding. For valid videos, summarization was performed using the following protocol (See Appendix A for more details): (1) watch the entire video to understand its content and narrative structure; (2) rewatch and identify crucial segments; (3) mark each segment with start/end timestamps, assign a concise textual description, and rate its relevance on a 1–3 scale; (4) iteratively refine segments while keeping the total interval length within 15% of the video duration, a budget commonly adopted in prior video summarization benchmarks to balance coverage and conciseness; and (5) submit the final annotations. Each video was annotated by 10 independent annotators. We manually reviewed submissions and discarded annotations that violated the instructions or the length budget. Of the initial 100 videos, 11 were filtered due to personally identifiable or sensitive content, and 17 were removed due to updated content usage restrictions from source websites, leaving 72 videos that constitute the LVSum dataset .Figure 1 shows the category distribution across the 13 higher-level categories.

multiple human references, and (3) explicit interval-level importance scores at scale. VideoXum [14] contains more videos but inherits structural limitations from ActivityNet Captions, including redundant dense captions and the absence of explicit importance scores, making it an incomplete proxy for real-world summarization. Instruct-V2Xum [10] provides large-scale frame-level annotations but only a single reference per video, and lacks contiguous key-shot intervals that can be stitched into coherent summaries. EDUVSUM [7] similarly offers only one expert annotation and is domain-restricted to lecture videos, limiting both robustness and generalization. LVSum fills a critical gap by providing long-form videos, multi-reference annotations, and interval-level importance supervision, enabling meaningful benchmarking of modern MLLMs for subjective video summarization. 

4 Experiments and Results 4.1 Models Used 

To benchmark LVSum against state-of-the-art multimodal LLMs, we evaluate three representative systems: Sonnet-4.5, Gemini-2.5-Pro, and Qwen3-VL-235B-A22B-Instruct (Qwen3-VL-235B) –reflecting both proprietary and open-source systems. Since nearly all videos include spoken narration or dialogue, we generate timestamped transcripts using Gemini-2.5-Pro. Each model receives 96 uniformly sampled frames along with the transcript as input. The prompt template used for summary prediction is shown in Appendix B. 

4.2 Evaluation Metrics 

In practice, current MLLMs are prone to producing summary segments whose accumulated duration exceeds the target 15% budget, even when explicitly instructed with a length constraint. Moreover, [20] showed that Kendall’s τ and Spearman’s ρ are more robust than F1-based metrics when segment lengths vary. For these reasons, we use Kendall’s τ and Spearman’s ρ as our primary metrics and report the average predicted summary length to contextualize metric values. Because LVSum contains long videos, we compute importance rankings at 

second-level granularity rather than frame-level granularity used in short-video benchmarks such as SumMe and TVSum. We uniformly subsample videos at 1 fps and assign segment-level saliency scores at the corresponding timestamps. This resolution aligns with the temporal precision of MLLMs, which typically generate timestamps at the level of seconds rather than frames, and provides a more faithful comparison for long-video summarization. We also introduce MLLM-as-Judge metrics for content relevance and modality coherence: 

Content Relevance (CR) : This metric measures how well the generated summary captures the important information from the video relative to a ground-truth text summary corresponding to annotated key moments. The main intuition is to verify: Did the model select or describe the right events? Does it omit LVSum: A Benchmark for Timestamp-Aware Long Video Summarization 7

irrelevant or trivial segments? Are the semantic concepts, objects, interactions, and outcomes aligned with the reference annotations? Each summary is rated on a scale from 1 (poor relevance) to 5 (high relevance), with higher scores indicating stronger semantic alignment with the ground truth. 

Modality Coherence (MC) : This metric evaluates whether the predicted summary is cross-modally consistent within the predicted summary intervals and does not hallucinate content unsupported by the corresponding video segments. Given a predicted summary consisting of timestamped segments with descriptions, we extract the visual and auditory content from each predicted interval and compare them with the associated textual description. MC therefore asks whether the visual, auditory, and textual cues for a segment agree: Does the segment show what the description claims? Is speech or sound described that does not exist in video? Are actions or objects hallucinated? Each segment is scored on a scale from 1 (severe cross-modal inconsistency or hallucination) to 5 (strong cross-modal consistency), and scores are averaged across segments to obtain the final MC score. We use Gemini-2.5-Pro [19] as judge. The prompts used for MLLM-as-Judge metrics are provided in Appendix C. 

4.3 Baselines 

We provide following simple baselines: 

1. Random interval baseline: We randomly sample a set of temporal seg-ments for each video, where segment lengths are drawn from {3, 4, 5} seconds. This range is chosen to reflect the typical perceptual duration of a visually or semantically meaningful moment in long-form videos. Each segment is assigned a random importance score from {1,2,3}. The total duration of sampled segments is constrained to ≈ 15% of the video length to match the summary budget. 

2. Evenly spaced interval baseline: We uniformly space temporal segments of {3, 4, 5}-seconds lengths across each video and assign importance scores uniformly at random from {1, 2, 3}. The total duration is again constrained to ≈ 15% of the video length. These baselines establish lower bounds for temporal selection and highlight the contribution of visual and multimodal cues in MLLM-based summarization.

5.3 Ablation Analysis 

Input Modality Ablations

In the transcript-only setup, each model receives only the time-stamped transcript; in the video-only setup, each receives 96 uniformly sampled frames (prompts in Appendix B). Across all three models, the transcript-only (T) modality outperforms video-only (V). 5.4 Qualitative Analysis 

Comparison of outputs from various models 

A qualitative comparison (Figure 3) illustrates how different models summarize the same long video and highlights gaps in current MLLM-based summarization. The human summary is concise and event-centric, selecting key narrative beats (e.g., the performance, the historic studio context, the drum used by artists, and the producer’s closing remarks), while omitting peripheral details. Opus-4.5 captures most salient events and preserves narrative ordering, but produces a longer and more descriptive summary than necessary, reflecting a tendency toward semantic extraction rather than compression. Gemini-2.5-Pro produces video summaries by selecting a compact set of segments that adhere to the main storyline with minimal redundancy, consistent with its superior budget compliance observed quantitatively. In contrast, Qwen3-VL-235B includes nearly all events in the video, yielding a verbose summary that resembles a paraphrased transcript rather than a compressed key-shot summary. While Qwen3-VL-235B shows strong semantic understanding, it fails to distinguish primary from secondary content and frequently extends beyond the intended summary boundary, corroborating the instruction drift and verbosity effects observed in modality and constraint analyses. Overall, the qualitative results reinforce that long-video summarization requires not only semantic comprehension but also temporal prioritization and compression—capabilities that current MLLMs inconsistently exhibit. To further illustrate the generalization of these patterns across different video types, we did a side-by-side comparison of model summaries for two more videos with distinct content: a news report about monsoon flooding in Mumbai and a school trip video to Normandy (See Appendix F). Across both videos, we observe consistent behavior: Opus-4.5 provides detailed but lengthy summaries, Gemini-2.5-Pro produces concise summaries that align well with human selections, and Qwen3-VL-235B generates verbose outputs that include most video events regardless of their relative importance. This cross-video consistency reinforces that the summarization patterns observed in Figure 3 reflect systematic model behavior rather than video-specific artifacts.

6 Conclusion 

In this work we introduced LVSum, a benchmark for timestamp-aware long-video summarization with multi-reference human annotations and interval-level importance supervision. Through comprehensive evaluation of both proprietary and open-source MLLMs, we showed that current systems exhibit strong semantic understanding yet lack robust temporal compression, multimodal grounding, and constraint adherence. Our analyses further exposed category-dependent behavior and failure modes that are obscured under traditional rank-based metrics, motivating the need for richer evaluation dimensions such as Content Relevance and Modality Coherence . We hope LVSum provides a foundation for studying temporally-grounded summarization and stimulates research toward MLLMs that balance semantic salience, timestamp precision, and multimodal consistency for real-world long-video understanding. A Annotation Guidelines for LVSum Dataset 

Goal : To create a short, informative summary of each video by selecting, describing, and ranking the most relevant segments. 

General Guidance :

– Relevance : Focus on segments that are most relevant to the core topic and purpose of the video. 

– Accuracy : The selected segments and their descriptions must accurately reflect the content of the video. 

– Conciseness : The summary should be as short as possible while still conveying the key information. 

– Non-Redundancy : Avoid including segments that repeat information already covered. 

– Informativeness : Prioritize segments that provide new or important informa-tion. 

Video Segmentation 

This step involves segmenting the video to identify key moments that reflect its main purpose, enabling a concise summary of its core content. Ask yourself: "What are the most crucial moments in this video?" Your segment choices should answer that question. 

DO Select the Segments :

– Informative : Choose segments that convey meaningful information related to video. 

– Major Highlights : Prioritize segments showcasing the most exciting, impactful, or informative moments. 

– Fact-Based Representation : The segment must reflect the factual audio and visual content. Avoid inferences. 

DO NOT Select the Segments :

– Low-Value : Waiting (loading screens), Silence (no commentary), or Transitions. 

– Overlapping : Avoid segments that duplicate information presented elsewhere. 

– Irrelevant : Avoid segments unrelated to the video’s primary topic. 

Segment Length Restriction : Ensure the segment fully covers the target information. 

– Maximum Length : Total duration must NOT exceed 15% of total video length. 

– Example : 5-min video = 45s max; 10-min video = 90s max. 18 A. Patel et al. 

Segment Ranking 

This step involves ranking segments by relevance. Ask yourself: "How essen-tial is each segment to understanding the video’s main message?"
