# Reasoning Resides in Layers: Restoring Temporal Reasoning in Video-Language Models with Layer-Selective Merging 

Zihang Fu ♠, Haonan Wang ♠, Jian Kang ♣, Kenji Kawaguchi ♠, Jiaying Wu ♠∗ ♠National University of Singapore ♣MBZUAI 

zihangfu@u.nus.edu, jiayingw@nus.edu.sg 

Abstract 

Multimodal adaptation equips large language models (LLMs) with percep-tual capabilities, but often weakens the reasoning ability inherited from language-only pretraining. This trade-off is especially pronounced in video-language models (VLMs), where visual alignment can impair temporal reasoning (TR) over sequential events. We propose MERIT, a training-free, task-driven model merging framework for restoring TR in VLMs. MERIT searches over layer-wise self-attention merging recipes between a VLM and its paired text-only backbone using an objective that improves TR while penalizing degradation in temporal perception (TP). Across three repre-sentative VLMs and multiple challenging video benchmarks, MERIT con-sistently improves TR, preserves or improves TP, and generalizes beyond the search set to four distinct benchmarks. It also outperforms uniform full-model merging and random layer selection, showing that effective recovery depends on selecting the right layers. Interventional masking and frame-level attribution further show that the selected layers are dis-proportionately important for reasoning and shift model decisions toward temporally and causally relevant evidence. These results show that tar-geted, perception-aware model merging can effectively restore TR in VLMs without retraining. 

1 Introduction 

Reasoning abilities acquired during language-only pretraining are often expected to transfer naturally to multimodal models. In practice, however, multimodal adaptation can weaken the very capabilities that make LLMs effective reasoners (Li et al., 2024b). A standard recipe for building video-language models (VLMs) is to attach a visual encoder to a pretrained LLM, as in models such as LongVA (Zhang et al., 2024b). While this design yields strong perceptual performance, it can substantially compromise the reasoning ability inherited from the language backbone. As illustrated in Figure 1, this degradation appears in two forms. First, multimodal adaptation can disrupt the model’s intrinsic text-based temporal reasoning, causing the adapted VLM to fail on temporal logic problems that the original LLM solves correctly. Second, even when the VLM correctly identifies salient objects and visual cues in a video, it may still fail to infer the temporal-causal structure of the event sequence. We attribute these failures to a mismatch in adaptation objectives : existing multimodal training pipelines are dominated by spatial perception and object-level alignment, while providing much weaker supervision for temporal abstraction and causal inference (Ratzlaff et al., 2025). As a result, multimodal adaptation can erode internal representations that support higher-level reasoning, leaving the model with strong perceptual competence but limited temporal understanding. This tension is especially pronounced for temporal reasoning (TR), which requires tracking event order, causality, and state changes across time (Chu et al., 2024; Wang & Zhao, 2024). For VLMs, such reasoning is indispensable: understanding a video requires not only perceiving objects and actions, but also relating events over time to infer how they unfold and causally interact (Chen et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2024; Nie et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2025).

Existing approaches to recovering these lost capabilities remain unsatisfactory. Training-based methods, such as Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Li et al., 2024b), can improve reasoning, but they require additional supervision and substantial computation. Training-free model merging is more efficient, but existing approaches (Chen et al., 2025) typically merge the VLM and its paired text backbone uniformly at the full-model level, treating the language backbone as a homogeneous block. This coarse-grained strategy may recover some reasoning ability, but does so unreliably and often at the cost of visual performance. At the same time, recent work suggests that reasoning-related behavior in large models is not uniformly distributed, but can depend disproportionately on specific layers or subnetworks (Sharma et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024; Ju et al., 2024). We therefore argue that the trade-off between reasoning recovery and perceptual preservation in multimodal models is not fundamental. Instead, TR in VLMs may depend disproportionately on a localized subset of layers , such that selectively restoring these layers can recover reasoning more effectively than uniform full-model merging while minimizing interference with temporal perception. Motivated by this, we propose MERIT, a training-free, task-driven model merging frame-work for restoring TR in VLMs through selective layer-level intervention. Instead of uni-formly averaging parameters across the full model, MERIT searches over layer-wise self-attention merging recipes between a VLM and its paired text-only backbone. The search is driven by an explicit objective that rewards TR gains while penalizing degradation in temporal perception (TP), allowing MERIT to recover temporal reasoning under perceptual constraints. As this objective is evaluated on a targeted temporal subset, MERIT identifies effective layer-wise recipes without requiring full-benchmark search or retraining. In this way, MERIT replaces coarse full-model merging with targeted capability recovery. Extensive experiments across three representative VLMs show that MERIT consistently improves TR while preserving, and in many cases improving, TP and overall performance. These gains transfer well beyond the targeted Video-MME (Fu et al., 2025) search subset of 55 TP examples and 177 TR examples to four additional popular video benchmarks, indicating that the discovered recipes recover reasoning behavior that generalizes across datasets. Uniform full-model merging and random layer selection both perform worse, showing that effective recovery depends critically on selecting the right layers. Interventional masking further shows that the layers selected by MERIT are disproportionately important for reasoning, while frame-level attribution reveals a shift away from locally salient cues and toward temporally and causally relevant evidence. These findings suggest that targeted, perception-aware model merging may provide a scalable path for restoring TR and, more broadly, other specialized reasoning capabilities in multimodal systems without costly retraining. 2Preprint. 2 Related Work 

Video Temporal Reasoning. Temporal reasoning has long been a central challenge in video understanding. Early benchmarks such as CLEVRER (Yi et al., 2019) and NExT-QA (Xiao et al., 2021) explicitly evaluate causal and temporal relations, but strong performance on these datasets does not necessarily indicate robust temporal understanding in modern Video-LLMs. Recent diagnostic benchmarks reveal substantial gaps. TempCompass (Liu et al., 2024) shows that current models struggle with fine-grained temporal perception, while analyses on TOMATO (Shangguan et al., 2024) and TVBench (Cores et al., 2024) show that many existing benchmarks remain vulnerable to static biases and shortcut exploitation, allowing models to answer correctly without genuine temporal reasoning. More controlled evaluations, including Vinoground (Zhang et al., 2024a) and LongVideoBench (Wu et al., 2024), further highlight persistent weaknesses in tracking event dependencies and reasoning over long video contexts. Recent work also suggests that these failures are not purely perceptual. Although visual encoders may capture relevant temporal cues, the adapted language backbone often fails to transform those cues into coherent temporal reasoning (Li et al., 2024a). This perspective is central to our work: rather than treating temporal failure as solely a representation problem, we target the reasoning bottleneck in the language backbone itself. 

Enhancing Reasoning in Video-Language Models. Existing approaches to improving reasoning in VLMs can be broadly divided into training-based and training-free methods. Training-based methods typically introduce time-aware architectural components, instruc-tion tuning (Ren et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024), or explicit supervision on intermediate reasoning traces through self-training or distillation (Shi et al., 2025; Qiu et al., 2025; Fei et al., 2024). In a related direction, Li et al. (2024a) show that targeted text-only supervision can improve temporal reasoning, further suggesting that the language backbone is a primary bottleneck. While effective, these approaches require additional data, supervision, and computation. Training-free approaches instead aim to improve reasoning either at inference time, for example through modular prompting and task decomposition (Min et al., 2024; Liao et al., 2025), constrained decoding that enforces temporal consistency (Qi et al., 2025), or more effective use of reasoning demonstrations at test time (Wang et al., 2025a), or at the parameter level through model merging (Chen et al., 2025). Among these, model merging is a particularly promising direction because it modifies model behavior directly at the param-eter level without retraining. Chen et al. (2025) show that merging a reasoning-specialized LLM into a vision-language model can recover logical deduction, and suggest that percep-tion and reasoning may reside in different parts of the network. However, existing merging methods remain coarse-grained: they focus primarily on static image settings and apply uniform parameter averaging across the model. In contrast, we study the video setting, where temporal reasoning is essential, and propose a task-driven, layer-selective merging framework that restores reasoning while explicitly preserving temporal perception. 

3 Task-Driven Layer-Selective Merging for Temporal Reasoning 

Uniformly merging a VLM with its paired text-only backbone treats the language stack as a homogeneous component and often forces a trade-off between perceptual competence and reasoning ability. We instead propose MERIT ( M erging for Enhanced Reasoning I n Temporal Tasks ), a task-driven framework for selective layer-level merging. As illustrated in Figure 2, MERIT searches for a layer-wise merging recipe that restores temporal reasoning while preserving temporal perception, enabling targeted capability recovery within the VLM’s language backbone.

3.2 Evolutionary Search with Interpolated Self-Attention Merging 

The objective in Eq.2 defines a combinatorial search problem over layer-wise merging recipes. This is challenging for two reasons. First, for a VLM with L self-attention layers, the space of possible layer-level structures grows exponentially with L. Second, the fitness of a recipe is defined by downstream evaluation on temporal reasoning and temporal perception, making F (g) non-differentiable and expensive to optimize directly. We therefore adopt the Covariance Matrix Adaptation Evolution Strategy (CMA-ES) (Hansen & Ostermeier, 2001), which is well suited to black-box optimization over structured, non-differentiable objectives. The result is a task-specific merged VLM whose layer-wise configuration is optimized to recover temporal reasoning while preserving temporal perception.

Can MERIT improve tem-poral reasoning in diverse VLMs while preserving temporal perception, and does this recovery require layer-selective merging rather than uniform or random merging? 4.1 Experimental Setup Models. We evaluate MERIT on three representative VLMs: LongVA-7B (Zhang et al., 2024b), InternVL3-8B (Zhu et al., 2025), and Qwen3-VL-4B-Instruct (Bai et al., 2025). For layer-wise merging, each VLM is paired with its corresponding text-only backbone as speci-fied in the original technical reports: LongVA-7B with Qwen2-7B-Instruct (Yang et al., 2024), InternVL3-8B with Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (Qwen et al., 2025), and Qwen3-VL-4B-Instruct with Qwen3-4B-Instruct-2507 (Yang et al., 2025). The language backbones of LongVA-7B and InternVL3-8B contain 28 self-attention layers, while Qwen3-VL-4B contains 36. 

Benchmarks. We search for merging recipes on a targeted subset of Video-MME (Fu et al., 2025), using only samples labeled as Temporal Perception (TP) (55 exam-ples) and Temporal Reasoning (TR) (177 examples). We then evaluate the resulting recipes on the full Video-MME dataset, the validation split of LongVideoBench (Wu et al., 2024), LVBench (Wang et al., 2025b), MMBench-Video (Fang et al., 2024), and Video-Holmes (Cheng et al., 2025). For each benchmark, we report the metric most closely aligned with temporal reasoning, together with overall performance. Specifi-cally, we report Temporal Reasoning for Video-MME and MMBench-Video, Relation for LongVideoBench, Reasoning for LVBench, and overall accuracy for Video-Holmes, whose design is inherently reasoning-centric. 5 Conclusion 

We present MERIT, a training-free, task-driven model merging framework for restoring temporal reasoning in VLMs. By formulating merging as an optimization problem over layer-wise self-attention interventions, MERIT identifies configurations that improve tem-poral reasoning while preserving temporal perception. Across diverse VLMs and multiple challenging benchmarks, MERIT yields consistent gains in reasoning, generalizes beyond the search set, and is supported by interventional and attribution analyses that localize reasoning-critical layers and reveal shifts toward temporally grounded evidence. These findings show that reasoning deficits introduced during multimodal adaptation can be addressed through targeted architectural intervention, pointing toward a scalable path for recovering specialized capabilities in future multimodal systems. 9Preprint. A Limitations and Future Work 

Our results show that targeted, perception-aware model merging can effectively restore tem-poral reasoning in VLMs without retraining. At the same time, they open several promising directions for future work. First, MERIT currently relies on benchmark-defined evaluation signals for temporal perception and temporal reasoning , so the discovered recipes may depend on how these capabilities are operationalized. Extending the framework to settings with weaker, noisier, or less structured supervision would broaden its applicability. Second, 

the current formulation reflects a practical balance between expressivity and tractability. 

In principle, one could jointly optimize a continuous L-dimensional parameterization that unifies gating variables and interpolation weights, yielding a richer search space than our discretized formulation. Developing more efficient optimization methods for such continu-ous merging objectives is a promising next step. Finally, the optimal recipes discovered by MERIT vary across models, suggesting that layer-selective recovery is currently model-specific. Understanding whether there exist more transferable principles for layer selection, or even reusable recipes across related architectures, is important for future exploration.
