ABSTRACT 

Vision-Language Models (VLM) have revolutionized multi-modal learning by jointly processing visual and textual in-formation. Yet, they face significant challenges due to the high computational and memory demands of processing long sequences of vision tokens. Many existing methods rely on local heuristics, such as attention scores or token norms. However, these criteria suffer from positional bias and information dispersion, limiting their ability to pre-serve essential content at high pruning ratios and leading to performance degradation on visually detailed images. To address these issues, we propose SVD-Prune, a training-free, plug-and-play token pruning method based on Singular Value Decomposition. It decomposes the vision token fea-ture matrix and selects the top-K tokens using statistical leverage scores, ensuring only tokens contributing most to the dominant global variance are preserved. Experiments show that SVD-Prune consistently outperforms prior pruning methods under extreme vision token budgets, maintaining strong performance even with 32 and 16 vision tokens. Code:

1. INTRODUCTION 

The rapid advancement of Large Language Models (LLMs) has driven remarkable progress in Vision-Language Models (VLMs), which integrate visual and textual modalities to en-able sophisticated multimodal reasoning. Typical VLMs con-vert images into discrete vision tokens via a vision encoder and process these tokens sequentially alongside text through an LLM decoder. Through techniques such as modal align-ment and instruction tuning, these models effectively adapt the strong perception and reasoning capabilities of LLMs to the visual domain. While this paradigm has yielded im-pressive results on cloud-scale infrastructure, deploying and continuously adapting VLMs on resource-constrained edge devices remains challenging due to the prohibitive memory and computational demands of backpropagation, where acti-vation storage, particularly for vision tokens, often dominates the footprint. To alleviate these limitations, recent efforts have explored lightweight VLM architectures with reduced parameter counts, such as SmolVLM [1] and lightweight im-plementations like nanoVLM [2], which achieves competitive performance while using significantly less memory during in-ference. LLaVAMini [3] employs a pre-fusion stage to jointly encode visual and textual tokens, and a subsequent compres-sion module that reduces the set of vision tokens to a single token before input to the language model. However, these small-footprint models typically require extensive training or fine-tuning on large-scale multi modal datasets, limiting their accessibility and adaptability in resource-limited settings. An alternative, increasingly popular direction focuses on pruning vision tokens to mitigate redundancy in visual rep-resentations. Empirical studies reveal that vision tokens exhibit substantial redundancy, with the LLM decoder often attending more heavily to text tokens than to visual ones. Consequently, numerous methods prioritize selecting salient vision tokens while discarding less informative ones. Despite these advances in accelerating VLM inference, prevailing ap-proaches primarily rely on local heuristics, such as attention scores, norms, or cross-modal similarities, which are prone to positional biases, attention-sink effects, and suboptimal cap-ture of global visual structure. These limitations often lead to inconsistent performance degradation, particularly under ag-gressive pruning ratios or in complex scenes where preserving diverse semantic and spatial information is essential. To overcome these challenges, we propose an SVD-based vision token pruning technique adapted from low-rank ten-sor decomposition. Our method decomposes the vision token feature matrix into principal components to capture variance globally, computes leverage scores to quantify each token’s contribution to the dominant patterns, and selects the minimal subset that retains a fraction of the variance while preserving the original spatial order for positional consistency.

dundancy and semantic sparsity in visual data, leading to increased memory usage, computational cost, and inference latency. As a response, recent approaches have investigated token pruning mechanisms that leverage attention-derived importance scores to reduce visual redundancy. Depending on the pruning stage, these methods operate either within the vision encoder [4, 5, 6, 7, 8, 9], or during multimodal decoding [10, 11, 12, 13]. Adaptive, multi-stage pruning strategies across the vision–language pipeline has been also explored [14]. Decoder-side methods primarily differ in how pruning decisions are controlled across layers. FastV [10] adopts fixed token budgets, FitPrune [11] relies on offline, recipe-driven pruning schedules, while SparseVLM [12] introduces adaptive, text-aware pruning with token recycling. Pyra-midDrop [13] further enforces a depth-aware strategy by progressively increasing the pruning ratio across decoder lay-ers. Encoder-side pruning strategies differ in whether token reduction is embedded directly into vision backbone, as in ToMe [4] or applied as a post-processing step on the encoder outputs [5, 6, 7, 8, 9], thereby preserving the original visual computation. HiRED [8] and FasterVLM [5] apply attention-guided, one-shot token discarding based on [CLS] attention under controlled token budgets. VisionZip [6] augments token selection with similarity-based merging to retain con-textual information, while TRIM [7] introduces text-aware token selection via CLIP similarity and complements pruning with an aggregated token. In practice, most token pruning methods are evaluated un-der moderate post-pruning token budgets, typically retaining 64-128 vision tokens, where they achieve a favorable trade-off between efficiency and accuracy. Performance in extreme low-token regime (e.g., 32 or 16 tokens) remains underex-plored in existing VLM pruning literature.

3.2. SVD-Prune 

Motivated by these observations, we propose SVD-Prune, a training-free and attention-independent vision token pruning method that selects informative tokens based on their global representational structure. As illustrated in Fig. 3, SVD-Prune operates outside the vision encoder on fully contextualized vi-sual representations, preserving the backbone and enabling a plug-and-play design. Our approach proceeds in four stages. First, it performs a Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to capture global visual patterns. Next, it truncates the decom-position to retain only dominant variance components. Then, it estimates each token’s contribution using leverage scores. Finally, it selects and prunes tokens based on their cumulative importance. 

3.2.1. Global Pattern Extraction via SVD Decomposition 

Given an input image processed by the vision encoder, we obtain a feature matrix F ∈ RT ×D, where T is the number of vision tokens and D is the hidden dimension. To capture the global structure across all tokens, we perform a SVD on the entire feature matrix : 

F = UΣV ⊤, (1) where the matrix U ∈ RT ×r contains the left singular vec-tors, whose columns represent the principal directions in to-ken space, i.e., the most important linear combinations of to-kens that capture the dominant patterns across the entire fea-ture set. Σ ∈ Rr×r is the diagonal matrix of descending singular values s1 ≥ s2 ≥ · · · ≥ sr > 0, which quantify the amount of variance (or ”strength”) explained by each princi-pal direction, sorted from most to least significant. And the matrix V⊤ ∈ Rr×D contains the right singular vectors (trans-posed), whose rows define the principal directions in feature space, i.e. the key linear combinations of the original dimen-sions that form each important pattern. Together, these com-ponents allow us to identify and prioritize the global variance structure of the vision tokens. Unlike local scoring heuristics, SVD provides a global characterization of visual representations by jointly analyz-ing all tokens, enabling the identification of shared informa-tive structures across the image (e.g., edges, textures, objects). This holistic perspective mitigates positional bias and more effectively captures redundancy that may be overlooked by token-wise or locally defined importance measures.

EXPERIMENTS AND ANALYSIS 4.1. Experimental settings and evaluation criteria 

In this study, we adopt LLaVA-1.5-7B [15] as the baseline model. We evaluate our method on widely used multimodal benchmarks, including GQA [16] and TextVQA [17] which cover a compositional visual reasoning and text-centric visual understanding, respectively. Following standard LLaVA-1.5 settings, input images are resized to a resolution of 336 ×336 ,resulting in 576 vision tokens before pruning. All experi-ments are conducted using PyTorch on a single NVIDIA A40 GPU (48GB VRAM) with an Intel Core i7-14700 CPU. 

4.2. Comparison experiments 

We compare our method against representative encoder-side (ToMe, HIRED, VisionZip, TRIM) and decoder-side (FastV, PyramidDrop, SparseVLM) pruning approaches under vary-ing post-pruning token budgets. As summarized in Table 1, the comparison spans token retention levels from 192 down to 16 vision tokens, starting from the vanilla setting. Our method exhibits strong robustness to token pruning rate on GQA. It incurs only a 2.02-point drop at 192 tokens and remains competitive at 128 tokens (-3.20 points), outper-forming all prior methods at these budgets. Even under ex-treme compression, it degrades gracefully, achieving 53.77 at 64 tokens and maintaining stable performance at 32 and 16 tokens (-8.38 and -8.86 points, respectively). On TextVQA, where accurate text localization and con-textual reasoning are essential, our method demonstrates high stability under aggressive pruning. It incurs minimal degra-dation at 192 and 128 tokens (-0.96 and -2.06 points) and achieves the best performance at 64 tokens (-3.06 points). Even at 32 and 16 tokens, performance degrades gracefully (-3.39 and -4.17 points), preserving text-relevant visual infor-mation under severe compression. Overall, these results demonstrate that our training-free, encoder-side pruning strategy achieves state-of-the-art per-formance retention in the extreme low-token regime, making it particularly well suited for efficient vision–language infer-ence.

5. CONCLUSION 

In this work, we revisited the role of vision tokens in VLMs and showed that, despite their numerical dominance, vision tokens contribute unevenly and often marginally to multi-modal reasoning. Our analysis also revealed that attention-based importance metrics are strongly affected by positional bias induced by causal masking, limiting their reliability for principled token pruning. Motivated by these insights, we in-troduced SVD-Prune that leverages global low-rank structure to identify informative vision tokens. Without retraining or architectural modification, our method consistently outper-forms existing encoder- and decoder-side pruning methods, particularly in the extreme low-token regime, where prior methods suffer substantial performance degradation. Com-pared to the 576-token baseline, our method incurs a moderate performance drop of 8.36 points on GQA and 3.39 points on TextVQA at 32 tokens, and 8.86 points on GQA and 4.17 points on TextVQA at 16 tokens. Our results suggest that reliable multimodal reasoning does not require dense vision token representations, and that extreme vision token pruning represents a promising direc-tion for deploying efficient VLMs under tight computational constraints.
