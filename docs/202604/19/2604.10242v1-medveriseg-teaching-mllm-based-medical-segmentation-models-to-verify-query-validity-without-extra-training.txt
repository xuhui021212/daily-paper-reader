A B S T R A C T 

Despite recent advances in MLLM-based medical image segmentation, existing LISA-like methods cannot reliably reject false queries and often produce hallucinated segmentation masks for absent targets. This limitation reduces practical reliability in both medical education and clinical use. In this work, we propose MedVeriSeg , a training-free verification framework that equips LISA-like medical segmentation models with the ability to identify and reject false queries which contain non-existent targets. Our key observation is that the similarity map between the [SEG] token feature and MLLM image features exhibits markedly different distribution patterns for true and false queries. Based on this, we introduce a Similarity Response Quality Scoring Module that characterizes the similarity map from three aspects: strength , compactness , and purity , producing an initial target-existence prediction. We further incorporate qualitative visual evidence by using GPT-4o to jointly assess the similarity heatmap and the results of Similarity Response Quality Scoring Module for final verification. Experiments on a small-scale benchmark constructed from SA-Med2D-20M show that MedVeriSeg effectively rejects false-query segmentation requests while maintaining reliable recognition of true queries. 

## 1. Introduction 

Medical image segmentation aims to accurately delin-eate regions of interest across diverse imaging modalities Wang, Lei, Cui, Zhang, Meng and Nandi (2022); Ramesh, Kumar, Swapna, Datta and Rajest (2021). Among vari-ous approaches, text-prompt-based methods employ textual prompts as instructions to guide models in segmenting target regions within medical images Zhao, Gu, Yang, Usuyama, Lee, Kiblawi, Naumann, Gao, Crabtree, Abel et al. (2025); Cheng, Fu, Ye, Wang, Li, Wang, Li, Yao, Cheng, Li et al. (2025). This paradigm substantially enhances the interactiv-ity of medical image segmentation by enabling more flexible and intuitive human–model interaction. More recently, several MLLM-based medical segmenta-tion models Tong, Lu, Liu, Zheng and Lu (2025a); Huang, Shen, Liu, Shang, Li, Huang and Yang (2025a); Huang, Peng, Zhao, Yang, Yang and Shen (2025b); Yan, Diao, Yang, Jing, Xu, Zhang, Yang, Liu, Liang and Ma (2025); Tong, Lu, Liu, Zuo and Lu (2025b) have further improved the model interactivity in medical image segmentation. In addition to supporting simple queries, they can also respond to implicit instructions, significantly enhancing efficiency in both clinical and medical education scenarios. These methods typically adopt a LISA-like structure. Specifically, an MLLM ( e.g. , LLaVA-Med Li, Wong, Zhang, Usuyama, Liu, Yang, Naumann, Poon and Gao (2023)) is employed to reason over the medical image and the query, and then

Although the resulting segmentation performance is highly competitive under such a well-crafted design, they all suffer from a common limitation: when confronted with false instructions ( i.e. , cases in which the target object specified in the instruction is actually absent from the input medical image), the models are unable to refuse the request and instead still produce a hallucinated segmentation result. This limitation can have serious consequences in medical image segmentation because in medical education, hallucinated results may mislead students’ understanding of different anatomical or pathological entities in medical images; and in clinical practice, such results may directly lead to in-correct diagnoses. Therefore, addressing this issue not only enhances the practical utility of these methods, but also effectively reduces unnecessary downstream segmentation computations, thereby saving computational resources. Our initial attempt is to adopt a retraining-based strategy, i.e. ,introducing an appropriate number of negative samples into the training data set so that the model could learn to make such intelligent judgments. However, this strategy suffers from two major drawbacks: (1) the cost of data recon-struction and model retraining is prohibitively high, and 

(2) the model may overfit to the newly constructed training data, resulting in limited generalizability. Therefore, we raise the following question: can these LISA-like structures for medical image segmentation be endowed with the ability to reject false queries in a training-free manner, thereby avoiding hallucinated segmentation outputs? Existing studies have demonstrated that, for LISA-like methods, the information encoded in the [SEG] token di-rectly influences the segmentation results Tong et al. (2025a); Qian, Yin and Dou (2025). This motivates us to further investigate whether the feature distribution of the [SEG] token exhibits distinguishable patterns under true and false queries. We draw on the visualization strategies adopted in MediSee Tong et al. (2025a) and READ Qian et al. (2025) to analyze the feature distribution of the [SEG] token. Specifically, we compute the similarity between the [SEG] token feature and the image features derived from the last hidden layer of the MLLM output, and then visualize the resulting similarity matrix as a heatmap. As shown in Figure 1, we observe that, when presented with a valid query, these models tend to focus their attention on the true target region and its surrounding areas, with the heatmap exhibiting clear concentration and well-defined intensity patterns. In contrast, when the queried object does not actually exist in the medical image, the heatmap typically appears irregular, diffuse, and unfocused. Motivated by these findings, in this paper, we propose a novel training-free verification framework for medical segmentation, termed MedVeriSeg , which is specifically designed for existing LISA-like medical image segmenta-tion methods. Specifically, we first introduce a Similarity Response Quality Scoring Module , which quantitatively analyzes the distribution of the aforementioned similarity matrix from three perspectives, i.e. strength , compactness ,and purity , to produce an initial judgment on whether the target object is present in the input medical image. To further incorporate qualitative visual evidence into the final decision, we leverage GPT-4o Achiam, Adler, Agarwal, Ahmad, Akkaya, Aleman, Almeida, Altenschmidt, Altman, Anadkat et al. (2023) to perform a comprehensive evaluation by jointly considering its qualitative interpretation of the similarity heatmap and the output of the Similarity Response Quality Scoring Module, ultimately producing the final re-sult. Equipped with the proposed MedVeriSeg framework, the LISA-like medical image segmentation model not only prevents users from receiving incorrect hallucinated seg-mentation results, thereby providing clearer feedback about the target object and improving their understanding of the medical image, but also reduces unnecessary computational overhead in the downstream decoder. To validate the effec-tiveness of MedVeriSeg, we construct a small-scale test set based on SA-Med2D-20M dataset Ye, Cheng, Chen, Deng, Li, Wang, Su, Huang, Chen, Jiang et al. (2023). Exper-imental results show that MedVeriSeg enables LISA-like methods to effectively reject segmentation requests triggered by false queries in medical images, while preserving reliable recognition of true queries without causing misclassifica-tion. At the same time, our method also provides a new per-spective for addressing the same hallucinated segmentation issue encountered by LISA-like methods in other domains. In summary, our contributions are as follows: 

• We investigate how the distributional characteristics of the similarity matrix between the [SEG] token and

## 2. Related Work 

LISA Lai, Tian, Chen, Li, Yuan, Liu and Jia (2024) is the first MLLM-based model for reasoning segmentation in natural images, and its classical architecture inspires many subsequent studies. Recently, several MLLM-based methods for medical segmentation, such as MediSee Tong et al. (2025a), MedPLIB Huang et al. (2025a), MedReasoner Yan et al. (2025), MedSeg-R Huang et al. (2025b) and others Tong et al. (2025b); Shao and Hou (2025); Oh, Park, Byun, Cho, Lee, Kim and Ye (2024) have also emerged, most of which build upon the LISA framework. Although these methods advance reasoning and interactive segmentation in medical imaging, they generally overlook a key limitation of the LISA framework: when the queried target does not exist, the model still tends to generate the [SEG] token and produce a segmentation result, leading to segmentation hallucination. In this paper, we introduce a training-free method to effectively alleviate this issue.

3.2. MedVeriSeg Framework 

As illustrated in Figure 2, we can already observe from a visual perspective that the heatmap of the similarity matrix exhibits markedly different distribution patterns depending on whether the target specified in the query 𝑥 𝑡𝑥𝑡 is present. However, purely qualitative analysis is often insufficiently rigorous. Therefore, we next seek to formalize this phe-nomenon and quantitatively determine the presence or ab-sence of the queried target based on the distribution of values in the similarity matrix, while further integrating qualitative analysis to arrive at a comprehensive final judgment.

3.2.2. GPT-Based Overall Assessment 

Considering that relying solely on quantitative analysis of the similarity map may result in misjudgments ( e.g. , small lesions), we incorporate GPT-4o into the framework to pro-vide a macro-level visual assessment. Specifically, through a carefully crafted prompt, GPT is guided to combine the existing quantitative analysis results with its own qualitative interpretation of the heatmap, thereby generating the final judgment. It is worth noting that when GPT is not fully confident in its own qualitative assessment, it is required to treat the output of the Similarity Response Quality Scoring Module as an important reference for comprehensive evalua-tion. In contrast, when it has high confidence in its qualitative interpretation, it makes judgments with less reliance on the output of Similarity Response Quality Scoring Module. To further improve its understanding of the heatmap, we em-bedded into the prompt explicit characteristics of heatmap distribution patterns under conditions where the target object is present or absent. Finally, if GPT outputs false, we terminate the segmen-tation process and inform the user that the target object does not exist. This not only reduces the likelihood of producing hallucinated segmentation results, but also effectively avoids unnecessary computational overhead.

4.2. Verifying the Validity of the Query 

The overall performance in verifying whether the query contains a false entity is presented in Table 2. Since there are currently no comparable methods in the field of medical seg-mentation, we first compare our approach with Random Ver-ification and Human Verification. The results show that Ran-dom Verification, due to its inherently random nature, yields an accuracy that is practically unusable. Although Human Verification can achieve reasonably strong performance, it is fundamentally dependent on manual intervention, and its evaluation cost and time will increase substantially as the sample size continues to grow. Moreover, it cannot be guar-anteed that every evaluator possesses the required medical expertise. For a more comprehensive comparison, we further incorporate existing popular MLLMs Chen, Wu, Liu, Pan, Liu, Xie, Yu and Ruan (2025); Li et al. (2023); Bai, Bai, Chu, Cui, Dang, Deng, Fan, Ge, Han, Huang et al. (2023) into our experiments. Specifically, we provide the original image and the query jointly to the MLLM, and ask it to verify the validity of the query before the segmentation model performs segmentation. The results show that, although this strategy achieves a certain level of effectiveness, its overall performance remains suboptimal. In contrast, MedVeriSeg performs strongly across different data sets. On the Posi-tive Set, its performance even approaches Human Verifica-tion, confirming its effectiveness. Although it misclassifies a small number of valid queries, this trade-off is acceptable given its strong results on the Negative Set. We further eval-uate MedVeriSeg without GPT-4o, using only the Similarity Response Quality Scoring Module. The performance drops in this setting, highlighting the effectiveness of combining visual judgment with quantitative analysis.

Notably, although GPT-4o has a relatively low token cost, it is not open-source. ## 5. Conclusion 

In this work, we proposed MedVeriSeg, a training-free verification framework for LISA-like MLLM-based medical image segmentation methods. By exploiting the distinct dis-tribution patterns of the similarity map under true and false queries, our method combines quantitative and qualitative assessment to verify target existence before segmentation. Experiments demonstrate that MedVeriSeg can effectively reject false-query segmentation requests.
