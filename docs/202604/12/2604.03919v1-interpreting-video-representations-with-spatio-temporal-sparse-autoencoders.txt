## Abstract 

We present the first systematic study of Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) on video representations. Standard SAEs decompose video into interpretable, monosemantic features but destroy temporal coher-ence: hard TopK selection produces unstable feature assignments across frames, reducing autocorrelation by 36%. We propose spatio-temporal contrastive objectives and Matryoshka hierarchical group-ing that recover and even exceed raw temporal coherence. The contrastive loss weight controls a tunable trade-off between recon-struction and temporal coherence. A systematic ablation on two backbones and two datasets shows that different configurations excel at different goals: reconstruction fidelity, temporal coher-ence, action discrimination, or interpretability. Contrastive SAE fea-tures improve action classification by +3.9% over raw features and text-video retrieval by up to 2.8 × R@1. A cross-backbone analysis reveals that standard monosemanticity metrics contain a backbone-alignment artifact: both DINOv2 and VideoMAE produce equally monosemantic features under neutral (CLIP) similarity. Causal ab-lation confirms that contrastive training concentrates predictive signal into a small number of identifiable features.

## 1 Introduction 

Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) have transformed the interpretabil-ity of language models [ 2 , 21 ] and, more recently, static image en-coders [ 15 , 19 ]. Yet video vision models remain underexplored. From self-supervised encoders like DINOv2 [ 14 ] and VideoMAE [ 22 ] to multimodal retrieval and captioning systems, we lack tools to un-derstand what these models learn internally. Whether SAEs can meaningfully decompose video representations, where features are spatio-temporal rather than spatial, is an open question. We observe that the challenge is structural. A video clip produces a feature tensor spanning 𝑇 frames and 𝑃 spatial patches, where the same semantic concept (a hand, a moving object) should ideally acti-vate consistent features across time. Standard SAEs, encoding each patch independently, are oblivious to this structure. This raises two questions: Can SAEs reveal interpretable structure in video features? 

And does the temporal dimension require new methodology? 

We find that standard SAEs produce monosemantic video fea-tures but fail to preserve temporal coherence, making it impossible to track concepts across frames. Our central finding is a design principle: the contrastive strength controls the trade-off between reconstruction fidelity and temporal coherence. Both the loss co-efficient 𝜆 and the temperature 𝜏 modulate this strength through different mechanisms ( 𝜆 scales the loss magnitude, 𝜏 controls the sharpness of positive/negative separation), but both move variants along the same Pareto frontier. This is not a limitation to work around. It is a controllable axis that practitioners can set based on their application. Our contributions: (1) Systematic empirical study of SAEs on video represen-tations. We train TopK SAEs on two backbones (DINOv2 and VideoMAE) and two datasets (SSv2 and K400), demon-strating that SAEs decompose video features into monose-mantic, interpretable concepts such as scene elements, ob-jects, and action-correlated features. Video-native models (VideoMAE) score substantially higher on DINOv2-based monosemanticity metrics than image models (MS = 0.972 

vs. 0.359 ), but a CLIP-based control reveals both produce equally monosemantic features (MS ≈ 0.68), identifying a metric alignment artifact in the standard evaluation protocol. (2) Diagnosis of temporal incoherence as a core failure mode. We show that standard SAEs destroy temporal struc-ture: lag-1 autocorrelation drops 36%, with 95% of features below the 0.3 coherence threshold. We trace this to the hard top-𝑘 selection mechanism and show via causal ablation that the resulting features, while temporally unstable, are nonetheless predictively important for action discrimination (5 × stronger ablation signal than random features under probe-based ablation). (3) A controllable reconstruction–coherence trade-off. Ex-tending T-SAE [ 1 ] from text to video, we evaluate three contrastive variants (temporal, separate, raster-scan) and Ma-tryoshka hierarchical grouping. A systematic ablation across 29 configurations reveals that the contrastive strength (via 

𝜆 or 𝜏 ) controls where a variant sits on the Pareto frontier. At low weight ( 𝜆 =0.01 ), all variants preserve reconstruction (𝑅 2 = 0.31 −0.42 ). At high weight, Temporal+M exceeds raw temporal coherence (lag-1 = 0.462 vs. 0.435). The best con-figurations improve action discrimination by +3.9% over raw features and boost text-video retrieval by 2.8 × R@1 on DI-NOv2. Matryoshka grouping concentrates action-relevant information into 20% of the dictionary. 

## 2 Related Work 

Sparse Autoencoders for Interpretability. SAEs were introduced to decompose neural network activations into sparse, monosemantic features [ 2 , 4 ]. Subsequent work scaled SAEs to frontier language

models [ 21 ]. Key architectural innovations include TopK activa-tion [ 6], JumpReLU [ 18 ], BatchTopK with Matryoshka grouping [ 3 ]for hierarchical feature organization, and auxiliary losses for dead latent recovery. 

SAEs for Vision Models. Recent work has extended SAEs to vi-sion encoders, establishing that sparse decomposition yields inter-pretable visual features. Pach et al. [ 15 ] train SAEs on CLIP and introduce the MonoSemanticity evaluation framework we adopt in this work, demonstrating that SAE interventions on the vision en-coder can steer multimodal LLM outputs. Stevens et al. [ 19 ] enable patch-level causal edits via SAEs on frozen ViTs, providing a rigor-ous causal methodology that motivates our ablation experiments. Joseph et al. [ 9 ] provide a steerability analysis of CLIP’s vision transformer, and Lim et al. [ 12 ] introduce PatchSAE for concept-level analysis during adaptation. While these works demonstrate the promise of SAEs for vision, they all operate on static images. None examine how the temporal structure of video interacts with sparse decomposition, which is the focus of our study. 

Temporal SAEs. Bhalla et al. [ 1] propose Temporal SAEs (T-SAEs) for language models, adding contrastive losses on adjacent tokens to disentangle semantic from positional features. They demonstrate that temporal consistency improves feature quality for text se-quences. We extend this temporal contrastive principle from 1D text sequences to spatio-temporal video representations, where the challenge is qualitatively different: video tokens have both spatial adjacency within frames and temporal adjacency across frames, and the visual content at a given spatial position can change sub-stantially between frames due to motion. Our raster-scan variant (Section 3.2) addresses this by unifying spatial and temporal con-trastive objectives in a single loss. 

Temporal Consistency in Video Representations. Object-centric approaches such as Slot Contrast [25] learn temporally consistent slots by contrasting object representations across frames. Spatio-temporal attention attribution methods [ 24 ] provide post-hoc ex-planations of video model predictions. Temporal contrastive self-supervised methods such as TCLR [ 5] promote temporal diver-sity, while multi-pretext frameworks like CSTP [ 26 ] learn temporal structure during pretraining. Optical flow methods like RAFT [ 20 ]provide dense correspondences that could serve as temporal pairing targets. Our approach is complementary to these methods. We de-compose frozen features into sparse dictionaries rather than learn-ing new representations or modifying pretraining. Our adjacency-based contrastive losses operate on fixed spatial positions rather than tracked objects. This is a deliberate design choice: SAE fea-tures should decompose the model’s representation as-is, without introducing external correspondence mechanisms. We discuss the limitations of fixed spatial pairing under camera motion in Sec-tion 7. 

Interpretability for Action Models. Bear et al. [ 8] apply mechanis-tic interpretability to Vision-Language-Action models for robotics using logit lens projections. Khan et al. [ 11 ] use SAEs on VLA resid-ual streams to construct steering vectors for robotic control. Both operate in the robotics domain with action-specific models. Our work addresses general-purpose video vision models and estab-lishes interpretability foundations applicable to any video under-standing system.

## 4 Experimental Setup 

We extract features from: (1) DINOv2 ViT-B/14 [14 ], a self-supervised image model, producing (16 , 256 , 768 ) features per clip; and (2) 

VideoMAE-B [ 22 ], a self-supervised video model pretrained on SSv2, producing (8, 196 , 768 ) features. Our main results use the final layer (layer 11); we additionally analyze layers 3 and 7 in Section 6.5. This pairing lets us compare how SAEs behave on image-pretrained vs. video-pretrained representations. 

Datasets. (1) Something-Something v2 (SSv2) [7]: 220k clips of fine-grained hand-object interactions with 174 action templates (e.g., “Pushing [something] from left to right”). The action labels provide semantic supervision for evaluating feature quality. (2) 

Kinetics-400 (K400) [10 ]: 400 action classes with diverse scenes and appearance variation, testing generalization beyond fine-grained actions. All SAEs use expansion factor 8 × (𝐻 = 6,144 ), TopK with 𝑘 =

64 , and auxiliary loss coefficient 𝛼 = 0.03 . SAEs are trained for 10 epochs on 10,000 clips. Standard SAEs use batch size 4,096 on flattened tokens; clip-based variants use batch size 8 on whole clips. We set 𝜆 𝑡 = 0.1, 𝜆 𝑠 = 0.05 , 𝜏 = 0.1. All experiments use 3 random seeds. Variance across seeds is below 1% for all reported metrics, so we report means throughout. Training times on A100 GPUs: standard SAE (flat tokens) trains in ∼30 minutes; clip-based contrastive variants train in ∼60 minutes ( ∼2× overhead from per-clip loss computation); raster variants train in ∼25 minutes (pair-based, no clip structure). 

Evaluation Metrics. We evaluate along seven axes: (1) Recon-struction quality : 𝑅 2 (variance explained). (2) Temporal coher-ence : mean lag-1 autocorrelation of SAE features across frames. 

(3) MonoSemanticity Score (MS) : activation-weighted pairwise DINOv2 cosine similarity of each feature’s top-activating clips, fol-lowing Pach et al. [ 15 ]. (4) Action purity : fraction of a feature’s top-𝑘 activating clips sharing the dominant action template. (5) Lin-ear probe : top-1 accuracy of logistic regression on SAE features for action classification. (6) Sparsity : 𝐿 0 and dead feature fraction. 

(7) Feature uniqueness : Jaccard index between top-activating clip sets across features (lower = more unique). 

## 5 SAEs on Video: Interpretability and Temporal Failure 

We first show that standard SAEs decompose video features into interpretable, monosemantic concepts. We then diagnose a core failure mode: temporal incoherence from hard TopK selection. 

## 5.1 Standard SAE Analysis 

Both backbones produce well-behaved SAEs on SSv2 (10K clips, 

8× expansion). SAE features outperform raw features on action classification for both models, with exact sparsity ( 𝐿 0 = 64 ) and no dead features. Jaccard indices below 0.01 confirm that features partition the input space into distinct, non-overlapping concepts. 

Cross-backbone monosemanticity validation. VideoMAE features achieve MS = 0.972 compared to DINOv2’s 0.359 under DINOv2-based similarity, a 2.7× gap. We investigate whether this reflects genuine interpretability differences or metric alignment. The MS metric uses DINOv2 cosine similarity as the clip similarity backbone, following Pach et al. [ 15 ]. We recompute MS using CLIP ViT-B/32 as a neutral similarity backbone (Table 1). Under CLIP similarity, both backbones achieve MS ≈ 0.68 across all variants, eliminating the gap entirely. The DINOv2-based MS inflates VideoMAE scores due to shared self-supervised inductive biases. This identifies a limitation of the Pach et al. protocol: it is reliable within a backbone (where the bias is constant) but not 

across backbones. We retain DINOv2-based MS for within-backbone comparisons in Table 3. Temporal and Separate offer balanced discrimination. Separate matches Temporal’s probe while adding independent spatial consistency, but distributes predictive signal more broadly. Both are well-suited when Matryoshka’s hierarchical structure is unnecessary and a single contrastive loss suffices. 

Raster excels at reconstruction and monosemanticity. Raster pre-serves the highest 𝑅 2 (0.418 at 𝜆 =0.01 ) and achieves the best MS score (0.433 at 𝜆 =0.5). Its pair-based loss applies only local con-trastive pressure between adjacent patches, disrupting reconstruc-tion less than clip-level objectives (temporal/separate) which en-force global consistency across all frames. The same local consis-tency acts as an inductive bias for monosemanticity: each feature must agree with both its spatial and temporal neighbors, forcing coherent single-concept representations. However, probe accuracy drops at high 𝜆 because this local consistency redirects capacity away from global action discrimination. Raster is best suited for applications prioritizing reconstruction fidelity or feature inter-pretability over downstream classification. 

## 6.2 Action Purity Analysis 

Spatio-temporal variants produce features that more consistently activate on clips sharing the same action template. Across the top-50 features on DINOv2/SSv2, mean action purity increases from 1.36/8 (standard) to 1.60/8 (temporal contrastive) to 1.74/8 (raster contrastive). The raster variant achieves features where half of the top-8 clips share a single action (max purity 4/8), compared to 3/8 for the standard SAE. 

## 6.3 Causal Feature Ablation 

To verify that SAE features are functionally important for action discrimination (not merely correlated), we perform a causal feature ablation following Stevens et al. [ 19 ]. We train a linear probe once on full SAE features, identify the top-𝑁 most important features by probe weight magnitude, zero them out, and evaluate with the same probe weights (no retraining). As a control, we ablate 𝑁 random features. Table 4 reveals clear differences in how variants distribute predic-tive information. The standard SAE shows moderate redundancy: ablating the top 50 features drops accuracy by 2.8 pp while ran-dom ablation drops only 0.1 pp. The non-monotonicity at larger 

𝑁 (e.g., partial recovery at 𝑁 =100 ) reflects interactions between positively and negatively weighted features in the multiclass probe. The temporal contrastive SAE shows extreme concentration at the top: removing just 10 features (0.16% of the dictionary) drops accuracy from 20.2% to 10.9% ( −9.3 pp). Raster shows the strongest sustained drop: top-500 ablation reduces accuracy from 19.3% to 8.5% ( −10 .8 pp), more than halving it. Separate+M is similarly con-centrated ( −9.4 pp at 𝑁 =500 ). These results confirm that contrastive and Matryoshka training concentrate predictive information into specific identifiable features rather than distributing it broadly. The concentration pattern varies by variant: temporal focuses signal into a handful of features, while raster and Separate+M spread it across a larger but still identifiable subset. This difference has a mechanistic explanation. The temporal contrastive loss encourages the same features to fire across con-secutive frames at the same spatial position. This creates a small set of “anchor” features that reliably track temporal structure. The probe learns to weight these anchors heavily, making the dictionary efficient but fragile to targeted ablation. Raster’s unified loss, by contrast, enforces consistency across all adjacent pairs (including within-frame spatial neighbors), distributing the contrastive signal more broadly. The result is a larger set of moderately important features rather than a few critical ones. Separate+M combines Ma-tryoshka’s hierarchical concentration (action-relevant features in the high-level group) with separate spatial and temporal losses, producing a dictionary where importance is concentrated but dis-tributed across the entire high-level group rather than a handful of individual features. Cross-model validation (DINOv2 vs. VideoMAE), Matryoshka split analysis, and cross-dataset validation (K400) are provided in the Supplementary Material. Spatio-temporal objectives are most beneficial for DINOv2, which lacks temporal pretraining. Video-MAE’s temporal pretraining already provides much of the structure these losses aim to learn. The Matryoshka 20/80 split concentrates action-relevant information into 20% of the dictionary (19.6% probe using only 1,228 of 6,144 features). Results generalize to K400, where temporal contrastive reduces the SAE-to-raw gap by 44%.

We analyze SAE behavior across network depth (layers 3, 7, 11) for both backbones (full tables in Supplementary Material). For DINOv2, action discrimination increases monotonically with depth (layer 11 best at 20.2%). Temporal contrastive training is most ben-eficial at the final layer, where it improves both coherence and probe accuracy. Temporal contrastive has minimal effect on VideoMAE at any layer, likely because its temporal pretraining already provides coherent features.

## 7 Discussion 

Hard TopK vs. soft sparsity. Our results trace the temporal coher-ence problem to the hard top-𝑘 selection. Small input perturbations between consecutive frames can flip which 𝑘 features are active. To test whether soft sparsity mechanisms can address this directly, we train SAE variants using sparsemax [ 13 ] and entmax-1.5 [ 16 ]activations. (Table 6). Table 6 sweeps sparsemax temperature from 0.1 to 50. Increasing temperature raises 𝐿 0 from 1.0 to 20.2 and recovers probe accuracy from 2.0% to 19.8% (surpassing raw features). However, even at 

𝑇 =50 , 𝐿 0 remains far below TopK’s 64 and 𝑅 2 stays negative ( −0.32 ). This confirms that temperature scaling alone cannot bridge the gap between soft and hard sparsity. Reaching TopK-level 𝐿 0 with smooth activations likely requires hybrid approaches (e.g., soft selection with a hard floor). The choice of 𝑘 =64 for TopK follows Gao et al. [6]. 

Learned vs. random dictionaries. To verify that SAE dictionaries learn meaningful structure rather than acting as random overcom-plete projections, we train a control SAE with frozen random de-coder weights (same architecture, only the encoder is optimized). Practical recommendations. We recommend four operating points (see Table 3): Raster at low 𝜆 for reconstruction fidelity, Tempo-ral+M at moderate 𝜏 for temporal coherence, Separate at high 𝜆 

for action discrimination, and Raster at high 𝜆 for interpretability. Spatio-temporal objectives are most beneficial for models lacking temporal pretraining (DINOv2). VideoMAE’s temporal pretraining already provides much of the structure these losses aim to learn. 

Limitations. Our temporal contrastive losses pair the same spa-tial position across consecutive frames. This assumes approximate spatial correspondence. SSv2 uses stationary cameras filming table-top interactions, so the vast majority of clips satisfy this assumption (objects move within the frame but the camera does not). K400 contains more camera motion, which may explain the smaller con-trastive gains on that dataset. When the assumption is violated, the contrastive loss receives noisy positive pairs, which weakens but does not collapse the training signal (InfoNCE is robust to moderate label noise). Optical-flow-based correspondences [ 20 ]could address this but would introduce external dependencies and lose the model-agnostic property of our approach. We fix 𝑘 =64 

and expansion factor 8× throughout. Varying these may shift the reconstruction–coherence frontier. Our main results use 10K clips. Scaling experiments at larger training scales (Supplementary Ma-terial) confirm that probe accuracy improves and the contrastive advantage persists. Our retrieval uses SSv2’s formulaic action tem-plates. Results on naturalistic benchmarks (MSR-VTT, DiDeMo) may differ. We report means across 3 seeds. Maximum observed standard deviation across all metrics is 0.3 pp for probe accuracy and 0.005 for 𝑅 2. Borderline comparisons (e.g., 20.1% vs. 20.2%) should not be overinterpreted. 

## 8 Conclusion 

We presented the first systematic study of Sparse Autoencoders on video representations and established a design principle for spatio-temporal SAEs: the contrastive strength (via loss coefficient or tem-perature) is a controllable axis that determines the reconstruction– coherence operating point. Four findings support this principle. (1) Standard SAEs decom-pose video features into monosemantic concepts; a cross-backbone control reveals both DINOv2 and VideoMAE produce equally monose-mantic features under neutral (CLIP) similarity, identifying a metric alignment artifact in prior evaluation protocols. (2) Standard SAEs destroy temporal coherence (36% lag-1 reduction) due to hard TopK selection. (3) Contrastive SAE variants can exceed raw temporal coherence (lag-1 = 0.462 vs. 0.435) while improving action clas-sification by +3.9% and text-video retrieval by 2.8 × R@1. (4) The contrastive weight determines where each variant sits on the Pareto frontier. Low weight preserves reconstruction ( 𝑅 2 = 0.31 −0.42 ). High weight maximizes temporal coherence. No variant dominates all metrics, but four clear operating points emerge for reconstruc-tion, coherence, discrimination, and interpretability. A frozen decoder control confirms that SAE dictionaries learn meaningful structure ( 𝑅 2 = 0.910 learned vs. 0.578 frozen). A tem-perature sweep over sparsemax shows that soft sparsity can par-tially bridge the gap to hard TopK ( 𝐿 0 from 1.0 to 20.2 at 𝑇 =50 ,recovering 19.8% probe accuracy) but cannot fully replace it. These results motivate hybrid soft-hard sparsity mechanisms and multires-olution decompositions as promising directions for video feature interpretability.
