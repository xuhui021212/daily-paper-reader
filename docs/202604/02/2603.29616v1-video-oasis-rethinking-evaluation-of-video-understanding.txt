Abstract. The inherent complexity of video understanding makes it difficult to attribute whether performance gains stem from visual per-ception, linguistic reasoning, or knowledge priors. While many bench-marks have emerged to assess high-level reasoning, the essential criteria that constitute video understanding remain largely overlooked. Instead of introducing yet another benchmark, we take a step back to re-examine the current landscape of video understanding. In this work, we provide Video-Oasis, a sustainable diagnostic suite designed to systematically evaluate existing evaluations and distill spatio-temporal challenges for video understanding. Our analysis reveals two critical findings: (1) 54% of existing benchmark samples are solvable without visual input or tem-poral context, and (2) on the remaining samples, state-of-the-art models exhibit performance barely exceeding random guessing. To bridge this gap, we investigate which algorithmic design choices contribute to robust video understanding, providing practical guidelines for future research. We hope our work serves as a standard guideline for benchmark con-struction and the rigorous evaluation of architecture development. Code is available at https://github.com/sejong-rcv/Video-Oasis. 

Keywords: Video Understanding · Video-LLM · Benchmark Auditing 

1 Introduction 

The rise of multi-modal language models has steered video understanding from specific tasks toward the integration of perception and reasoning. While earlier benchmarks focused on narrow domains like action recognition or temporal lo-calization, video large language models (video-LLMs) [1, 5, 20, 33, 41] are now required to handle both fine-grained dynamics [9, 18, 24] and long-form reason-ing [12, 43, 55]. Given the comprehensive nature of video understanding, it is difficult to disentangle the specific sources of performance gains in the bench-marks, whether they stem from visual perception, linguistic reasoning, or even background knowledge. As benchmarks continue to proliferate, each emphasiz-ing unique differentiating factors, it is impractical to validate every benchmark across all aspects of video-LLMs. A unified set of criteria to be upheld at least from the standpoint of video understanding would benefit both benchmark cre-ators and users.

2 Related Work 

2.1 Large Language Models for Video Understanding 

The evolution of video-LLMs [1, 5, 20, 33, 41] has centered on aligning high-dimensional visual features with the semantic space of LLMs. Early research employed temporal pooling [20] or attention mechanisms [41] to compress video frames into token sequences within the model’s context window. More recently, the field has shifted toward scaling context windows [5, 33, 35] to process longer video sequences without aggressive temporal compression. Despite these ad-vancements, video-LLMs often struggle with complex temporal narratives due to static processing manner. To address this, dynamic agentic frameworks [3, 4, 34, 42, 45, 50, 51, 54] have emerged as a proactive paradigm, utilizing structured task decomposition and iterative reasoning loops. While the synergy between video-LLMs and agentic frameworks has led to rapid gains on existing bench-marks [12, 24, 43, 55], it remains unclear whether these gains stem from visual perception, linguistic reasoning, or knowledge priors. 

2.2 Challenges in Video Benchmark Construction 

As video-LLMs [5, 21] and agentic methods [3, 10, 51] rapidly improve perfor-mance on existing benchmarks [12, 24, 43, 55], evaluation benchmarks must be constructed with greater rigor to properly attribute these gains. However, the inherent complexity of video understanding, combined with the large volume of data, often makes the construction of evaluation datasets challenging. Conse-quently, dataset construction pipelines frequently rely on automatic strategies, such as generating questions from selected keyframes [15, 17] or using LLMs to produce questions based on video transcripts [18, 43, 48]. In this process, it be-comes unclear whether the resulting benchmarks truly evaluate video-essential properties that distinguish the modality from others, such as temporal continu-ity, causal interaction, and multi-event narratives. This necessitates a rigorous re-examination of whether current pipelines preserve essential video understand-ing, and if performance gains reflect spatio-temporal reasoning. 

2.3 Toward Robust Video Understanding Benchmarks 

As benchmark proliferation enables comprehensive evaluation, it also introduces unintended issues. Apollo [58] highlights the redundancy across existing bench-marks, while Cambrian-S [49] observes that many tasks remain overly concen-trated on perception-oriented evaluation. In line with these works [29, 49, 58], we take a step back to re-examine the current landscape of video understand-ing. Building upon these efforts, we introduce Video-Oasis (V-Oasis), a diag-nostic suite that distills existing datasets to isolate core spatio-temporal chal-lenges while suppressing unintended shortcuts through visual–temporal decou-pling tests. By integrating cross-model consensus and human-in-the-loop verifi-cation, V-Oasis extends prior analyses by providing a sustainable framework for auditing modern video understanding benchmarks. 3 Video-Oasis: Diagnostic Suite for Video Understanding 

Establishing reliable protocols for measuring spatio-temporal reasoning remains a critical yet underexplored challenge in video understanding. To address this, we introduce Video-Oasis (V-Oasis), a diagnostic framework for verifying the fun-damental criteria of video understanding in video-LLMs. This section proceeds as follows. First, we introduce our test design, which systematically decouples video understanding into its essential dependencies (Section 3.1). Next, we au-dit existing benchmarks using our diagnostic protocols (Section 3.2). Finally, we present empirical analyses demonstrating the scalability and robustness of the framework (Section 3.3). Experimental settings and prompt designs are detailed in the supplementary material. 

3.1 Design of the Diagnostic Framework Is the Video Strictly Necessary? We define a task as visually dependent only if it cannot be resolved through the following non-visual shortcuts, as illustrated in Figure 2(a): (i) Blind Test , which provides only the question and answer options to identify cases solvable through linguistic bias or world knowledge without any visual input; (ii) Audio Test , where the video’s audio track is transcribed into text and given to the model in place of the video; and (iii) 

Narrative Test , where the raw video is replaced by a concatenated sequence of captions [44] extracted at fixed intervals. Both the Audio and Narrative tests evaluate whether models rely on simple text matching rather than grounded visual perception. While recent methods develop sophisticated text-based pipelines, such as iterative document retrieval and updating, to treat video reasoning as long-document comprehension [23,42], our setup is intentionally naive for purely diagnostic purposes. We clarify that our objective focuses on intrinsic visual perception, ensuring that models cannot bypass visual processing by merely reading these basic text summaries. 6 G. Lim et al. 

Is the Temporal Context Indispensable? Temporal dependency is a defin-ing characteristic of video data; without it, the essence of video understanding is weakened. As illustrated in Figure 2(b), we verify this dependency through three rigorous stress tests: (i) Center-Frame Baseline , which provides only the middle frame to detect if the task functions merely as spatial recognition without temporal depth; (ii) Frame Shuffling , where we randomly permute the frame order to disrupt temporal causality and measure the model’s sensi-tivity to chronological sequences; and (iii) Bag-of-Frames (BoF) Matching ,which employs a frozen CLIP-based encoder [31, 36, 53] that lacks any temporal prior, to perform top-k frame matching against the query. If this non-temporal, similarity-based approach succeeds, the task does not necessitate temporal rea-soning but rather simple visual pattern recognition. Human-in-the-loop Verification. Unlike other modalities, the large volume of video data introduces task ambiguities, such as imprecise annotations. While many existing dataset construction pipelines often overlook this uncertainty, we propose three representative criteria that necessitate manual inspection, as illustrated in Figure 3: (i) Consistency , identifying cases where models [1, 5, 11, 21, 40] fail to reach a consensus, which suggests inherent ambiguity or non-unique answers; (ii) Redundancy , investigating cases solvable via any arbitrary video segment, revealing flawed question designs or global biases that fail to anchor the answer to a specific temporal segment; and (iii) Sensitivity , where we manually verify cases in which models succeed despite frame shuffling to account for potential uncertainty in the sequential understanding of video-LLMs. As shown in Table 4, the consistency and redundancy test identified 666 and 477 ambiguous samples, respectively. Three human annotators reviewed these cases and excluded 410 samples by majority vote. For the sensitivity criteria, we re-evaluated the unique samples that passed the initial frame shuffling test, leading to the replacement of 804 samples that were manually verified to require temporal dependency. 4 Distilling the Challenges of Video Understanding 

We next examine the core challenges that remain after the Video-Oasis diagnos-tic process and how state-of-the-art video understanding models behave under these constraints. Here, we: (i) investigate what challenges are distilled through V-Oasis into structured evaluation settings (Section 4.1); (ii) evaluate state-of-the-art models under this distilled setting, revealing the limited capability of current models in handling strict spatio-temporal dependencies (Section 4.2). 

4.1 Understanding the Video-Native Challenges 

Through the rigorous V-Oasis diagnostic suite, samples requiring stronger spatio-temporal dependencies are identified. Beyond identifying these samples, we ana-lyze the challenges that characterize the remaining set. To this end, rather than manually inspecting each sample, we design an efficient pipeline that leverages existing annotations. Specifically, we aggregate the metadata from the surviving QA pairs and prompt an LLM [13] to perform an initial grouping. Building upon these initial clusters, we systematically re-label the QA pairs. To ensure rigor-ous annotation, we employ an ensemble of five proprietary LLMs (OpenAI o3, o4-mini, GPT-4o, GPT5-mini, and GPT-5 [26–28]), requiring agreement from at least three models. Any samples failing to reach this consensus (122 samples) are manually inspected and labeled by the authors. As a result, we identify the following five core categories that characterize the video-native challenges. 

– Fine-Grained Perception: requires grounding fine-grained recognition in a spatio-temporal context, where visual identification depends on how details evolve across space and time. 

– Spatial World Understanding: requires synthesizing fragmented, multi-view evidence across frames to deduce 3D context, including relative posi-tions, geometry, and trajectories. 

– Temporal Dynamics & Tracking: requires monitoring explicit changes over time, such as object tracking, action sequencing, and state transitions, strictly demanding temporal ordering to prevent reliance on unordered frame matching. 

– Causality & Logical Reasoning: requires deducing latent cause-and-effect relationships, physical laws, and unobserved intentions, going beyond pixel-level observations to probe the implicit logic of the video. 

– Global Narrative: requires aggregating disparate events across the entire timeline to summarize overarching plots or long-term semantics, effectively filtering out irrelevant contexts over extended video horizons. Our goal is not to introduce novel tasks, but to refine the fundamental capa-bilities of video understanding. While these categories may resemble traditional taxonomies, they are derived from a bottom-up, data-driven process. 5 Exploring Algorithmic Designs 

Building upon the video-native challenges revealed by V-Oasis, we investigate the algorithmic design choices that shape video reasoning performance. In this section, we leverage our diagnostic testbed to systematically examine how dif-ferent architectural designs and optimization paradigms affect model behaviors. 

5.1 The Role of Temporal Localization 

Temporal video grounding, the ability to precisely associate user queries with their corresponding temporal segments, serves as a critical design component in environments where spatio-temporal dependency is strict. To investigate its practical impact, we conduct an ablation study by employing AKS [38] to re-trieve the 16 most relevant frames as an initial exploration. As shown in Table 8, integrating this grounding pipeline yields consistent improvements across mod-els. However, the magnitude of these gains, specifically 1.4% for Eagle2.5 [5] and 2.3% for Qwen3-VL-Instruct [1], appears marginal given their relatively low baseline performance. This observation leads to a fundamental inquiry: is the bottleneck the model’s reasoning capacity or the precision of the grounding algorithm?

6 Conclusion 

In this work, we establish Video-Oasis (V-Oasis), a rigorous diagnostic suite for robust video understanding. Through this diagnostic lens, we analyze the vulnerabilities of existing benchmarks with respect to video dependency and re-examine the current landscape of video understanding. Beyond diagnosis, our algorithmic exploration provides several insights, highlighting temporal ground-ing and adaptive reasoning as primary drivers of spatio-temporal reasoning. We further identify an underexplored area in training paradigms, raising the balance between SFT and RLVR as an open question. Our work is fully reproducible, and we open-source the entire pipeline to enable large-scale auditing of existing datasets and provide an extensible evaluation protocol for the community. We hope V-Oasis serves as a foundation for developing more rigorous benchmarks and drives the next generation of models toward genuine video understanding. A.1 More Than Hard Questions: Validity of Video-Oasis 

We investigate whether Video-Oasis reveals diagnostic properties rather than simply identifying questions that current models struggle with. C Implementation Details of Video-Oasis 

C.1 Visual Dependency Tests 

In this setting, visual input is removed, and the model ( M) is evaluated based solely on textual context. 

Response = M (V, Pdiag (Ctype , Q, O)) , (1) where V denotes the visual input and Pdiag is a system prompt (Table S9) that formats the context Ctype together with the question Q and options O. For all tests, we set V = ∅ to remove the influence of visual context. 

Blind Test ( Cblind = ∅) In this configuration, all auxiliary inputs are removed. The model must rely solely on linguistic priors and internal world knowledge. 

Audio Test ( Caudio = Twhisper ) Here, the context is replaced by a text tran-script T generated from the video’s audio track. We use whisper-v3 (large) [32] to extract speech. This setup identifies questions that can be solved solely from the speech transcript, indicating that they primarily require textual understanding rather than visual reasoning. 

Narrative Test ( Cnarrative = Sconcat ) The video is uniformly partitioned into 8 temporal chunks (16 frames each), where a single caption is extracted per chunk via CARE [44] and concatenated chronologically to form Sconcat . Since our setup relies on simple caption concatenation without sophisticated summarization, suc-cess in this test suggests that the task reflects document-level understanding (e.g ., pattern matching or attribute recognition) rather than spatio-temporal video understanding.

Temporal Context Strategy ( S) We vary the frame selection strategy S to examine how models respond under different conditions. 

– Center Frame ( Scenter ): Returns a single frame from the temporal center of V. This test identifies whether the task can be solved using spatial cues alone, often due to redundancy in video frames. 

– Frame Shuffling ( Sshuffle ): Returns a randomly permuted subset of 128 uniformly sampled frames from V. This process disrupts the causal arrow of time by breaking chronological order. 

– Top-k Matching ( Stopk ): Returns a subset of k frames ( k = 32 ) exhibiting the highest cosine similarity with the query Q in the VLM embedding space. 

Model Perspective ( M) We categorize the models into two types based on how they process the configured temporal subset S(V):

– MLLM ( MMLLM ): Multimodal large language models [1,5,21], pretrained to reason over temporal dynamics in video data. We evaluate the model responses under temporal disruption ( e.g ., center-frame ( Scenter ) or shuffling (Sshuffle )) to assess whether the question relies on temporal context. 

– VLM ( MVLM ): Vision-language models pretrained on static image-text pairs ( e.g. , CLIP [31,36,53]), which process frames independently. We evalu-ate them via top-k cosine-similarity matching ( Stopk ) between frame embed-dings and answer candidates. This setup is temporal-agnostic, as the model processes frames independently without modeling temporal relations. These tests diagnose whether the question depends on temporal context by systematically disrupting the temporal structure of the input video. The over-all diagnostic procedure for the temporal dependency tests is summarized in Algorithm 1. 28 G. Lim et al. 

C.3 Ambiguity Tests 

In this setting, we introduce diagnostic tests to identify potential annotation issues arising during dataset construction: 

– Consistency Test. We employ five different models [1, 5, 11, 21, 40] and identify samples where all models produce different predictions. Such strong disagreement suggests that the question may admit multiple plausible inter-pretations, indicating potential annotation ambiguity. 

– Redundancy Test. Using Eagle2.5 [5], we divide each video into 8 tempo-ral chunks (16 frames each) and evaluate the model on each chunk indepen-dently. If all chunks lead to the correct answer, the question may not rely on specific temporal evidence and can be answered from multiple segments, indicating that the annotation may be weakly constrained.

D.3 Extended Qualitative Examples: Filtered Shortcuts 

Our diagnostic framework, Video-Oasis (V-Oasis), identifies tasks that can be re-solved without visual input (Visual Dependency) or temporal context (Temporal Dependency). Figure S6 presents qualitative examples of these filtered shortcuts across various diagnostic tests. We categorize visual shortcuts, which allow tasks to be resolved without video input, as follows: Commonsense , which enables inferring logical outcomes such as placing trash in a dustbin; World Knowledge , which relies on pre-trained facts such as Gucci’s history; Dataset Bias , which exploits frequent correlations such as the association between a red hat and Super Mario; and Document Un-derstanding , which involves text-based pattern matching from video captions or audio tracks that bypasses the need for spatio-temporal grounding. Similarly, temporal shortcuts that do not require video-specific temporal con-text include: Lack of Dynamics , where a single frame provides sufficient in-formation due to the absence of temporal change; Single Object Attribute ,which focuses on time-invariant properties such as weather or object color that require only static reasoning; and Permutation-Invariant tasks, such as count-ing or identifying shared features, which remain solvable regardless of the frame sequence.
