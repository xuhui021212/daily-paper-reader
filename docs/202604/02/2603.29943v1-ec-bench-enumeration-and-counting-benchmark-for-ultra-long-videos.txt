Abstract. Counting in long videos remains a fundamental yet under-explored challenge in computer vision. Real-world recordings often span tens of minutes or longer and contain sparse, diverse events, making long-range temporal reasoning particularly difficult. However, most ex-isting video counting benchmarks focus on short clips and evaluate only the final numerical answer, providing little insight into what should be counted or whether models consistently identify relevant instances across time. We introduce EC-Bench , a benchmark that jointly evaluates enu-meration , counting , and temporal evidence grounding in long-form videos. EC-Bench contains 152 videos longer than 30 minutes and 1,699 queries paired with explicit evidence spans. Across 22 multimodal large language models (MLLMs), the best model achieves only 29.98% accuracy on Enu-meration and 23.74% on Counting, while human performance reaches 78.57% and 82.97%, respectively. Our analysis reveals strong relation-ships between enumeration accuracy, temporal grounding, and counting performance. These results highlight fundamental limitations of current MLLMs and establish EC-Bench as a challenging benchmark for long-form quantitative video reasoning. The data and code are publicly avail-able at: https://github.com/matsuolab/EC-Bench .

1 Introduction 

Understanding long-form videos is rapidly emerging as a frontier problem in computer vision. Real-world recordings often span tens of minutes and con-tain sparse and heterogeneous events, requiring models to maintain entity con-sistency, reason over event dependencies, and integrate limited visual evidence across extended temporal contexts [5,44,52,59]. Although multimodal large lan-guage models (MLLMs) now support very long context windows, most existing evaluations still focus on short-video settings [24, 48, 50], and therefore do not test whether models can sustain coherent reasoning over long temporal horizons. In this work, we posit that accurate long-video counting requires three closely related abilities: identifying the correct set of instances, determining their tem-poral spans, and producing a consistent count. To evaluate these abilities jointly, we introduce EC-Bench , a benchmark for long-form quantitative video reason-ing. Unlike existing long-video bench-marks [32, 44, 59] and counting benchmarks [11, 12, 31], EC-Bench jointly sup-ports ultra-long videos, multi-hop reasoning across temporally distant events, explicit temporal evidence, and enumeration-based evaluation, within a single benchmark (Table 1). These properties enable systematic evaluation of the core components required for quantitative reasoning in long-form videos, which can-not be measured with existing short-video counting datasets or long-video QA benchmarks alone. The best model achieves only 29.98% accuracy on Enumeration and 23.74% on Counting, while human performance reaches 78.57% and 82.97%, re-spectively. Further analysis suggests that enumeration accuracy, temporal ground-ing, and counting performance are closely related, with many errors associated with failures in temporal evidence localization. Consistent with these findings, explicitly enumerating instances improves counting accuracy, while increasing the number of input frames yields only limited gains. These observations sug-gest that accurate instance identification and temporal grounding are closely associated with strong counting performance. Overall, EC-Bench provides a unified framework for structured and inter-pretable evaluation of quantitative reasoning in long-form videos. Our main contributions are summarized as follows:

– We conduct a comprehensive evaluation across 22 MLLMs, revealing sub-stantial limitations in both open-source and proprietary systems for long-video quantitative reasoning.

2 Related Work 

2.1 Multi-modal Large Language Models 

Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) have accelerated the de-velopment of MLLMs [3, 14, 18, 36]. These models achieve strong performance across diverse vision–language tasks [16,20,33], and open-source systems such as LLaVA [28] and InstructBLIP [10] demonstrate the effectiveness of instruction tuning. A key emerging direction is video understanding, where recent video-capable MLLMs [9, 17, 43, 54, 57, 58] extend image-based architectures to the video domain by treating images and videos as unified multi-frame sequences. However, these models remain optimized for short clips, typically seconds to a few minutes, and struggle with long-term temporal reasoning, such as maintain-ing context across scene transitions or integrating evidence from distant tempo-ral segments. To address these limitations, prior work has explored compressing video inputs into lightweight visual tokens [27, 30, 40, 42, 47] and introducing system-level optimizations for long-context processing [8, 56]. Despite these ad-vances, current MLLMs still face challenges in sustained reasoning and memory over extended video sequences. 

2.2 Benchmarks for Video-Based MLLMs 

Early video benchmarks focused on short clips [21,22,48,50]. Traditional VideoQA datasets such as TVQA [22] and NExT-QA [48] target temporal reasoning but remain limited to short durations. More recent MLLM-oriented benchmarks such as MVBench [24] and EgoSchema [32] evaluate temporal understanding in short or first-person videos, typically spanning only a few to tens of sec-onds. Subsequent work extends to longer videos, including ActivityNet-QA [53], VideoMME [13], and MLVU [61], while datasets such as LongVideoBench [59] EC-Bench 5

and MoVQA [25] reach tens of minutes. Ultra-long video benchmarks, including CG-Bench [6], LVBench [44], VRBench [52], HourVideo [5], and HD-EPIC [37], further extend to hour-scale videos. Despite this progress, existing benchmarks remain limited in evaluating long-form quantitative reasoning (Table 1). First, many benchmarks rely on multiple-choice formats, making it difficult to determine whether models perform genuine multi-step reasoning or exploit answer biases, especially in tasks requiring tempo-ral grounding or multi-frame integration. Second, counting-related questions are sparse and typically limited to simple cases. More importantly, existing bench-marks do not explicitly evaluate whether models identify all relevant instances and maintain their consistency across time, which is essential for reliable count-ing in long videos. 

2.3 Video Counting Benchmarks 

Compared with general video benchmarks, only a few datasets explicitly eval-uate video counting capabilities of MLLMs. Recent work such as Every Shot Counts [41] studies repetition counting in videos, while datasets such as DVD-Counting [1] and VideoNIAH [60] focus on object counting. However, these datasets typically involve short videos and therefore do not capture the chal-lenges of long-term temporal reasoning, where instances must be identified and aggregated across extended time spans. To move beyond purely visual settings, CG-AV-Counting [31] introduces cross-modal counting that incorporates both visual and auditory cues. Nevertheless, existing benchmarks do not explicitly evaluate whether models correctly identify all relevant instances and aggregate them consistently over time, which is essential for reliable counting in long videos. To address this limitation, EC-Bench evaluates structured counting and enumer-ation reasoning in ultra-long videos with explicit temporal evidence spans. 

3 EC-Bench 

We introduce EC-Bench, a benchmark for long-form quantitative video under-standing. EC-Bench evaluates whether models can (i) identify the relevant set of countable instances , (ii) localize temporal evidence spans supporting those in-stances, and (iii) aggregate them into a correct numerical answer over extended temporal contexts.

Evidence spans. For each query, we annotate one or multiple evidence spans ,temporal sub-intervals that directly support the queried instances or their oc-currences. In addition to producing an answer (a list for Enumeration and an integer for Counting), models are required to predict evidence spans on the orig-inal video timeline. This enables transparent inspection of model reasoning and facilitates interpretable evaluation. 

Reasoning categories. To capture diverse reasoning patterns in long-form videos, EC-Bench organizes queries into six reasoning categories. Fig. 2 provides representative Enumeration and Counting examples for each category. 

3.2 Data Construction 

EC-Bench consists of 152 long-form videos, each exceeding 30 minutes in du-ration. The dataset is built upon LVBench [44] and expanded with additional sources across diverse domains, including sports, TV programs, documentaries, and online self-media. For each video, we construct both Enumeration and Counting queries, resulting in 1,699 open-ended queries. Multiple-choice for-mats are excluded to avoid answer biases and to better evaluate long-horizon reasoning with explicit evidence. 

Prompt design for QA generation. We design a structured prompt to gen-erate high-quality Enumeration and Counting queries for long-form videos. The prompt instructs the model to analyze both visual and audio content over the entire video and generate queries spanning six reasoning categories. It further EC-Bench 7

enforces that queries depend on full-video context, include explicit evidence spans, and follow standardized answer formats. To ensure consistency, we adopt a JSON-based output format and provide template examples across different video genres. Detailed prompts are provided in the supplementary material. 

Annotation and quality control. We adopt an annotation protocol inspired by prior long-video benchmarks [44, 59]. We first generate initial query–answer pairs using Gemini-2.5-pro , followed by human verification. Annotators re-vise ambiguous or redundant queries, correct missing or misidentified instances, and refine evidence span boundaries to align with corresponding visual or audio events. Each sample is further cross-checked by another annotator to ensure con-sistency. Details of the annotation workflow are provided in the supplementary material.

3.4 Evaluation Metrics Enumeration. For Enumeration, we evaluate the quality of predicted item lists using precision, recall, and F1-score. To match predicted items with ground-truth annotations, we employ an external LLM-as-a-Judge procedure that classifies EC-Bench 9

each predicted item as a True Positive (TP), False Positive (FP), or False Neg-ative (FN). Precision ( T P/ (T P + F P )), recall ( T P/ (T P + F N )), and F1-score are computed from these classifications. We validate the LLM-as-Judge through a human study on 100 Enumeration queries, showing a strong correlation with human F1 scores (Spearman ρ = 0 .8233 , p < 0.0001 ). 

Counting. For Counting, we report exact-match accuracy and Mean Absolute Error (MAE), as exact matching alone may be overly strict when the predicted count is close to the ground truth. 

Evidence spans. We evaluate temporal evidence grounding using temporal IoU (tIoU) between predicted and ground-truth evidence spans. When multiple spans are present, predicted spans are merged into a single interval P , and ground-truth spans into a single interval G. We then compute tIoU = |P ∩ G|/|P ∪ G|. This metric reflects how accurately the model localizes the overall temporal evidence region. We report mean tIoU over all queries.

4.3 Ablation Studies Effect of enumeration prompting. To investigate the impact of enumera-tion on counting, we introduce an explicit enumeration-first prompting strategy. In this setup, models are instructed to first enumerate relevant events before performing the counting step. The full prompt specification is provided in the supplementary material. Table 7a evaluates this strategy on two open-source and two proprietary models. As shown in Table 7a, enumeration-first prompting consistently improves counting accuracy and reduces MAE across all evaluated models. The gains are larger for open-source models, while proprietary models show smaller but consistent improvements. These results suggest that explicitly enumerating events before aggregation helps stabilize numerical reasoning in long-form videos and improves counting performance without additional model training. 

Effect of frame density. Fig. 7b analyzes the impact of input frame count on model performance. For Gemini-2.5-Flash, increasing the number of frames from 128 to 512 improves both Enumeration and Counting accuracy, indicating that broader temporal coverage helps reduce missed events. However, the gains diminish beyond this point and slightly decrease at higher frame counts, sug-gesting that simply increasing the number of frames introduces redundancy or noise without improving instance identification. A similar pattern is observed for Qwen3-VL 8B, where moderate frame increases provide small improvements but performance drops when too many frames are used. These results suggest that while additional frames can improve temporal coverage, naive increases in frame density do not consistently improve performance. Instead, performance is likely limited by the model’s ability to identify and integrate relevant instances over long temporal contexts.

Failure Analysis. We analyze failure cases using GPT-4o, a representative multimodal model with competitive performance on EC-Bench. The analysis reveals several consistent failure patterns across both Enumeration and Counting tasks: - Undercounting in Crowded Scenes. In crowded scenes, the model often undercounts people or objects because it does not individuate each instance. For example, when asked “What is the maximum number of people trapped in capsules?”, the ground truth is 87 while GPT-4o outputs 3. - Audio-based Failure. For audio-based counting, the model frequently pre-dicts numbers that match the general topic of the video rather than the actual number of occurrences, such as answering 5 instead of the correct 26 for “How many times is AI-generated content shown?”. - Abstraction in Enumeration. In Enumeration tasks, the model often replaces detailed lists with abstract categories, ignoring the requirement to list all items. A typical case is “List the technical terms for Changi Airport 14 F. Tsuchiya et al. 

T4”, where specific items are replaced by broad labels such as “self-service systems”. - World-Knowledge Hallucination. For slide or timeline enumeration, the model sometimes hallucinates items based on world knowledge rather than visual evidence, such as generating NVIDIA-related terms for “List the items under ‘GENERATIVE AI’ at 6:54”. These patterns highlight systematic limitations in instance-level reasoning and evidence grounding in long-form videos. 

5 Discussion 

EC-Bench reveals that long-form video understanding remains a major challenge for current multimodal LLMs. Although proprietary systems such as GPT-5 and the Gemini family outperform open-source models, their absolute accuracy remains far below human performance. Our analysis suggests that the primary difficulty lies in temporal grounding rather than numerical aggregation itself. In long-form videos, models must first identify relevant instances and localize their supporting temporal evidence before aggregating them into a final count. The strong correlation between enumeration accuracy, temporal span localization, and counting performance supports this interpretation. Our ablation studies further reinforce this observation. Enumeration-first prompting consistently improves counting accuracy across models, indicating that explicitly structuring reasoning around instance identification can stabilize numerical reasoning. In contrast, increasing the number of input frames provides only limited improvements and quickly saturates, suggesting that naive scaling of temporal coverage alone does not address the underlying challenge. In summary, these findings highlight that accurate instance identification and temporal grounding are more critical than simply increasing input frame density. Progress in long-video quantitative reasoning will likely require models that explicitly reason over long temporal contexts and ground events in time, rather than relying on architectures designed for short-video settings. 

6 Conclusion 

We introduced EC-Bench, the first benchmark that jointly evaluates enumer-ation, counting, and temporal evidence grounding in long untrimmed videos. Across 22 MLLMs, the best model achieves only 29.98% accuracy on Enumer-ation and 23.74% on Counting, while human performance reaches 78.57% and 82.97%, highlighting the difficulty of quantitative reasoning in long-form videos. Our analysis suggests that accurate instance identification and temporal ground-ing are crucial for reliable counting. Human Performance Evaluation. To provide a reference point for model performance on EC-Bench, we conducted a human evaluation on a subset of benchmark queries. Annotation Protocol 

1. Operations. Annotators update and verify the following fields: 

– Video Information : Video URL (A), Video ID (B), Genre (C) 

– Question Information : Question ID (D), Question Genre (E), Question Type (F: Counting / Enumeration), Question Text (G) 

– Answer and Evidence : Pre-generated Answer (H), Pre-generated Clips (I), Re-vised Answer (J), Evidence Clips (K; format “[t s, t e]”) 

– Additional Fields : Invalid-Question Flag (L), Notes for Difficult Cases (M), Enumeration Corresponding to Counting (N) 2. Step 1: Feasibility Check. Annotators examine the video to determine whether the question is answerable: 

– whether the question is grounded in visible content, 

– whether the pre-generated answer matches the video, 

– whether the assigned task type is appropriate, 

– whether the question can be answered using video-only evidence. If unanswerable, column L is marked with ( ✓). 3. Step 2: Answer Verification and Revision. Annotators validate the pre-generated answer (H). If it is correct, it is retained; otherwise, the corrected answer is written in column J. 

Rules for Counting: 

– Provide only a numerical value (e.g., “3”). 

– Reappearances within 5 seconds count as one occurrence. 

– For type-based questions, multiple instances of the same category count as one type. 

– If no instance exists, write “0”. 

Rules for Enumeration: 

– List items using comma-separated entries. 

– Use arrows for ordered steps (“A → B → C”). 

– Remove modifiers and keep only the core category. 

– If no items exist, write “None”. 

Ambiguous cases: When unclear, annotators select the most reasonable interpreta-tion and briefly describe the difficulty in column M. 4. Step 3: Evidence Extraction. Annotators list all supporting video segments in column K: 

– Second-level precision is sufficient. 

– Include only the minimum necessary clips. 

– For comparison questions, include evidence for all referenced entities. 

– If one appearance suffices, extract only that segment. 

– Merge clips separated by under 5 seconds. 5. Additional Annotation Rules. – Use neutral placeholders when names are unknown. 

– Remove ownership markers (e.g., “A’s car” → “car”). 

– For type-based tasks, count categories rather than instances.

Six Counting Categories A. Parallel Event Counting. Definition: Counting events that occur independently along the timeline, focusing on semantic event recognition. 

Counting example: “How many times does an autonomous truck appear driving on the high-way?” 

Enumeration example: “List all types of flying vehicles that appear in the video.” 

B. Causal Event Counting. Definition: Counting events that involve temporal sequence or causal relationships. 

Counting example: “How many shots are exchanged in the rally beginning at 4:15?” 

Enumeration example: “List the steps involved in the face-recognition payment process.” 

C. Speech & Audio Counting. Definition: Multi-modal counting involving speech or sound events. 

Counting example: “How many times does a siren sound?” 

Enumeration example: “List all industrial fields mentioned by the lecturer.” 

D. Appearance Counting. Definition: Counting how frequently specific objects or entities visually appear. 

Counting example: “How many times does a player wearing a blue uniform appear on screen?” 

Enumeration example: “List all constellation-shaped creatures appearing in the video.” 

E. Spatial Counting. Definition: Counting the number of entities present in a scene or frame. 

Counting example: “How many bicycles are on the road?” 

Enumeration example: “List the future medical technologies introduced in the video.” 

F. Spatio-Temporal Conditional Counting. Definition: Advanced reasoning requiring both spatial and temporal conditions. 

Counting example: “How many shots occur inside the penalty area during the second half?”

– Items separated by commas. 

– Use “ →” for ordered sequences. 

– Do not add explanations.

LLM-as-Judge Prompt 

You are an evaluation model designed to compare two enumeration-style answers. Your goal is to judge correctness, completeness, and semantic equivalence between a ground-truth enumeration and a generated answer. 

Evaluation Tasks – Identify the essential items/entities present in the ground truth 

– Determine which items appear in both answers (true positives) 

– Identify incorrectly added items in the generated answer (false positives) 

– Identify missing ground-truth items (false negatives) 

– Treat semantically equivalent expressions as the same entity 

Handling Synonyms and Paraphrases – Merge expressions referring to the same real-world object or event 

– Combine alternate names, abbreviations, and titles (e.g., “fire truck” = “fire engine”; “goalkeeper” = “goalie”) 

– Ignore modifiers unless they fundamentally change the identity

Rules – Arrays must always be present (empty allowed)

– Output JSON only—no commentary or markdown fencing 

Inputs – True answer: {true_answer}
