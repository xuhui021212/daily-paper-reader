## Abstract 

Composed image retrieval, multi-turn composed image retrieval, and composed video retrieval all share a common paradigm: com-posing the reference visual with modification text to retrieve the desired target. Despite this shared structure, the three tasks have been studied in isolation, with no prior work proposing a unified framework, let alone a zero-shot solution. In this paper, we propose UniCVR, the first unified zero-shot composed visual retrieval frame-work that jointly addresses all three tasks without any task-specific human-annotated data. UniCVR strategically combines two comple-mentary strengths: Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) for compositional query understanding and Vision-Language Pre-trained (VLP) models for structured visual retrieval. Concretely, UniCVR operates in two stages. In Stage I, we train the MLLM as a compositional query embedder via contrastive learning on a cu-rated multi-source dataset of approximately 3.5M samples, bridging the heterogeneous embedding spaces between the MLLM and the frozen VLP gallery encoder. A cluster-based hard negative sampling strategy is proposed to strengthen contrastive supervision. In Stage II, we introduce an MLLM-guided dual-level reranking mechanism that applies adaptive budgeted subset scoring to a small number of top-ranked candidates, and then exploits the resulting relevance signals through a dual-level re-scoring scheme, producing more accurate final rankings with minimal computational overhead. Ex-tensive experiments across five benchmarks covering all three tasks demonstrate that UniCVR achieves cutting-edge performance, vali-dating its effectiveness and generalizability. Our data and code will be released upon acceptance.

While some ZS-CIR methods [ 11 , 57 ] bypass framework design by constructing pseudo-triplets for supervised training, the core challenge lies in how to handle multimodal query understanding and cross-modal visual retrieval. From this perspective, current ZS-CIR methods can be broadly categorized into two groups. The first category consists of textual-inversion-based methods [ 2,23 , 35 ]. These methods train a lightweight network to map a ref-erence image into one or more pseudo-word embeddings in the word space of a Vision-Language Pre-trained (VLP) model ( e.g. ,CLIP [ 34 ]). The pseudo-word embeddings are concatenated with the modification text and encoded by the VLP’s text encoder for visual retrieval. Apparently, this approach relies on the text en-coder primarily designed for simple descriptive text and lacking compositional reasoning, to understand multimodal queries, limit-ing retrieval performance. The second category [ 7, 52 , 53 ] adopts a training-free approach by leveraging (Multimodal) Large Language Models (MLLMs/LLMs) for query understanding, prompting them to generate textual descriptions of the intended target for VLP-based retrieval. While this approach bypasses training, it inevitably loses visual information during the explicit query-to-text conversion, and the quality of the generated descriptions is highly sensitive to prompt design. Furthermore, the resulting descriptions may not align with the textual style preferred by the VLP encoder [ 9 ], lead-ing to suboptimal cross-modal alignment. Therefore, inspired by the recent success of adapting (M)LLMs as embedders [ 18 , 19 , 24 ], rather than employing MLLMs and VLPs in a fully training-free manner, we propose a unified zero-shot CVR framework that strategically leverages their complementary strengths: VLPs are kept frozen to preserve their well-structured cross-modal metric space for reliable visual retrieval, while MLLMs are fine-tuned to enhance compositional and multimodal query understanding, enabling the model to reason about complex visual modifications and align them effectively with the retrieval space. However, realizing this design for strong performance across diverse downstream CVR tasks presents two key challenges. 1) Heterogeneous Embedding Spaces. The output representations of MLLMs reside in a fundamentally different space from the well-structured metric space of VLP models. How to bridge this gap while preserving the MLLM’s powerful multimodal compositional understanding on the query side and maintaining the integrity of the VLP metric space on the target side is the first challenge. 

2) Computational Feasibility of MLLM Reranking. In zero-shot CVR, applying the pre-trained model to a new domain often yields suboptimal initial rankings due to domain shift. Fortunately, the initial retrieval results implicitly encode gallery distribution and candidate relevance, which can be exploited for on-the-fly ranking refinement without additional annotations. MLLMs can interpret complex visual modifications and refine the ranking ac-cordingly [ 24 ]. However, directly applying them to all candidates is computationally prohibitive. The key challenge is thus to de-sign a mechanism that practically harnesses MLLMs’ reasoning capabilities for effective reranking. In this paper, we propose UniCVR , a unified framework for zero-shot Composed Visual Retrieval that addresses both challenges, as shown in Figure 2. UniCVR operates in two stages. In Stage I, Pre-Training for MLLM-VLP Alignment bridges the heterogeneous embedding spaces between the MLLM query encoder and the VLP gallery encoder. An MLLM serves as the composed query encoder, natively handling image-text, multi-turn image-text, and video-text inputs, making it directly applicable to all three CVR tasks. We specifically adopt PEcore [ 3 ] as the gallery encoder, which is jointly pre-trained on large-scale image-text and video-text cor-pora, enabling a unified gallery embedding across both images and videos. To provide diverse and sufficient alignment supervision, we curate a multi-source pre-training dataset of approximately 3.5M samples, with a cluster-based hard negative sampling strat-egy to strengthen the contrastive supervision signal. Notably, the dataset’s effectiveness stems more from its diversity than from its scale, which is modest compared to existing pseudo-triplet dataset. In Stage II, MLLM-Guided Dual-Level Reranking leverages the reasoning capabilities of MLLMs to refine the initial retrieval results. To avoid the prohibitive cost of scoring all gallery candidates with an MLLM, we introduce an adaptive budgeted subset scoring mech-anism that evaluates only a small subset of top-ranked candidates with early termination. The resulting MLLM relevance scores are then exploited through a dual-level re-scoring mechanism: a global re-scoring that refines the query embedding via score-weighted fusion of candidate embeddings and recomputes similarities for all gallery items, and a local re-scoring that directly injects the explicit MLLM relevance scores for the scored candidates, producing more accurate final rankings without incurring excessive computation. Our contributions can be summarized as follows: 

• We propose UniCVR, a unified zero-shot framework for Com-posed Visual Retrieval (CVR) that bridges the powerful multi-modal reasoning of MLLMs with the well-structured metric space of VLP models. To the best of our knowledge, UniCVR is the first to unify CIR, MT-CIR, and CoVR within a single zero-shot frame-work. 

• We propose an MLLM-Guided Dual-Level Reranking mecha-nism that adaptively scores a budgeted subset of candidates and exploits the resulting relevance signals through dual-level re-scoring, i.e., global query refinement and local MLLM score in-jection, achieving effective ranking refinement. 

• Extensive experiments on FashionIQ, CIRR, CIRCO, Multi-Turn FashionIQ, and WebVid-CoVR validate the effectiveness and gen-eralizability of UniCVR across all three CVR tasks. 

## 2 Related Work 

Composed Visual Retrieval . CIR has been studied under both supervised and zero-shot settings [ 36 ]. ZS-CIR methods include textual-inversion approaches [ 1, 2, 12 , 23 , 35 , 39 ] that project ref-erence images into pseudo-word tokens for VLP-based retrieval, training-free captioning approaches [ 7 , 20 , 40 , 52 , 53 ] that prompt (M)LLMs to describe the intended target. Another line of work [ 11 ,16 , 21 , 57 ] constructs pseudo-triplets for contrastive pre-training. MT-CIR [ 14 , 31 , 47 , 56 ] extends CIR to iterative query refinement across multiple rounds, but all existing methods require supervised training on annotated multi-turn dialogues. CoVR [ 6, 42 , 44 , 45 , 51 ]replaces the reference image with a video clip; most works focus on supervised training and ZS-CoVR remains largely unexplored. Despite the shared compositional query paradigm, no prior work has unified these three tasks under a single zero-shot framework. UniCVR bridges this gap by employing an MLLM as a unified com-positional query encoder across all three tasks, paired with a VLP gallery encoder that supports both image and video retrieval. 

MLLMs as Rerankers . Leveraging LLMs for reranking originates from text retrieval. RankGPT [ 37 ] prompts GPT models to perform listwise passage reranking via a sliding-window strategy. Subse-quent works explore pairwise prompting [ 33 ], as well as fine-tuning open-source LLMs for ranking [ 29 ]. In the multimodal domain, LamRA [ 26 ] adapts an MLLM as a listwise reranker for image-text retrieval by fine-tuning with ranking-oriented instructions. MM-Embed [ 24 ] combines MLLM-based bi-encoder retrieval with MLLM pointwise reranking, demonstrating that the two stages are complementary. However, these methods typically apply the MLLM to a fixed candidate set without considering computational cost. UniCVR introduces adaptive budgeted scoring with early termi-nation and a dual-level re-scoring scheme, making MLLM-based reranking computationally feasible for large-scale galleries. 

## 3 Methodology 

In this section, we first give problem formulation and the detail our UniCVR framework. Unlike existing training-free methods [ 7 ,52 , 53 ] that use MLLMs as captioners, UniCVR treats the MLLM as a compositional query embedder, mapping the composed query directly into a dense embedding aligned with the VLP gallery space. This approach avoids the information bottleneck caused by textual intermediaries and the need for manually designed prompts.

## 3.2 Pre-Training for MLLM-VLP Alignment 

While the ZS-CVR setting precludes human-annotated triplets for any downstream task, readily collectible triplets can serve as a viable proxy for learning the alignment between the MLLM query encoder and the VLP gallery space. This component leverages such data to train the composed query encoder via contrastive pre-training, laying a solid foundation for zero-shot generalization to unseen downstream tasks.

3.2.1 Multi-Source Pre-Training Data. Several existing datasets can serve as pre-training resources for zero-shot compositional image retrieval, including MMC [21] and FiGMaQ [16], which have dis-tinct data formats. MMC follows ⟨reference image, modification text, target image caption ⟩, while FiGMaQ supports ⟨reference image, modification text, target image ⟩. These differences offer complementary supervision for semantic transformation and target matching. Beyond these, we argue that large-scale image editing datasets [ 4 , 46 , 54 ] naturally satisfy the triplet structure required by ZS-CVR. Their stricter query–target alignment is particularly ben-eficial for our required cross-modal embedding alignment. Based on the above analysis, we curate a multi-source dataset comprising approximately 3.5M samples from three complementary data types, providing a solid foundation for alignment pre-training (Table 1). 

• Type I (with Pseudo Modification Text and Pseudo Target Description) follows the MMC construction pipeline rather than directly adopting MMC, since MMC relies on older models (e.g., LLaMA2 [ 43 ]) and mainly covers the general domain, lacking high-quality fashion-domain data, a key scenario for visual com-positional retrieval. We prompt Qwen3-VL-8B to generate three diverse modification texts and corresponding target image de-scriptions per source image, filtering trivial transformations via CLIP similarity between the source image and target description. We use LLaVA-Pretrain [ 25 ] as the general-domain source and incorporate Fashion200K [15] to enhance domain coverage. 

• Type II (with Pseudo Modification Text) directly adopts FiG-MaQ [ 16 ], where visually correlated image pairs are mined from the ImageNet1K test split [ 8], and modification texts are gener-ated by LLaMA 3.1-70B. 

• Type III (with Pseudo Synthesized Target Image) leverages AnyEdit [ 54 ], a large-scale image editing dataset with high-quality edited images. It naturally forms triplets ⟨real image, modification text, edited image ⟩, and we retain only retrieval-relevant cate-gories (object replacement, color alteration, appearance swap), discarding less relevant operations such as depth modification.

## 3.3 MLLM-Guided Dual-Level Reranking 

To enhance zero-shot CVR performance at test time, we propose an MLLM-guided dual-level reranking strategy. Unlike prior works that rely on shallow networks to adjust initial rankings, our ap-proach leverages the reasoning capabilities of MLLMs to refine retrieval results. While several studies have highlighted the poten-tial of MLLMs as powerful rerankers [ 24 , 26 ], they often overlook the computational feasibility: applying an MLLM to a large-scale gallery is computationally prohibitive. To address this, we first perform adaptive budgeted subset scoring, estimating relevance only for a subset of top-ranked candidates using the MLLM. We then employ a dual-level mechanism for adjusting candidate rele-vance scores: a global adjustment that refines the query embedding via a score-weighted fusion of the MLLM-assessed candidate em-beddings, and a local adjustment that directly injects the explicit MLLM relevance scores. This design balances computational cost and accuracy, enabling practical and reliable reranking.

3.3.2 Global Re-Scoring with Query Refinement. Since we only obtain MLLM scores for a subset of candidates, direct score-level fusion across the entire gallery, i.e., combining the original Stage I scores with Stage II MLLM scores, is not feasible. To address this limitation, we propose a global re-scoring strategy based on intermediate query refinement, that utilizes the MLLM objectively-accessed relevance signals to refine the query embedding. The refined embedding is then used to recompute relevance scores for all candidates in the gallery, effectively propagating the refined signal globally. ## 4.1 Experimental Setup 

4.1.1 Datasets and Evaluation Metrics. We evaluate UniCVR on five benchmarks spanning all three CVR tasks. For ZS-CIR, we adopt FashionIQ [50 ], a fashion-domain CIR dataset covering three clothing categories (Dresses, Shirts, and Tops&Tees); CIRR [27 ], a general-domain CIR dataset; and CIRCO [2], a CIR benchmark with multiple ground truths per query. Following [ 2 , 23 , 52 ], we report R@10 and R@50 on the FashionIQ validation set, R@ 𝑘 (𝑘 ∈{1, 5, 10 , 50 }) and R subset @𝑘 (𝑘 ∈ { 1, 2, 3}) on the CIRR test set, and mAP@ 𝑘 (𝑘 ∈ { 5, 10 , 25 , 50 }) on the CIRCO test set. For MT-CIR, where no prior zero-shot attempts exist, we adopt Multi-Turn FashionIQ [ 56 ], which extends FashionIQ to the multi-turn setting, and report R@5 and R@8 across three clothing categories following supervised works [ 31 , 47 , 56 ]. For CoVR, we adopt the mainstream benchmark WebVid-CoVR [45 ] and report R@ 𝑘 (𝑘 ∈ { 1, 5, 10 , 50 })following existing works [44, 45]. 

4.1.2 Implementation Details. For the model architecture, we adopt Qwen3-VL-32B [ 41 ] as the MLLM query encoder, with LoRA fine-tuning parameters set to 𝑟 = 8 and 𝛼 = 16 . Inspired by the ex-perimental analysis in [ 32 ], we set the number of learnable query tokens as 𝑀 = 128 . The frozen gallery encoder is implemented via PEcore-G [ 3], which generates 1280 -dimensional embeddings. For Stage I, we train on our curated multi-source dataset of ap-proximately 3.5M samples using the AdamW optimizer [ 28 ] with a learning rate of 1 × 10 −5 for the LoRA parameters and 1 × 10 −4 for the remaining parameters, along with a cosine annealing schedule for 1 epoch. We adopt GradCache [ 10 ] to expand the effective mini-batch size to 512 . ## 4.2 Performance Comparison. 

UniCVR is benchmarked against representative baselines across five datasets, with results detailed in Table 2 and 3 (CIR), Table 4 (MT-CIR), and Table 5 (CoVR), where all baselines are compared in their best-performing configurations. Note that for MT-FashionIQ and WebVid-CoVR, we additionally include supervised baselines for reference, as dedicated zero-shot methods for these two tasks remain scarce. We highlight the following observations. 

(1) UniCVR achieves cutting-edge performance across all three CVR tasks. Even without MLLM-guided dual-level rerank-ing, Stage I alone already outperforms most baselines. After Stage II, UniCVR further advances state-of-the-art across all benchmarks (es-pecially on CIRCO with 43 .09% mAP@5 vs. 34 .10% of MagicLens), and even rivals supervised methods on MT-FashionIQ ( e.g. , 57 .6% 

Avg. vs. 48 .1% of the supervised FashionNTM) and WebVid-CoVR (e.g. , 85 .81% Avg. vs. 85 .34% of the supervised HUD). These results validate the effectiveness of both stages: Stage I establishes a strong zero-shot foundation via MLLM-VLP alignment pre-training, while Stage II further refines retrieval through MLLM-guided dual-level reranking, together enabling a unified framework that generalizes well across diverse CVR tasks. 

(2) Stage II consistently improves over Stage I, especially at top ranks. The gains are observed across all five benchmarks: R@10 on FashionIQ (Avg.) improves by +3.63% in absolute terms (42 .38% →46 .01% ), R@1 on CIRR by +7.83% ( 40 .10% →47 .93% ), mAP@5 on CIRCO by +8.12% ( 34 .97% →43 .09% ), R@5 on MT-FashionIQ (Avg.) by +3.6% ( 51 .0% →54 .6% ), and R@1 on WebVid-CoVR by +3.81% ( 62 .96% →66 .77% ). The particularly large gain on CIRCO suggests that the MLLM-guided dual-level reranking is especially effective at disambiguating among multiple plausible ground truths. 

(3) Treating the MLLM as a query embedder generalizes better than using it as a captioner. Training-free methods such as LDRE, CIReVL, SEIZE, OSrCIR, AutoCIR, and CoTMR leverage powerful MLLMs, either closed-source ( e.g. , GPT series) or large open-source models ( e.g. , Qwen2-VL-72B), as query reasoners to generate target descriptions. While these methods benefit from strong multimodal reasoning, they exhibit inconsistent cross-dataset performance. For instance, SEIZE ranks 2nd on FashionIQ but falls to mid-tier on CIRCO, and CoTMR ranks 4th on FashionIQ yet drops further on CIRCO. By contrast, UniCVR achieves top performance consistently across all benchmarks without any task-specific prompts, demon-strating that bypassing the query-to-text bottleneck improves gen-eralization across diverse downstream tasks. 

(4) Multi-source pre-training yields better generalization than any single data source. Pre-training-based methods ( e.g. ,FiRE, MagicLens, and CompoDiff) exemplify the limitation of rely-ing on a single data source. FiRE, pre-trained solely on Type II data (FiGMaQ) with pseudo modification texts, suffers from limited mod-ification diversity and lacks domain-specific coverage ( e.g. , fashion), resulting in strong performance on open-domain benchmarks like CIRR and CIRCO but noticeably weaker rankings on FashionIQ. More strikingly, even scaling up a single data source does not re-solve this issue: MagicLens and CompoDiff are trained on ∼36.7M and ∼18.8M pseudo triplets, respectively, both far exceeding our 

∼3.5M samples, yet neither achieves consistent top-tier rankings across benchmarks. By contrast, UniCVR Stage I combines com-plementary Type I, II, and III data, achieving consistently strong rankings across all five benchmarks. This confirms that given the inevitable limitations of any single data source, diversity matters more than scale alone in the zero-shot pre-training stage.

## 5 Conclusion 

In this paper, we presented UniCVR, a unified zero-shot frame-work for composed visual retrieval that jointly addresses CIR, MT-CIR, and CoVR without any task-specific human-annotated triplets. UniCVR combines the compositional reasoning of MLLMs with the well-structured metric space of VLP models through a two-stage design. In Stage I, we bridge the heterogeneous embedding spaces between the MLLM query encoder and the frozen VLP gallery en-coder via contrastive pre-training on a curated multi-source dataset, enhanced by a cluster-based hard negative sampling strategy. In Stage II, an MLLM-guided dual-level reranking mechanism refines UniCVR: From Alignment to Reranking for Unified Zero-Shot Composed Visual Retrieval Arxiv Preprint, 2026, 

the initial retrieval results through adaptive budgeted subset scor-ing and dual-level re-scoring. Extensive experiments across five benchmarks demonstrate that UniCVR achieves cutting-edge per-formance on all three CVR tasks, confirming both its effectiveness and generalizability. We hope that UniCVR provides a solid foun-dation for future research on ZS-CVR. In future work, we plan to explore more efficient MLLM reranking strategies and extend UniCVR to broader scenarios. ## A Details of Multi-Source Pre-Training Data 

As described in Section 3.2.1 of the main paper, our multi-source pre-training dataset comprises approximately 3.5M samples from three complementary data types. In this section, we provide detailed de-scriptions of the data construction process for each type. For Type I, which is constructed following prior work, we elaborate on the source selection rationale, prompt design, and filtering criteria. For Type II, which is directly adopted from existing datasets, and Type III, which is curated from existing datasets, we describe the data characteristics and curation strategies. ## A.1 Type I: Pseudo Modification Text and Pseudo Target Description 

Type I data follows the triplet form ⟨real image, pseudo modification text, pseudo target description ⟩. We construct Type I data from two sources: LLaVA-Pretrain 1 for the general domain and Fashion200K 2

for the fashion domain. We specifically incorporate Fashion200K because fashion is a prominent application scenario in composed visual retrieval. This domain requires fine-grained attribute distinc-tions, such as neckline style, sleeve length, or fabric pattern, which are often underrepresented in general-domain data. For each source sample, we prompt Qwen3-VL-8B 3 with three diverse prompts to generate three (pseudo modification text , pseudo target description )

pairs, which are then filtered for quality. 

A.1.1 LLaVA-Pretrain. LLaVA-Pretrain [ 4 ] contains approximately 558K images with BLIP-generated [ 3 ] brief captions, originally cu-rated for the feature alignment stage of visual instruction tuning in LLaVA. We choose this dataset for two main reasons. First, it provides broad and diverse visual content covering a wide range of everyday scenes and objects. Second, the accompanying brief captions offer effective contextual cues for prompting the gener-ative model to produce more precise and diverse modifications.

Prompt Design. All three prompts take the source image and its brief caption as input, each adopting a different perspective to elicit diverse modifications: Prompt A ( Open-Ended User Request )lets the model freely imagine what a user might wish to change, without any restriction on the modification scope; Prompt B ( Image-Grounded Modification ) requires the model to propose a modifica-tion grounded in the observed source image content; and Prompt C (Real-World Search Scenario ) frames the image as a near-miss search result, asking the model to propose a specific change that would redirect it toward a different search intent. 

(1) Prompt A — Open-Ended User Request. This prompt uti-lizes a fully open-ended, divergent strategy, promoting exploration across various dimensions (e.g., appearance, quantity, background, style, season, and mood) to maximize the diversity of generated triplets. Consequently, the resulting modifications are often creative and encompass long-tail modification types. 

Prompt A — Open-Ended User Request 

Look at this image carefully. Its brief description is: "{caption}" Imagine a user found this image online but wants aslightly different version of it. Think about what specific aspect of this image a real user might want to change — it could be anything: appearance, quantity, background, style, season, mood, or anything else that naturally comes to mind when looking at this image. Generate one realistic user request and describe what the resulting image would look like. Respond in JSON format only, no explanation: {"modification": "<what the user wants to change, written as a natural request, in 5–15 words>", "target_description": "<specific visual description of the resulting image, in 15–25 words>" }

(2) Prompt B — Image-Grounded Modification. This prompt emphasizes modifications that are closely grounded in the visual content of the source image. The model is first asked to identify the most salient visual elements in the image, and then propose a meaningful modification grounded in what it actually observes. Unlike the unconstrained Prompt A, Prompt B ensures that mod-ifications are tightly anchored in the visual characteristics of the source image, yielding more content-faithful triplets. (2) Semantic filtering : We use CLIP ViT-bigG 4 as an off-the-shelf semantic similarity tool for data filtering. Triplets with a similarity above 0.95 are discarded, as they indicate a trivial modification that does not meaningfully alter the image seman-tics. ## C Detailed Results of Ablation Study 

In Section 4.3 of the main paper, we report the ablation results using averaged metrics for brevity. Here we provide the complete per-dataset, per-metric breakdown for all ablation variants, enabling a more fine-grained analysis. Tables 7–10 report the detailed results on FashionIQ, CIRR, CIRCO, Multi-Turn FashionIQ, and WebVid-CoVR, respectively. Below we highlight several observations that emerge from these fine-grained results but are not apparent from the averaged numbers in the main paper. 

(1) Category-level breakdown on FashionIQ. FashionIQ and Multi-Turn FashionIQ are the benchmarks that provide category-level annotations, enabling a fine-grained analysis of Stage II gains across different clothing categories. As shown in Tables 7 and 9, Stage II consistently improves performance across all clothing cat-egories (Dresses, Shirts, and Tops&Tees) in both single-turn and multi-turn settings, confirming the robustness of Stage II reranking regardless of clothing category or query complexity. 

(2) Stage II improvements concentrate at top ranks. The detailed per-metric breakdown reveals a consistent pattern across all five benchmarks: Stage II yields substantially larger gains at higher-precision metrics. For example, on CIRR, R@1 improves by 

7.83% , R@5 by 5.03% , while R@50 gains a more modest 0.22% ; on WebVid-CoVR, R@1 improves by 3.81% , R@5 by 2.64% , while R@50 gains 0.48% . Similar trends are observed on FashionIQ, Multi-Turn FashionIQ, and CIRCO. This may be because Stage I already places most relevant targets within the top-50 candidates, as evidenced by the initially high R@50 across all benchmarks. Stage II then refines the ranking order among these top candidates, pushing relevant items from lower positions ( e.g. , rank 10–50) to the very top (e.g. , rank 1–5). resulting in concentrated gains at higher-precision metrics such as R@1 and R@5. (3) MLLM scale matters most for fine-grained benchmarks. 

Comparing Stage I models at 4B, 8B, and 32B scales, the scaling ben-efit varies significantly across benchmarks. The most pronounced gain appears on CIRCO, where mAP@5 improves by 9.31% from 4B to 32B, and CIRR shows a similar trend with R@1 improving by 4.87% . In contrast, the gains on FashionIQ and Multi-Turn Fash-ionIQ are more moderate, and on WebVid-CoVR the 8B model even slightly outperforms the 32B model. These results imply that bench-marks requiring fine-grained compositional reasoning over diverse visual interpretations ( e.g. , CIRCO and CIRR) benefit substantially from larger query encoders, while benchmarks with constrained visual domains ( e.g. , FashionIQ and Multi-Turn FashionIQ) can be adequately handled by smaller models. It also indicates that in-creasing model size does not guarantee consistent improvements across all benchmarks, highlighting the interaction between model capacity and the scope of pre-training data. 

## D Sensitivity Analysis of Scoring Budget 

In Stage II, the scoring budget 𝐾 ′

> 1

controls how many top candi-dates from Stage I are evaluated by the MLLM assessor. A larger budget allows the MLLM to assess more candidates, potentially im-proving ranking quality, but at the cost of increased computational overhead. The main paper reports the aggregated trend on Fash-ionIQ, CIRR, and CIRCO in Figure 3. Here we provide the detailed per-metric breakdown across all benchmarks in Tables 11–14. We make the following observations from these results. First, Stage II is effective even under tight computational budgets. Even the smallest budget 5/10 already yields substantial gains across all benchmarks, and performance consistently plateaus beyond a moderate budget, with diminishing returns from 20 /40 to 30 /60 .This indicates that Stage I already ranks most relevant targets near the top of the list. As a result, even a small budget ( 5/10 ) allows Stage II to refine the query embedding and re-score the candidates, while increasing the budget further brings limited additional ben-efit, leading to diminishing returns. Second, the optimal budget varies slightly across benchmarks. On Multi-Turn FashionIQ, the default 10 /20 already achieves near-optimal performance. This is likely because its gallery size is smaller, making targets easier to rank near the top, which is also reflected in its narrower evalua-tion metrics (R@5 and R@8 rather than R@10 and R@50). On the remaining benchmarks, 20 /40 yields the best trade-off, with dimin-ishing returns beyond that. These detailed results are consistent with the aggregated trends reported in Figure 3 of the main paper, further validating our default budget choices.
