Abstract 

Human understanding of video dynamics is typ-ically grounded in a structured mental represen-tation of entities, actions, and temporal relations, rather than relying solely on immediate deduc-tive reasoning. In contrast, existing Video-LLMs largely depend on unstructured video reasoning, where critical visual evidence is embedded in ver-bose textual descriptions and temporal causality is often weakly modeled. This leads to ineffi-cient processes and fragile causal inference. To bridge this cognitive gap, we propose construct-ing a compact representation of salient events and their causal relationships, which we name Struc-tured Event Facts , prior to the reasoning stage. This structured prior serves as an explicit con-straint to promote concise and causally grounded reasoning, while also making intermediate evi-dence easier to verify. To effectively train models on such structured facts, we introduce CausalFact-60K and a four-stage training pipeline compris-ing facts alignment, format warm-start, think-ing warm-start, and reinforcement learning-based post-training. During RL stage, we find that this framework introduces competing objectives, as structural completeness and causal fidelity must be balanced against reasoning length, making it difficult to optimize. We address this challenge by formulating the optimization as a Multi-Objective Reinforcement Learning (MORL) problem and explicitly optimizing toward the Pareto-Frontier 

to balance these trade-offs. As a result, we in-troduce Factum-4B , which yields more reliable reasoning and delivers stronger performance on challenging video understanding tasks requiring fine-grained temporal inference.

1. Introduction 

Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have achieved remarkable success in interpreting static images by adapt-ing the architectural paradigms of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Liu et al., 2024a; Bai et al., 2023). Recently, this progress has extended to the video domain, aiming to cap-ture dynamic temporal information (Zhang et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2024). To enhance the reasoning capabilities of these models, the research community has predominantly adopted the Chain-of-Thought (CoT) paradigm (Wei et al., 2022) in Large Language Models, which encourages models to generate intermediate reasoning steps before deriving a final answer. Later on, CoT has also been proven effective to multimodal tasks (Bai et al., 2025a). Unlike text, video data is characterized by dense spatio-temporal redundancy. When models simply employ unstruc-tured CoT like text-only tasks, the resulting generation often devolves into verbose, non-linear narratives where pivotal vi-sual cues are drowned in irrelevant tokens. Empirically, we observe that reasoning-enhanced Thinking models often degrade in performance compared to their Instruction-tuned counterparts (Bai et al., 2025a). They suffer from reasoning drift, losing focus on the user query, and fre-quently reduce the temporal dimension to isolated frame retrieval rather than analyzing causal relationships. This behavior contrasts sharply with human cognitive processes. Psychological research suggests that human perception re-lies on forming a structured mental sketch, which registers entities, actions and event boundaries before engaging in abstract reasoning (Zacks & Swallow, 2007). 1

Motivated by this cognitive mechanism, we propose a 

Structure-First paradigm, a module generated prior to the reasoning phase. As shown in Figure 1, we enforce the model to synthesize a high-density schema containing time, object, human action, scene, and causal event caption before the reasoning process begins. This structured prior serves as an explicit constraint, anchoring the subsequent reasoning to be concise, salient, and causally grounded. However, there is often a trade-off between maintaining structural strictness and ensuring reasoning length. We find that this multi-stage reasoning process presents a non-trivial optimization challenge during Reinforcement Learning (RL) stage. In resource-constrained settings with limited length budgets, maximizing facts density can inadvertently trun-cate reasoning depth, and vice versa. Standard RL strate-gies (Christiano et al., 2017), typically optimized via Proxi-mal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017), or Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) (Shao et al., 2024) struggle to balance these conflicting rewards, lead-ing to training instability, where the advantages calculation cannot distinguish optimization direction. To avoid collapsing multiple rewards into a single scalar, which can obscure critical trade-offs, we treat multi-dimensional reward vectors as distinct signals for policy updates. Unlike standard GRPO, we treat each reward component as an independent objective to preserve task-specific gradients. Inspired by the Multiple Gradient De-scent Algorithm (MGDA) (D ´esid ´eri, 2012), we propose Pareto-Frontier guided Advantage Balancing (P-FAB) algo-rithm to effectively resolve rewards conflicts and dynam-ically balance distinct objectives. Our contributions are summarized as follows: • We identify the limitations of unstructured CoT in video understanding. To address this, we propose 

CausalFact-60K , a dataset that empowers the model to precisely output facts information and utilize causal relationships for video reasoning with dense captions. • We reveal that the standard GRPO advantage calcula-tion may lead to conflicts among multi-objective re-wards and ambiguous gradient directions. Motivated by the pareto-frontier, we propose Pareto-Frontier guided Advantage Balancing (P-FAB) algorithm. This algorithm dynamically balances multi-objective advan-tages based on pareto-optimal during the advantage calculation process. • Based on aforementioned contributions, we train a small but strong model: Factum-4B . Extensive ex-periments on standard benchmarks demonstrate that Factum-4B significantly outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, validating the effectiveness of structured rea-soning process and P-FAB algorithm. 

2. Related Work 

Video understanding LVLMs. Following the success of image-to-text LVLMs (Liu et al., 2024a; Dai et al., 2023), early video LLMs treated videos as sequences of static frames and employed token compression to fit high-dimensional spatio-temporal signals into limited LLM token budgets (Maaz et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a). How-ever, this frame-level approach overlooks core challenges in video understanding, particularly temporal grounding and causal reasoning (Liu et al., 2024c; Chen et al., 2024). To address this, recent works have introduced time-aware ar-chitectures (Ren et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024; 2025) to better capture temporal dynamics, or incorpo-rated memory and tool-augmented mechanisms for handling long-form, untrimmed videos (Song et al., 2024; Qian et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). Parallel efforts focus on improving training data quality and scale (Zhang et al., 2024) and es-tablishing reliable benchmarks for evaluating long-horizon temporal and causal reasoning (Mangalam et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2024). Yet, despite architectural advances, current video LLMs often rely on sparse frame heuristics rather than modeling true event dynamics (Mangalam et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2021), limiting their performance in complex real-world scenarios. Our method addresses this by learning an explicit structured prior that summarizes key events and their causal relationships before free-form reasoning, effectively enhancing temporal grounding and reducing dependence on brittle frame-selection shortcuts. 

Reasoning and chain-of-thought in LVLMs. Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2022) improves LLM reasoning by eliciting intermediate steps, and Multimodal-CoT extends this to vision-language models by conditioning reasoning traces on visual features (Zhang et al., 2023b). However, visual inputs are often both redundant, contain-ing many irrelevant frames, also underspecified with fine-grained temporal and causal cues easily missed. As a result, unconstrained vision Chain of Thought tends to become verbose, drift off-topic, or hallucinate unsupported details, especially when intermediate steps lack grounding in verifi-able evidence (Chen et al., 2023). Prior approaches address this through tool-augmented verification (e.g., ReAct/MM-ReAct) (Yao et al., 2023b; Yang et al., 2023), exploration of multiple reasoning paths (e.g., Tree-of-Thoughts, self-consistency) (Yao et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023). In contrast, we generate an internal Structured Event Facts , a compact, high-density schema encoding key events, enti-ties, and causal relationships, which explicitly constrains subsequent CoT reasoning to be concise, salient, and firmly grounded in extracted video facts.

3. Methodology 

3.1. Overview 

To reformulate complex video thinking, a straightforward approach would typically involve a two-stage process: 

Thinking Warm-start to establish causal logic, followed by 

Reinforcement Learning for alignment. However, the unique integration of Structured Event Facts within our framework necessitates a specialized orchestration of the training steps. We empirically find that this facts-first dependency intro-duces an optimization barrier. Directly optimizing accurate factual description and complex causal reasoning through these two simple stages overwhelms the model, leading to hallucinated facts or collapsed reasoning structures. To re-solve this conflict, we decouple the learning process into four progressive stages: Facts Training (Stage 1), Format Warm-Start (Stage 1.5), Thinking Warm-Start (Stage 2), and RL-based Post-training (Stage 3) .Stage 1 trains the model to produce high-quality facts out-puts with accurate descriptions of person, action, scene, etc. Stage 2 focuses on training the model’s causal reasoning ability. These two stages are both trained through instruction tuning. In Stage 1, we ask the model to output facts infor-mation from the video, while in Stage 2, we ask real QA questions such as temporal grounding and visual reasoning. 

Detailed prompts are shown in the Appendix B.4. 

Notably, to bridge the gap between pure factual descrip-tion (Stage 1) and complex reasoning (Stage 2), we in-troduce an intermediate Stage 1.5. In this stage, we ask for the same facts as in Stage 1 but require the model to strictly adhere to the thinking format (e.g., 

<thinking>...<answering> ), thereby warming up the structural format. Otherwise, the model hallucinates bad structure. Finally, Stage 3 employs our proposed Pareto-Frontier guided Advantage Balancing (P-FAB) to resolve the objective conflicts in multi-objective reinforcement learning. 3.2. Data Curation: Facts and Thinking 

3.2.1. M OTIVATION 

Although video reasoning has raised researchers’ attention, existing video understanding models often reason without structure (Wang et al., 2025; Bai et al., 2025a), where crit-ical clues are submerged in redundant text. Conventional Chain-of-Thought (CoT) can become verbose and unfo-cused on long videos, introducing invalid steps while under-emphasizing temporal causal logic in favor of discrete time points. This motivates us to propose a thinking process with clearer clues, better interpretability, and stronger causality. Thus, we propose to model the video understanding problem as fol-lows: Facts, Thinking, Answering , where a clear reasoning process with strong causality can be established. Specifically, we force the model to output Structured Event Facts as the primary step of our methodology to enhance structured information extraction, aiming to provide precise, compact, and query-relevant clues that establish a reliable semantic foundation for subsequent reasoning. In this stage, the model is required to describe the major events in the video at reasonable intervals, spanning approximately 10 to 30 seconds, and extract the key cues within each event, such as salient characters, human actions, scene context, and camera position, and then provide a detailed, time-ordered description of each event in chronological sequence, as illustrated Figure 2. Building upon the resulting Structured Event Facts, we aim to steer the model toward better causal reasoning. Leverag-ing the salient clues extracted in the facts phase, we enforce a structured causal reasoning process with three tightly cou-pled stages: 1. Global Search & Localization, 2. Causal Verification, and 3. Final Alignment. As shown in Figure 2. The first phase Global Search & Lo-calization requires the model to retrieve query-relevant clues from the structured facts list to quickly narrow attention to a small set of highly relevant time windows, providing a reliable starting point for downstream reasoning. In Causal Verification stage, it examines temporal coherence by an-alyzing the preceding and succeeding events around the hypothesized interval, and validates the localization through cause, which effects consistency rather than shallow pat-tern matching. To avoid over-reliance on textual priors and keep the decision grounded in visual evidence, the model performs a mandatory visual verification on the inferred interval using vision tokens sent with textual prompts. In Final Alignment stage, the model performs a global con-sistency check to ensure the inferred temporal interval is fully contained within the observed activity sequences, im-proving the overall reliability of the reasoning process. For pre-processing, we adopt a strict selection pipeline to distill informative samples from high-quality human-annotated Video Temporal Grounding datasets (Gao et al., 2017; Krishna et al., 2017). More video source details are shown in the Appendix B. We prioritize videos with high event density, assuming an ideal event segment spans 20– 30 seconds: for a video of duration T , we retain it only if event number N ≥ T / 30 , i.e., a 150-second video re-quires at least 5 events. For retained videos, we further apply gap filling to improve continuity: unlabeled intervals between adjacent annotated events (e.g., unlabeled [25 , 38]

The two strong models mutually critique and filter outputs, enabling facts to capture detailed visual cues. The data produced in this stage is served for Stage 1 and Stage 1.5. 3.3. Pareto-Frontier guided Advantage Balancing Motivations. After the first three stages, the model ac-quires structured reasoning capabilities. In Stage 3, we introduce reinforcement learning to align the model with real-world problem-solving. However, the integration of facts, thinking, and answering components naturally elon-gates the chain-of-thought, transforming the training into a complex Multi-Objective Optimization problem, where two problems may occur in standard Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO): (1) Inherent Conflicts : There is a natural Pareto trade-off between comprehensive fact extraction and token efficiency, which static weights cannot dynamically navigate; and (2) 

Directional Ambiguity : Standard GRPO cannot distinguish update differences between multiple objectives. As illus-trated in Figure 4, GRPO produces identical advantage val-ues for candidates with the same average rewards, thereby masking the underlying distribution and potential imbal-ances among different reward components. 

Pitfalls of traditional policy gradient optimization. In policy gradient optimization, updates follow the direction A·∇θ log πθ (oi | qi), where the advantage A is derived from a weighted reward Rfinal = P wiri. To manage conflicting objectives, a common practice is to tune the coefficients wi

to prioritize specific goals. However, since these weights remain static during training, they often exacerbate trade-offs rather than resolving the underlying conflicts.

Discussion on P-FAB. As illustrated in Figure 4, P-FAB 1) naturally amplifies sparse and hard-to-satisfy objectives while reducing the weights of rewards that are more easily obtained. Consequently, the resulting advantage values are more discriminative, prioritizing samples that satisfy critical, rare criteria over those that merely perform well on simpler tasks. 2) We consider the model to have reached a local 

Pareto-Frontier when the objectives achieve Pareto station-arity . In this state, the convex hull of the task gradients contains the origin ( min α ∥ P αm∇L m∥ ≈ 0), implying that no common descent direction exists. At this point, any further improvement in one objective necessitates a degrada-tion in another, signifying that the model has fully navigated the inherent trade-offs of the task.

In this paper, we present Factum-4B, a Video-LLM that bridges the cognitive gap between human video understand-ing and current models by introducing Structured Event Facts, serving as an explicit constraint that guides the model towards interpretable causal video reasoning. We develop a four-stage training pipeline that progressively equips the model with facts sketching and causal reasoning towards real-world task. To handle multi-objectives trade-offs un-der constrained length budgets during RL, we introduce Pareto-Frontier guided Advantage Balancing (P-FAB) al-gorithm. Empirically, Factum-4B yields more stable and reliable reasoning, delivering stronger performance on chal-lenging video understanding tasks that demand temporal localization and causal inference. Appendix Contents A. Detailed Information of Evaluation Benchmarks 

To comprehensively evaluate the capabilities of our model, particularly in temporal reasoning and open-ended generation, we utilize a diverse set of benchmarks categorized into two primary domains: Video Temporal Grounding and General Video Understanding. 

A.1. Video Temporal Grounding Benchmarks 

These benchmarks evaluate the model’s ability to precisely localize specific moments within untrimmed videos based on textual queries. • Charades-TimeLens (Zhang et al., 2025a): A high-quality dataset re-annotated based on the Charades-STA dataset, where 66.3% of low-quality annotations were rewritten. The final dataset contains 1,313 videos and 3,363 annotations, with an average video duration of 29.3 seconds. This benchmark focuses on daily indoor activities (e.g., cooking, reading) and is characterized by dense actions and complex temporal overlaps. Unlike the noisy annotations in the original dataset, Charades-TimeLens provides rigorously verified timestamps to evaluate fine-grained temporal localization. We report Recall@1 at IoU thresholds of 0.3, 0.5, and 0.7. • ActivityNet-TimeLens (Zhang et al., 2025a): A re-annotated version of the ActivityNet-Captions (Krishna et al., 2017) dataset introduced in the TimeLens benchmark, where 69.7% of low-quality annotations were rewritten. The final dataset contains 1455 videos and 4500 annoations, with and average video duration of 134.9s. It addresses the issue of loose boundaries in the original annotations by providing stricter and more precise start/end times for temporal queries. Evaluating on this split offers a more rigorous assessment of a model’s boundary alignment capabilities. • ActivityNet-Captions (Krishna et al., 2017): A large-scale Video Temporal Grounding benchmark containing 4885 open-domain YouTube videos for evaluation, with 17031 annotations and an average video duration of 118s. It covers a wide range of complex human activities. The dataset is widely used for dense video captioning and temporal grounding. C. Instruction Tuning Data Format 

To effectively train the model to reason over structured evidence, we design a progressive prompt engineering strategy that guides the generation of training data across three distinct stages. This multi-stage approach ensures the model first learns to extract high-density visual facts, then adapts to a strict structural format, and finally integrates these skills into a coherent reasoning chain. In the initial stage, the objective is to align the model’s visual perception with our structured event schema, as displayed in Figure 12. We explicitly instruct the model to localize activity events and generate a detailed report containing a global theme and detailed event factuals. We enforce a rigid output format for each temporal segment, requiring specific fields such as time, person, human action, scene, object, camera, and a casual event caption. This design forces the model to decompose complex video dynamics into discrete, verifiable atomic elements rather than generating generic captions, effectively transforming raw video data into the structured representation used throughout the pipeline. Before tackling complex reasoning tasks, the model must internalize the specific XML-like tag structure required for our framework. We therefore introduce a format warm-up stage, as shown in Figure 13. The system prompt defines the interaction protocol: the assistant must first output the factual block, followed by a thinking block, and conclude with the answering block. Since the task here is simply to reproduce video facts, the thinking block is populated with a trivial placeholder. This step ensures the model adheres to the syntactic structure without the cognitive load of complex problem-solving. The final stage focuses on generating high-quality chain-of-thought data, as depicted in Figure 14. We combine the structured facts from the first stage with challenging QA pairs. The prompt requires the model to first recall the structured factual context. Crucially, the subsequent thinking block is no longer trivial; it contains a detailed derivation process where the model performs global search, causal verification, and alignment based on the provided facts. This stage produces the gold-standard thinking data used to warm-start the policy model, ensuring that the reasoning process is explicitly grounded in the extracted evidence.
