Abstract. Camouflaged Object Detection (COD) aims to segment tar-gets that share extreme textural and structural similarities with their complex environments. Leveraging their capacity for long-range depen-dency modeling, Transformer-based detectors have become the main-stream approach and achieve state-of-the-art (SoTA) accuracy, yet their substantial computational overhead severely limits practical deployment. To address this, we propose a hierarchical Confidence-Aware Token 

Pruning framework ( CATP ) tailored for COD. Our approach hierarchi-cally identifies and discards easily distinguishable tokens from both back-ground and object interiors, focusing computations on critical boundary tokens. To compensate for information loss from pruning, we introduce a dual-path feature compensation mechanism that aggregates contex-tual knowledge from pruned tokens into enriched features. Extensive ex-periments on multiple COD benchmarks demonstrate that our method significantly reduces computational complexity while maintaining high accuracy, offering a promising research direction for the efficient deploy-ment of COD models in real-world scenarios. The code will be released. 

Keywords: Camouflaged Object Detection · Token Pruning · Confidence-Aware Token Pruning · Dual-Path Feature Compensation 

1 Introduction 

Camouflaged Object Detection (COD) aims to identify and segment targets that blend seamlessly into their surrounding environments. This task has significant applications in wildlife conservation [10], medical diagnosis [11], military recon-naissance [25], and precision agriculture [40, 46, 47]. Unlike generic object detec-tion, COD requires models to capture subtle discrepancies in texture, color, and structural patterns, as camouflaged targets exhibit extreme visual similarity to their backgrounds. Addressing this challenge is crucial for developing robust vi-sion systems capable of operating reliably in complex real-world scenarios, such as autonomous ecological monitoring and fine-grained lesion analysis.

Early COD approaches mainly rely on Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)-based architectures [9,10,23]. With the emergence of Vision Transformers (ViTs) [6, 28], transformer-based COD methods [44, 49] have achieved superior perfor-mance. This improvement largely stems from the ability of transformers to cap-ture long-range dependencies and model global contextual relationships [45]. Since camouflaged objects share strong visual similarity with their backgrounds, relying solely on local features often fails to effectively separate targets from surrounding regions. In contrast, transformers enable global comparisons that highlight subtle discrepancies critical for identifying concealed objects. As a re-sult, transformer-based architectures have become the dominant paradigm in COD research. Despite their strong performance, transformer-based COD models rely on heavy backbones and suffer from substantial computational overhead due to the quadratic complexity with respect to input sequence length. This burden becomes increasingly severe when considering applications on mobile or edge devices. Model compression [5, 16, 18] offers a promising direction to address this challenge, with token pruning emerging as a particularly efficient strategy that leverages the inherent architecture of Transformers. However, most existing token pruning methods in computer vision are designed for image classifica-tion [24, 38], which is a globally sparse prediction task that naturally tolerates aggressive token reduction. In contrast, COD is a pixel-wise dense prediction task, where maintaining accuracy requires careful assessment of each token’s im-portance prior to pruning. To tackle these efficiency bottlenecks, we design a confidence-aware token pruning framework specifically tailored for COD. While token pruning reduces computational cost by discarding tokens deemed less important, these removed tokens still retain rich information that may be critical for fine-grained perception. They carry contextual consistency and low-level visual structures including texture, color, and spatial continuity, which help preserve object integrity, enhance feature robustness, and impose bound-CATP 3

ary constraints that reduce false positives. In dense prediction tasks like COD, distinguishing camouflaged objects from their backgrounds often relies on these subtle cues. Simply discarding these tokens risks losing such nuanced contextual information, potentially leading to degraded boundary localization or increased false positives in challenging scenarios. To address this issue, we introduce a dual-path feature compensation mechanism that aggregates and preserves es-sential contextual cues from pruned regions, allowing the model to benefit from reduced complexity while maintaining competitive detection accuracy. In this work, we propose a Confidence-Aware Token Pruning ( CATP )framework for COD. By hierarchically removing redundant tokens based on their predicted confidence, our framework substantially reduces computational cost and accelerates inference while maintaining high detection accuracy. Specif-ically, a lightweight scoring head predicts the probability of each token belong-ing to a camouflaged object, and the probability is viewed as token confidence, with two thresholds defined to determine which tokens to prune and which to retain. Tokens outside the thresholds are definite and pruned with negligible accuracy loss, while those between are ambiguous and retained for deeper re-finement. Binary decision masks derived from these confidence scores are pro-gressively updated to guide token computation across subsequent Transformer blocks. To preserve information from pruned regions, we introduce a Dual-Path 

Feature Compensation ( DPFC ) mechanism. This module aggregates discarded tokens by compressing the foreground and background groups into two com-pact prototypes, each capturing essential contextual cues. Aggregation weights are confidence-based: high-confidence tokens contribute less due to their limited additional information, while low-confidence tokens near the decision boundary receive higher attention to exploit their potentially discriminative features yet to be fully learned. These prototypes are reintegrated into the token sequence, enriching subsequent layers with preserved information at minimal overhead. We extensively evaluate the effectiveness and generality of the proposed framework across multiple state-of-the-art (SoTA) COD models. As shown in Fig. 1, experimental results demonstrate that our approach significantly reduces computational complexity while maintaining competitive accuracy, providing a practical solution for efficient transformer-based COD in real-world applications. Our contributions can be summarized as follows: 

– We propose a confidence-aware hierarchical progressive pruning framework for COD that adaptively removes high-confidence foreground and background tokens while retaining ambiguous ones for multi-stage refinement. 

– We introduce a weighted information compensation mechanism that aggre-gates pruned tokens based on their predicted confidence, effectively preserv-ing contextual information and compensating for pruning-induced loss. 

– Extensive experiments demonstrate that our pruning method significantly reduces computational complexity while maintaining high accuracy for COD, offering a promising research direction for its practical deployment. 4 Gao et al. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 Camouflaged Object Detection 

COD is a pivotal task in computer vision, dedicated to detecting objects that are visually indistinguishable from backgrounds. Conventional methods predom-inantly leveraged hand-crafted features, such as texture [12,13] and color [17,41], to capture low-level visual discrepancies. Nevertheless, these approaches fre-quently falter in complex environments where foreground objects and back-grounds exhibit profound intrinsic similarities in both appearance and structure. The advent of deep learning has propelled the field forward, characterized by the evolution of two primary architectural paradigms: (1) CNN-based Bio-inspired Frameworks: Initial deep learning efforts adopted bio-inspired strategies that mimic human visual perception, exemplified by the search-and-identification mechanism in SINet [10] and its successors [27,32,33,43,50]. To mitigate the am-biguity inherent in concealed boundaries, subsequent research integrated multi-scale feature fusion [35, 36], uncertainty estimation [48], and frequency-domain analysis [4, 52]. (2) Transformer-based Global Modeling: More recently, ViT-based models [3, 14, 15, 36, 39, 44, 49] have established new SoTA benchmarks. By harnessing self-attention mechanisms [6, 28], these models effectively cap-ture long-range dependencies, a capability essential for aggregating sparse dis-criminative cues within camouflaged scenes. Notably, FSPNet [15] proposes a non-local token enhancement strategy to refine feature interactions. CFRN [44] reformulates multi-scale features into continuous functions to sustain discrimi-native representation. SENet [14] shows that a simple ViT architecture can ef-fectively capture global contextual cues for both camouflaged and salient object perception. Despite their remarkable performance, Transformer-based COD frameworks suffer from the quadratic computational complexity of self-attention. This leads to substantial inference latency and memory overhead, hindering their practical deployment in resource-constrained scenarios. Therefore, it is crucial to develop a more selective and efficient mechanism to improve computational efficiency while maintaining detection accuracy. 

2.2 Token Pruning 

Real-time applications such as drone-based surveillance and search-and-rescue operations require COD models to achieve high computational efficiency. How-ever, transformer-based COD models introduce substantial computational over-head due to the quadratic complexity of self-attention with respect to the number of tokens. Reducing the number of tokens while preserving informative features is therefore critical for enabling efficient COD inference. Token pruning has emerged as an effective strategy for accelerating ViTs by identifying and discarding redundant tokens [6]. Early approaches typically rely on static or predefined pruning rules, whereas more recent methods empha-size dynamic and content-aware token selection mechanisms. For instance, Dy-namicViT [38] introduces a lightweight importance predictor to generate token CATP 5

masks for adaptive pruning. Similarly, EViT [24] leverages attention responses to identify less informative tokens for removal. Instead of directly discarding tokens, ToMe [1] mitigates redundancy by merging similar tokens via bipar-tite matching, thereby preserving semantic information while reducing compu-tational overhead. Several recent works explore more general token reduction paradigms [2, 22, 26, 51], aiming to balance efficiency and representation quality through adaptive token reduction mechanisms. While effective for classification, token pruning is suboptimal for COD, as discarding tokens disrupts the spatial integrity and structural coherence that are essential for dense prediction. Only a few works attempt to address this limitation. For example, TR-PEFT [21] targets general DINOv2-based [34] segmentation using a conservative pruning strategy to maintain fidelity. In contrast, our CATP is specifically tailored for the unique challenges of COD. By leveraging confidence thresholding and feature compensation, CATP enables substantial token reduction while preserving the intricate spatial cues essential for precise camouflage detection. 

3 Method 

Our CATP framework is tailored for the dense prediction task of COD. In Sec. 3.1, we first present the overall architecture. In Sec. 3.2, we detail the hierar-chical confidence-aware token pruning strategy to efficiently prune tokens while preserving critical semantic cues. Finally, in Sec. 3.3 we introduce our dual-path feature compensation mechanism for mitigating information loss and leveraging useful information from the dropped tokens. 

3.1 Overview 

Our framework consists of a prunable ViT [6] encoder and a multi-level fea-ture fusion decoder, as shown in Fig. 2. Specifically, the encoder comprises L

Transformer layers, which are partitioned into S sequential stages. Each stage contains K = L/S layers, collectively producing a set of multi stage features. The framework incorporates lightweight scoring heads at different stages to per-form confidence-aware token pruning and binary decision masks M are derived by thresholding the predicted confidence scores, which subsequently guide token computation in deeper layers. In parallel, DPFC is integrated to aggregate and preserve critical information from the discarded tokens, offsetting the loss of con-textual cues caused by pruning. Finally, the decoder aggregates these multi-level features to generate the final prediction map.

Hierarchical Token Refilling. To construct a spatially consistent multi-level feature pyramid { ˆF1, . . . , ˆFS } after token pruning, it is necessary to recover dense feature representations while preserving the refined semantic information from deeper stages. However, progressive pruning introduces structural misalignment across stages, as only a subset of tokens remains active at each level. To address this issue, we adopt a recursive token refilling strategy that propagates features from deeper stages to reconstruct dense spatial representations for the decoder. with a unified spatial resolution T . Starting from the deepest stage, features are progressively propagated to shallower stages by refilling the spatial positions cor-responding to tokens that survive subsequent pruning stages, while the remaining positions retain their original features from the current stage. 4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setup Datasets. We conduct comprehensive evaluation of our approach on four widely used COD benchmarks: CAMO [20], CHAMELEON [42], COD10K [10], and NC4K [30]. (1) CAMO comprises 1,250 images across 8 categories, encom-passing both natural and artificial camouflage scenarios. (2) CHAMELEON 

provides a high-resolution collection of 76 meticulously annotated images with pixel-level precision. (3) COD10K , currently the largest COD dataset, contains 5,066 diverse images curated from various photography platforms to ensure high semantic variety. (4) NC4K consists of 4,121 images and is primarily utilized to assess the generalization and robustness of COD models. Following the standard protocol, we merge the training sets of CAMO and COD10K for model training (4,040 images), use CHAMELEON (76 images) for validation, and evaluate on the respective test sets of all four datasets (totaling 6,473 images). 

Implementation Details. All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU. Following prior token pruning works, we utilize ViT [6] as the backbone ( L = 12 , P = 16 , C = 768 ) with S = 4 pruning stages. Unless otherwise specified, the dual thresholds are set to θd = 0 .3 and θu = 0 .7 and remain constant across all stages. To ensure a fair comparison, our training configurations strictly adhere to the original baseline settings. Specifically, for CFRN-based experiments [44], we employ the AdamW optimizer [29] with a cosine annealing schedule (initial learning rate 3 × 10 −5), training for 30 epochs with a batch size of 12 using the dynamic difficulty-aware [43] and uncertainty-aware constraint [35] losses. In contrast, for SENet-based experiments [14], we utilize the Adam optimizer [19] with an initial learning rate of 1 × 10 −4, training for 25 epochs with a batch size of 8 while maintaining consistency with the original DWloss formulation.

4.2 Ablation Studies Effect of Pruning Thresholds. To investigate the impact of pruning thresh-olds θd and θu, we conduct ablation experiments on the CFRN baseline as shown in Tab. 1. Compared to the baseline CFRN, the 0.2/0.8 setting retains top ac-curacy on some datasets but yields marginal efficiency gains with only 8.7% GFLOPs reduction and negligible FPS improvement. In contrast, the 0.4/0.6 setting further reduces GFLOPs to 158.7 and boosts FPS to 30.0, yet suffers from noticeable performance degradation—for instance, Sα on CAMO drops to 0.842, failing to meet performance requirements. The 0.3/0.7 configuration, how-ever, achieves the best trade-off: it reduces GFLOPs by 38.8% to 219.8, boosts FPS by 34.8% to 25.2, while maintaining accuracy highly competitive with the best results across all benchmarks. Hence, we adopt 0.3/0.7 as the default thresh-old for subsequent experiments to balance performance and efficiency.

Comparision with SoTA Methods To further validate the superiority of CATP in camouflaged object detection, we compare it with TR-PEFT [21], a state-of-the-art pruning method for dense prediction tasks, on both SENet and CFRN baselines, with results shown in Table 6. 5 Conclusion 

This paper presents the first systematic study on token pruning for COD. We ob-serve that informative tokens in COD predominantly lie along object boundaries, whereas redundant ones within homogeneous foreground or background regions can be safely discarded to reduce computation and accelerate inference. Driven by this insight, we propose the CATP framework. A lightweight scoring head predicts token confidence, and two thresholds are applied to retain boundary-ambiguous tokens while pruning confident ones. Although pruned tokens are deemed less critical, they still encode useful contextual and object cues. To har-ness this, we introduce a DPFC mechanism that aggregates discarded tokens CATP 15 

into compact prototypes, mitigating information loss from pruning. Extensive experiments on multiple COD benchmarks demonstrate the effectiveness and generalization of our method. Comparisons with SoTA dense prediction pruning approaches further validate its superiority in COD. Future work will focus on developing more precise identification of redundant tokens and more effective strategies for reusing discarded information.
