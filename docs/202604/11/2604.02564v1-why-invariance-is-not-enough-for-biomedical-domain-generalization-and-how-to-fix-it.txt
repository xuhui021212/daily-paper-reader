Abstract. We present DropGen , a simple and theoretically-grounded approach for domain generalization in 3D biomedical image segmentation. Modern segmentation models degrade sharply under shifts in modality, disease severity, clinical sites, and other factors, creating brittle models that limit reliable deployment. Existing domain generalization methods rely on extreme augmentations, mixing domain statistics, or architec-tural redesigns, yet incur significant implementation overhead and yield inconsistent performance across biomedical settings. DropGen instead proposes a principled learning strategy with minimal overhead that lever-ages both source-domain image intensities and domain-stable foundation model representations to train robust segmentation models. As a result, 

DropGen achieves strong gains in both fully supervised and few-shot segmentation across a broad range of shifts in biomedical studies. Unlike prior approaches, DropGen is architecture- and loss-agnostic, compatible with standard augmentation pipelines, computationally lightweight, and tackles arbitrary anatomical regions. Its implementation is freely available at https://github.com/sebodiaz/DropGen. 

## 1 Introduction 

Medical image segmentation is considered largely solved in controlled environ-ments: with a few hundred labeled examples, strong augmentation, and a good training recipe, models achieve high performance when the test data is from a similar domain as its training samples [29, 32]. Unfortunately, real-world de-ployment is far less forgiving. Annotated medical datasets are often small, and domain shifts in disease prevalence, patient populations, imaging sites, acquisition parameters, and modalities all significantly degrade performance, necessitating costly, time-consuming cycles of retraining and new manual annotations. 

Domain generalization (DG) methods aim to address these shifts during train-ing, aiming to eliminate the need for retraining on distribution-shifted test data. Data-level DG uses extreme augmentations [47] or style transfer [75] to expand the diversity of the available training samples, but cannot guarantee coverage of un-seen target domains or remain compatible with standard augmentation pipelines. Model-level methods modify training architectures or strategies to mix feature

Further, semi- and unsupervised domain adaptation methods typically finetune, adapt, or train image translation models on each new (potentially unlabeled) target domain at test time [7, 8, 36, 60]. Critically, adaptation approaches demand substantial machine learning expertise and infrastructure, and require repeated effort for each new domain, limiting their practicality for biomedical users. Recent foundation models for interactive biomedical segmentation have shown strong performance by training on large, diverse datasets using clicks, scribbles, bounding boxes, and more as prompts [5, 20, 30, 62, 63]. However, they do not scale to fully automated workflows: prompting thousands of images is infeasi-ble in clinical practice, and interactive segmentation models often struggle on underrepresented domains, even with user inputs. In particular, anatomix [12] is a synthetically-trained 3D foundation model that extracts task-agnostic rep-resentations that are stable under nuisance imaging variations ( e.g ., scanner types, modalities, acquisition protocols). While training a segmentation model on its domain-invariant representations (instead of image intensities) could enable domain generalization, we find that it does not consistently outperform just using the raw images with extensive data augmentation (Sec. 4.3 and Fig. 4). This performance gap motivates the need for combining stable, domain-invariant representations with in-domain image intensities to train robust pre-dictors that generalize. Prior work [16, 34] shows that this combined approach can improve robustness, but assumes access to test data by design and requires finetuning or adaptation on each new task, which, again, cannot be practically ex-ecuted in clinical applications. Contributions. We present DropGen , a simple and effective domain generaliza-tion method for arbitrary domain shifts. DropGen trains robust segmentation models that leverage both the in-domain image intensities and stable, domain-invariant representations extracted by foundation models [12]. We provide a theoretical and empirical analysis of feature combination under distribution shift, and find näive combination induces shortcut learning, where models over-rely on in-domain features and fail to generalize out-of-distribution. To achieve the best of both worlds, we propose a simple, principled regularization mechanism to prevent this shortcut and force the model to jointly exploit both sources of information. Experimentally, DropGen achieves excellent performance across multiple highly variable biomedical applications and data availability settings, enabling reliable segmentation when applied to new domains. Further, DropGen 

does not require test time adaptation, yet performs competitively with methods that do retrain on (unlabeled) target-domain data. 

## 2 Related Work 

Domain Generalization (DG). DG methods aim to learn domain-invariant segmentors before deployment. Data-level DG methods often use strong data augmentation to expand the training domain, such as convolving images with ran-domly initialized filters [47, 65], stitching samples [69], and incorporating spatial mixing [47], among others [10]. Other model-level DG approaches modify archi-tectures or losses to reduce sensitivity to spurious cues [28, 75, 76]. When domain labels are available ( e.g ., imaging sites or sequences), DG methods can explicitly incorporate domain differences into their modeling or architectures [27, 44, 55, 76]. However, DG methods often add non-trivial computational overhead, do not reliably transfer across applications, may conflict with standard training pipelines, and explicit domain labels are rare in medical imaging datasets. DropGen avoids this dependency on domain labels, requires minimal extra computation, and is fully generic w.r.t. target applications, improving both the practicality and deployment flexibility of DG segmentation. 

Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA). UDA methods address do-main shifts by adapting models at deployment using unlabeled test data. Many approaches optimize surrogate losses that encourage confident or consistent pre-dictions, leveraging auxiliary tasks [54], entropy minimization [60], batch statistics updates [14, 58], augmentation consistency [61], or contrastive regularization [8]. Others focus on aligning feature distributions, either statistically [7, 35, 36, 53, 74] or via adversarial training [57]. Pseudo-labeling frameworks similarly use confident predictions as weak supervision [64, 68, 70, 73, 77]. However, these ad hoc strate-gies often fail to generalize beyond their intended domain shifts and anatomical regions and require considerable machine learning expertise and infrastructure to use, limiting their deployment. In contrast, DropGen does not require test time adaptation and generically handles arbitrary domain shifts and applications. 4 Diaz et al. 

Domain Randomization (DR). DR [18, 22, 56] generates diverse, unrealistic 

synthetic training data by sampling random image intensities within application-specific label maps. By training on heterogeneous data, these approaches achieve robust segmentation [3, 9, 11, 40, 67], registration [17, 23, 24], and pose estima-tion [13]. However, DR methods need to be trained for each biomedical application and require dense label maps, which are often unavailable. Our work builds exten-sively on a DR-based foundation model [12] that provides an application-agnostic 

representation extractor and thereby enables pan-dataset deployment. 

Promptable Foundation Models (PFMs). By training on broad aggregated datasets, recent large-scale promptable models [38, 49] generate segmentation masks conditioned on user inputs such as clicks, bounding boxes, or text, enabling flexible segmentation without task-specific retraining [5,25,30,63]. However, most PFMs operate on 2D slices and require per-image prompting, limiting their practicality for large studies involving thousands of images. Further, they do not exhibit domain-invariance by design and underperform when faced with distribution shifts, making them unsuitable for general-purpose feature extraction. 

Exploiting Stable Representations. Prior work in non-medical settings has explored obtaining and leveraging stable, domain-invariant representations, typically by solving a constrained optimization problem to minimize risk across all training domains [2]. However, the resulting representations can underperform on several tasks, as optimizing for cross-domain stability discards valuable domain-specific information [16, 50, 52, 72]. The key challenge is therefore not choosing between invariant and domain-specific information, but effectively utilizing both sources. Recent work trains two separate models, one capturing stable and one capturing unstable representations, and combines them at test time [16, 34]. However, these two-stage, two-model pipelines are both computationally expensive and may require careful domain-specific tuning at test time. DropGen sidesteps these complications by learning a single predictor at training time alone.

3.1 Methods 

DropGen is outlined in Figure 2 alongside our graphical model assumptions. It comprises three key components: domain invariant representation extraction, combination with image intensities, and regularizing the combination to prevent shortcuts. 

Stable Representation Extractor. Let fϕ : X → S denote a representation extractor parameterized by ϕ, mapping inputs x ∈ R1×H×W ×D to representations 

s = fϕ(x) ∈ Rd×H×W ×D where d is the embedding dimension and H, W , and D

indicate the spatial dimensions. Modern foundation models obtain transferable representations that can be used in our setting [12, 30]. In particular, the repre-sentation extractor we use in our primary experiments produces approximately invariant (stable) representations [12], i.e ., fϕ(xe) ≈ fϕ(xe′ ) ∀ e, e ′ ∈ E .

Combining Sources of Information. At test time, prior work combines S

and X by merging logits from models trained separately on S and X or by using a modeled trained on S as a pseudo-labeler to finetune a model trained on 

X [16, 34]. To avoid test time adaptation or training separate models for each source of information, we instead provide the predictor with an input that contains both the raw image and the representation extractor’s outputs: z = concat (x, f ϕ(x)) ∈

R(1+ d)×H×W ×D . The predictor hθ : Z → Y then operates on z directly. We write 

xu := x for the unstable source and xs := fϕ(x) for the stable source, such that 

z = ( xu, x s). In the analysis that follows, we use Xu, X s, and Z to denote the corresponding random variables. 

Regularizing the combination. Directly training hθ on z using ERM does not solve the generalization problem. As xu contains in-domain signal, the predictor trained with an in-domain loss (ERM) is incentivized to rely on the in-domain features xu and ignore the domain-invariant features xs. To prevent this, we randomly mask different channels (features) of z during training. As hθ is now trained on only partial views of z that may or may not include xu, it cannot ignore 6 Diaz et al. 

the stable inputs xs, as shown below. In practice, we apply channel dropout such that each of the (1 + d) channels in z has a probability p of being dropped out. 

3.2 Analysis 

While channel dropout might intuitively prevent overfitting to either information source, does it structurally prevent overfitting to Xu? Further, is this added complexity over training on the domain-invariant Xs alone justified? Here we investigate the following questions: 1. Does channel dropout structurally force hθ to use Xs? (Proposition 1) 2. Does training on (Xu, X s) improve over training on Xs alone? (Proposition 2) In the analysis below, H(·) denotes entropy, H(· | · ) conditional entropy, and 

I(·; ·) mutual information. Because hθ operates on the concatenation (Xu, X s),its first-layer weights are partitioned into slices Wu and Ws corresponding to the unstable and stable inputs, respectively. This holds for any architecture whose first layer is linear in the input, including CNNs and ViTs. We make the following assumptions, the first three of which follow [16]: 

Assumption 1 (Stable inputs) Y ⊥ E | Xs, i.e ., the stable inputs encode information about Y that is environment-invariant. 

Assumption 2 (Unstable inputs) Y̸ ⊥ E | Xu, i.e ., the relationship between the in-domain input Xu and Y depends on the environment. 

Assumption 3 (Complementary information) Y̸ ⊥e Xu | Xs for every e ∈E, where ⊥e denotes conditional independence under Pe, i.e ., in each environment 

Xu carries task-relevant information beyond what Xs provides. 

Assumption 4 (Informative stable inputs) I(Y ; Xs) > 0, i.e ., Xs possesses nonzero predictive information about Y .

Assumptions 1–3 are explicit in the graphical model (Fig. 2, right). Assumption 4 avoids trivially domain stable inputs ( e.g ., all constant values). 

Remark 1 (Shortcut Learning). Assumptions 2 and 3 imply that Xu carries in-domain predictive signal for Y , but the relationship shifts across environments. Given both (Xu, X s), the model is incentivized to primarily use Xu due to its predictive in-domain signal when training with an in-domain loss, thereby ignoring Xs and consequently failing at test-time when the environment changes. We hypothesize that shortcut learning is not due to the stable inputs being uninformative across environments, but is caused by the training objective focusing only on the training environments, an observation we empirically validate in Section 4. In the following, we show that channel dropout as a feature combiner is a principled strategy to regularize the use of both Xu and Xs and mitigates these harmful effects. DropGen 7

## 4 Experiments 

4.1 Experimental Setup Datasets and Domain Shifts. We evaluate on six datasets spanning five clinically relevant domain shifts: sequence, disease stage, clinical site, imaging phase, and modality. For sequence shifts , we train on T1w, T2w, and T1w-CE MRIs from MSD-BraTS [1] and evaluate on FLAIR MRI from unseen subjects. Site and scanner shifts are examined using a multi-site prostate MRI collection [4, 41,43,44], where training, validation, and testing data all come from distinct sites. To study phase shifts , we use PanDG [71], training on venous-phase abdominal MRIs and evaluating on out-of-phase scans. Finally, we evaluate modality shifts on two datasets: AMOS [33], training on CT and evaluating on MRI for large-scale multi-organ segmentation, and TopCoW [66], training on CT angiography (CTA) and validating/testing on MR angiography (MRA) for vascular structures. A table summarizing splits, sample sizes, and more is in Appendix C. 

Baselines. To reflect practical clinical deployment, we compare against state-of-the-art DG methods that neither require domain labels nor adapt on unlabeled test data. Unless otherwise noted, all methods use the same UNet architecture for their segmentation branch and we optimize each baseline’s primary hyperparameters using a grid search, as detailed in Appendix B. DSBN [76] employs custom BatchNorm layers and perturbs intensities around labels to generate synthetic contrasts. GIN and GIN+IPA [47] filter training data with randomly sampled convolutional kernels to generate diverse appearances and use spatial mixing to remove spurious correlations between locations and voxel intensities. Augmentations. Unless otherwise specified, we apply a strong and extensive data augmentation pipeline across all considered methods to maximize their out-of-distribution generalization potential. We use flips, rotations, resolution degradations, noise, bias fields, contrast adjustments, blurring, sharpening, and affine deformations, each with 0.33 probability. We omit flips and rotations in datasets where left/right correspondence is important. 

Data availability settings. We evaluate performance in two common medical imaging scenarios: datasets with abundant annotations (" all-data ") and those with limited annotations (" few-shot "). The all-data models are trained for 250K iterations following [29], while few-shot models are trained for 40K steps as in [12]. The few-shot experiments used five randomly selected subjects from each dataset for training and excluded the BraTS and PanDG datasets due to high inter-sample heterogeneity ( e.g ., in tumor presentations) that precludes meaningful training and interpretation of few-shot experiments across all methods.

## 5 Discussion 

Limitations and Future Work. DropGen has a single, tunable hyperparam-eter: the channel dropout probability p. While the optimal p is in a consistent range, for optimal performance, it must be tuned on validation data as in Table 3. However, higher p values induces an appropriate amount of regularization for most datasets. Future work can instead train a hypernetwork [15, 19] conditioned on p

using DropGen for segmentation that removes the need for training multiple models and can be freely tuned after training. Further, DropGen is compatible with any data-level domain generalization method and their combinations may have compounding benefits. However, this would require careful future work analyzing the interplay between stable representations and highly heterogeneous synthetic or augmented inputs. 

Conclusions. This article presented DropGen , a simple and general framework for domain-generalized biomedical image segmentation that combines raw image intensities with stable representations from foundation models. By enforcing robust usage of both information sources through a lightweight feature combina-tion regularizer, DropGen achieved strong and consistent gains across diverse domain shifts, modalities, anatomies, and data-availability settings, all without DropGen 15 

requiring complex architectural changes or specialized loss functions as needed by existing methods. DropGen ’s simplicity follows from and is supported by our theoretical analyses showing that jointly combining stable and unstable sources with regularization yields a principled solution. Our experiments demonstrated that DropGen outperformed state-of-the-art domain generalization methods in both standard training and few-shot regimes, enabling practitioners to no longer need substantial data- or model-level interventions to train and use robust segmentation models.

Representation stability and choice of feature extractor. In addition to the quantitative results shown in Table 4 of the main text, we now visual-ize the representations extracted by the two foundation models we considered (anatomix [12] and nnInteractive [30]) to qualitatively investigate their stability to domain shifts. In Figure 5, we compare T1 vs. T2 and CT vs. MRI features from BraTS and AMOS, respectively, to assess the consistency of learned features across different imaging modalities. In this comparison, anatomix produces visu-ally more stable and coherent representations under domain shifts, even though nnInteractive has been exposed to a substantially larger corpus of real-world data. This suggests that anatomix may better capture domain-stable features, as needed in our work. To explicitly measure representation stability, we compute the centered kernel alignment (CKA) similarity [39] (where higher is more similar) between the representations extracted from FLAIR and T1 subjects in BraTS. We plot the CKA results for all subjects in Figure 6A and C, and find that anatomix, being specifically trained for domain stability is stable to these shifts. Further, given random contrast perturbation with Gamma adjustment in Figure 6B, anatomix maintains robustness across corruption levels. These reasons together demonstrate why we choose anatomix [12] as a stable representation extractor for DropGen 

over other current foundation models for biomedical volumes.

Robustness Analysis. We now assess how robust each DG method is to controllable domain shifts that are commonly encountered in practice. We employ randomly generated bias fields and gamma/contrast adjustments as corruptions, as these frequently change between MRI domains. As random bias fields vary spatially, we generate three different bias fields at each corruption level and report the average performance, whereas gamma corruption is deterministic. All methods were trained with strong gamma and bias augmentation, except for nnUNet, which uses its default augmentation pipeline and is thus excluded from this experiment for fair comparison. As shown in Figure 9, DropGen maintains its robustness under gamma and bias corruptions across most datasets on held-out test datasets. We again find that the next most stable method changes in between datasets, indicating that our representation-based approach offers the most consistent benefits across anatomies and domain shift types. We also now examine the out-of-distribution robustness of the converged optimal weights of each method on held-out test data. As flat minima often generalize better [21, 37, 42], methods that maintain performance under weight perturbations are more robust. To assess flatness, we add Gaussian noise of varying magnitudes to all parameters and measure the resulting performance drop. For each noise level α, we average results over five random perturbations. Figure 9 shows that DropGen degrades gracefully under increasing noise, indicating a more robust solution than competing methods, which decline sharply with weight corruption across most datasets. C.5 Baseline implementations 

We summarize how each baseline method was implemented for the comparisons in the main paper. For reproducibility, we refer readers to the official open-sourced implementations provided by the authors of each respective method. 

nnU-Net. We used the latest publicly available version of nnU-Net from the official repository [29]. Training plans were generated exclusively from the training splits we randomly selected to prevent any leakage from the validation or test sets. For evaluation, we selected the best-performing validation checkpoint and DropGen 35 

disabled test-time augmentation to ensure a fair comparison across baselines. Further, its default five-network ensembling was not used in order to achieve a one-to-one comparison. 

GIN and GIN+IPA. GIN consists of a shallow convolutional block that randomly samples convolutional kernels at each forward pass; we used the au-thors’ GINGroupConv3D module with default settings, as recommended in the reference code [47]. Since no open-source implementation of IPA is provided, we implemented it following the descriptions in the original work. IPA generates a GPU-based B-spline bias field with user-controlled spacing; we used a spacing of 

[16 , 16 , 16]  for all experiments. IPA also incorporates a consistency regularization term, for which we followed the parameterization used in the reference code and tuned its consistency weight on the validation set. GIN+IPA effectively increases the batch size because multiple perturbations are applied per input ( e.g ., an initial batch size of 4 becomes an effective batch size of 12). This increase in batch size in addition to the B-spline computation causes a significant runtime increase as demonstrated in Table 7. Additionally, both GIN and GIN+IPA were highly sensitive to initialization, necessitating the use of multiple random seeds to avoid divergent training. We trained GIN and GIN+IPA with three different random seeds each, and selected the corresponding models with the best performance on the validation set. 

MixUp. MixUp [69] interpolates pairs of samples within each batch, producing soft labels that encourage smoother decision boundaries. We applied MixUp with a probability p that was tuned on the validation set and sampled interpolation coefficients from the recommended  Beta (0 .4, 0.4)  distribution. 

CutOut. CutOut [10] masks out randomly positioned cubic regions from both the image and its label, reducing the model’s reliance on small, highly discriminative regions. The size of the masked cube was set to  0.33 × the crop size along each dimension (rounded to the nearest integer). CutOut was applied with a probability p tuned on the validation set. Because the input was scaled to  [0 , 1] ,we zero-centered the volume before applying CutOut and then shifted it back to  [0 , 1] , producing a neutral “gray” cube instead of a zero-valued region. This stabilized training when using batch normalization. 

DSBN. Domain-Specific Batch Normalization [76] replaces standard batch normalization with multiple domain-specific branches. Since no domain labels are provided, domains were assigned based on whether the Bézier intensity transformation applied to the foreground increased or decreased the intensity. Transformations were applied with a probability p tuned on the validation set. 

MixStyle. MixStyle [75] mixes feature statistics within each batch to simulate domain variation. Because explicit domain labels are not available, we mixed statistics randomly between samples in the batch. We applied MixStyle to the UNet encoder. We applied the MixStyle with probability p tuned on the validation set. 36 Diaz et al. 

RSC. RSC [28] requires two forward passes: one to compute standard gradients and another in which the top 20% of dominant gradients are masked. This discourages the model from over-relying on a small set of highly discriminative features. As high-level features emerge in deeper encoder layers, we applied RSC at the deepest encoder block. We applied RSC with probability p tuned on the validation set. 

Stable Feature Boosting (SFB). Stable Feature Boosting (SFB) [16] ap-proaches the domain generalization problem by training two separate model: unstable and stable. At test time, SFB uses a calibrated stable model to produce pseudo-labels. These pseudo-labels serve to supervise the unstable model during test time. A bias correction procedure is used to mitigate the wrong predictions made by the stable feature during test time. In our context, the "stable" model is a model trained on the stable representations only and the "unstable" model is a model trained on the unstable representations only. We first calibrate the stable model via temperature scaling on the validation set. For each test volume, the stable model products calibrated softmax probabilities. The unstable model is then adapted over 3 outer rounds: in each round, it is finetuned for S gradients steps using a KL divergence loss against the pseudo-labels with a learning rate of LR . Unstable model’s predictions are bias corrected. Final segmentation is the argmax of the pseudo-labels after all 3 rounds. S and LR are tuned on the validation set.

Entropy Minimization (Ent. Min.). TENT [60] adapts a pretrained model at test time by minimizing the entropy of its predictions on each test volume. Only the batch normalization (BN) parameters are updated, while all other weights are frozen. For each test volume, we reset the model to its pretrained weights and run S gradient steps of SGD with learning rate of LR , where each step is a random spatial crop. S and LR are tuned on the validation set.
